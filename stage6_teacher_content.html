Artistry and Legacy · 36 Weeks

The final stage is not a destination but a disposition. Stage 6 asks what a musician does with six stages of accumulated skill — not for the next performance, but for a lifetime. Five phases, 72 sessions. The decisive question: is this a musician ready to sustain a lifetime of growth, contribution, and artistry independently?

Stage 6 at a Glance

Stage 6 is unlike the previous five stages in one fundamental way: there is no ceiling. The gateway does not mark completion. It marks the beginning of an artistic life that extends indefinitely beyond the curriculum.

The Stage 6 Principle

The goal of all technical practice is to make itself unnecessary. The pianist who is still thinking about technique while performing has not yet reached artistry. The one who has forgotten technique entirely — because it has become the body — has arrived.

Three words in Stage 6's title are carefully chosen: Artistry — the state in which all previously learned skills operate as an integrated whole in service of musical expression. And — the conjunction that insists on both things simultaneously. Legacy — the lasting mark left on the musical community.

The Stage 6 gateway question"Is this a musician ready to sustain a lifetime of growth, contribution, and artistry independently? Not the best they will ever be — the best they are now, and the evidence that it will keep deepening."

Phase A — Mastery Repertoire

Weeks 1–8 · Sessions 1–16 · Three mastery works begun, the Stage 6 learning standard established, performance discipline locked in (two real-audience performances per month minimum), and the self-direction plan for Phase B designed. The north star question set in Week 1.

Week 1 · Sessions 1–2
The Stage 6 Inventory and Mastery Programme Design
North star question · Mastery work selection · What artistry sounds like · Five interpretive commitments · Stage 6 learning standard
Phase A
▾
Session 1 The Stage 6 inventory
ArtThe Stage 6 inventory: teacher and student review the Stage 5 gateway programme and personal voice statement together. "In the time since Stage 5, what has deepened? What has emerged that was not present before?" This is not a progress report but an artistic audit — a map of the current terrain. 10 min.
RepertoireIdentifying the mastery repertoire: list ten works in order of desire. The top three are the Phase A gateway programme — chosen for personal meaning and genuine artistic ambition, not technical safety. 12 min.
LegacyThe north star question: "In ten years, what do you want people to say about your playing? Not technically — musically and humanly. What do you want them to have experienced?" Write this as one sentence. It becomes the standard against which every artistic decision in Stage 6 is measured. 8 min.
EarListening session: teacher plays recordings of three pianists at mastery level — one in Baroque repertoire (Andras Schiff), one Classical/Romantic (Argerich, Uchida, or Brendel), one 20th century (Zimerman or Pollini). "What is common to all three that goes beyond correct notes?" Student articulates three qualities. Those qualities become the artistic targets for Stage 6. 15 min.
ArtPresence: "Have you ever had the experience at the piano, even for a single phrase, where all attention was inside the music rather than on the performance of the music? That is what Stage 6 works to make consistently available." 15 min.
Session 2 The Stage 6 learning standard
ArtAt Stage 6 the sequence "learn notes → add technique → add interpretation" is collapsed. Every section is learned from bar one with technical accuracy, harmonic understanding, and interpretive intention simultaneously. A passage cannot be "technically correct" while "musically uninflected" — the musical inflection IS the technique. 10 min.
ArtInterpretive position before the first note: before playing the mastery work, the student states their interpretive intention in three sentences. "This work is about ___. The journey it takes the listener on is ___. My specific contribution to the existing interpretations of this work is ___." The third sentence is the most important. 10 min.
ArtInterpretive courage: "Many technically accomplished performers play safely — within the range of interpretation no critic can fault. This safety is the enemy of memorability. A memorable interpretation commits to a specific, personal position that involves genuine artistic risk." 10 min.
AdvancedFive specific interpretive commitments: for each mastery work, identify five interpretive decisions that are distinctly your own. Each is: (1) named; (2) justified in musical terms; (3) consistently present in every performance. These five commitments are your artistic signature on the work. 10 min.
Home practice — Week 1 (90 min/day)
  • 10 min — Advanced technique warm-up: scales and arpeggios in two difficult keys, one octave exercise, one trill. All from Stage 4 — now pre-performance maintenance, not learning
  • 20 min — Mastery work 1: structural analysis at home. First section learned HS with inner singing and interpretive intention from bar one
  • 15 min — Sight-reading: one Grade 7–8 piece daily. Never repeated
  • 10 min — Improvisation: one 5-minute improvisation using the Stage 5 personal voice fingerprints. Record
  • 15 min — Stage 5 gateway programme maintained: one complete performance simulation. Keep what was built
  • 10 min — Ear training: listening with score of a major work not in the current repertoire
Week 2 · Sessions 3–4
Advanced Memorisation — Inhabiting a Work
Random start Stage 6 version · Transposition memorisation · Aural memory: singing all voices · The performance without preparation · Second mastery work introduced
Phase A
▾
Sessions 3–4 Memory at Stage 6 depth
AdvancedRandom start test — Stage 6 version: teacher names any bar. Student begins playing immediately in tempo. Teacher then interrupts with a harmonic question — "What chord is in bar 40?" — while the student continues playing. All questions answered without stopping. Passed when analytical memory operates simultaneously with performance without either interfering with the other. 15 min.
AdvancedTransposition memorisation: play a fully memorised section transposed down a semitone. This eliminates muscle memory entirely — leaving only aural and analytical memory. If these two systems are fully active, transposition is possible within two to three weeks on any given section. If impossible, the analytical memory is not yet fully active. 12 min.
EarAural memory: student leaves the piano, faces away, and sings the mastery work — all voices, not just the melody. Inner voices, bass line, and harmonic changes all represented. Where the singing becomes uncertain is precisely where the performance is most vulnerable. 13 min.
ArtThe performance without preparation: teacher asks the student to perform a section immediately upon sitting at the piano, without any warm-up. "In a real performance, the first note is the first note. This reveals whether the piece is inhabitable at a moment's notice — or whether it requires a preparatory ritual." 10 min.
Weeks 3–4 · Sessions 5–8
Performance Mindset — Playing for Real Audiences Consistently
Pre-performance ritual · Post-performance review · Two real-audience performances per month minimum · Breath as anchor · First Stage 6 performance arranged
Phase A
▾
Sessions 5–6 Performance ritual and review
ArtThe pre-performance ritual — four stages: (1) Physical warm-up: 10 minutes of scales and technical exercises at the performance venue. (2) Score review without playing: 5 minutes reading through the score mentally, confirming analytical memory for the opening of each piece. (3) The silence: 2 minutes — sit with the piano, settle the body, find physical stillness, hear the programme's opening in the inner ear. (4) The intention: one sentence stated before sitting at the instrument: "Today this programme is about ___." 12 min.
ArtThe post-performance review: written within 24 hours of every performance. Three questions: (1) Where was the intention fully present in the music? (2) Where did the intention retreat — and what caused the retreat? (3) What would I do differently in the next performance of this programme? The review is diagnostic, not self-critical. 10 min.
ArtBreath as anchor: a deliberate complete out-breath before the first note resets the nervous system's baseline. "Play the breath, not the notes. The notes happen inside the breath." Practice this explicitly: in-breath before the phrase begins, out-breath through the phrase's arc, natural pause at the phrase end before the next breath begins. 8 min.
ArtFirst Stage 6 performance: arrange a small-audience performance within the first four weeks. The pre-performance ritual and post-performance review are used exactly as in a formal concert. "The first performance of Stage 6 begins your performance log. At the end of Stage 6, this log will contain 35–72 entries." 5 min.
Why regular performance is a technical disciplineThe physiological changes during performance — elevated heart rate, increased adrenaline, heightened sensory awareness — are not problems to be overcome. They are the natural preparation for a high-stakes activity. A performer who has performed regularly for years learns to work with these changes: using the additional energy for greater physical commitment, the heightened awareness for more intense musical listening. Stage 6 requires a minimum of two real-audience performances per month throughout its entire duration.
Weeks 5–8 · Sessions 9–16
Chamber Music, Programme Design, and Phase A Milestone
Ensemble disciplines · Dynamic recalibration · Programme as a unified artistic statement · First full programme performance · Phase A milestone
Phase A
▾
Sessions 9–12 Ensemble and programme design
ArtAccompaniment at Stage 6: at this level, accompaniment is a full artistic engagement — the accompanist makes interpretive decisions as significant as the soloist's, simply in service of the soloist's musical line. "The great accompanist is the soloist's musical shadow — always present, always ready to catch." 15 min.
ArtDynamic recalibration: in ensemble, the piano is almost always too loud. The pianist should use the dynamic that feels uncomfortably soft. When that level feels natural, the balance is probably correct. This recalibration transfers permanently to solo playing. 10 min.
ArtWhat ensemble gives back: after a chamber music session, the solo playing changes for one to three days. Inner voices become more audible, harmonic rhythm more alive. Stage 6 schedules ensemble sessions deliberately the day before important solo performance work to exploit this effect. 10 min.
ArtProgramme design: "A recital programme is a composition in three movements. The relationship between the pieces — in key, character, era, duration, and emotional content — is as intentional as the relationship between movements of a symphony. You do not choose pieces and then arrange them. You compose a programme." 5 min.
Sessions 11–12 First full programme performance
RepertoireFirst full programme performance: all three mastery works in programme order for an audience of at least three people (teacher plus two others). Teacher does not correct during the performance — receives it as audience. Post-performance review written by student within 24 hours. Debrief in session 12 focuses on three most significant gaps between stated intention and audience's reported experience. 45 min.
ArtTransitions between works: the silence between the last note of one work and the first note of the next is part of the programme. At Stage 6, transitions are actively designed — their duration, the quality of physical presence within them, the mental shift from one work's world to the next. Practice each transition explicitly: it is part of the performance. 12 min.

Phase B — Extended Composition

Weeks 9–16 · Sessions 17–32 · The major original work (5–10 min) as an artistic statement equivalent in ambition to the mastery repertoire. Compositional seed, form map, harmonic planning, five development tools, revision by external ear. Plus the fourth mastery work and the first 50–75 minute complete programme.

Teacher's framing for Phase BThe major compositional work is not a demonstration of compositional technique. It is the most direct statement of the student's musical identity available — more direct than any interpretation of someone else's music, because it involves no inherited constraints. The work must be genuine. The teacher's role is not to evaluate or improve the composition — it is to ask questions that help the student articulate and realise their own intention. "Is this what you meant to say?" is the only relevant question.
Weeks 9–10 · Sessions 17–20
Compositional Process at Scale — Seed, Form, Harmonic Planning
Compositional seed from improvisation recordings · Four landmarks (beginning, turning point, climax, resolution) · Harmonic planning · First 16 bars composed
Phase B
▾
Sessions 17–18 Seed and form
ComposeThe compositional seed: from improvisation recordings made in weeks 1–8, identify the single most compelling musical idea — carrying the three Stage 5 fingerprints, feeling inevitable, as if it could not have been any other way. This is the seed of the major work. Short (4–8 bars). 10 min.
ComposeForm design before composing: four landmarks are the structural skeleton. (1) Beginning: character, key, texture, dynamic level. (2) Turning point: the moment the music commits to its journey. (3) Climax: highest tension — dynamically, harmonically, emotionally. (4) Resolution: the arrival that makes the whole journey feel inevitable. Everything else is flesh. 12 min.
ComposeHarmonic planning: before writing bar 5, decide the harmonic world of the entire work. What is the home key? Most distant key visited — and why? Which chromatic chord (dim7, augmented sixth, Neapolitan) appears at or near the climax? What is the final harmonic gesture — resolution, irresolution, or open ending? 10 min.
ComposeThe first 16 bars: compose, notate, and play. Then: "Is this what you meant? Is the character what you intended? Does the first phrase have a shape — a question that demands an answer?" Every bar at Stage 6 composition is intentional. Nothing is written because it "sounds all right" — everything because it is the next necessary thing in the music's argument. 18 min.
Week 10 · Sessions 19–20
Development — Five Tools for Thematic Argument
Sequence · Fragmentation · Augmentation · Diminution · Inversion · Planning the climax backward
Phase B
▾
Sessions 19–20 Thematic development
ComposeThe five development tools: (1) Sequence — idea repeated one step higher or lower (creates momentum); (2) Fragmentation — only part of the idea repeated (creates urgency); (3) Augmentation — idea in longer note values (creates weight); (4) Diminution — idea in shorter note values (creates excitement); (5) Inversion — idea upside down (creates surprise). Apply all five to the compositional seed. Evaluate: which transformation most advances the emotional argument at this point? 15 min.
ComposePlanning the climax backward: plan it specifically — which bar? Which key? Which chromatic chord? What dynamic? What texture? Then compose backward from the climax to the current development section — "What must happen before bar 67 for bar 67 to feel inevitable?" This backward planning is one of the most powerful compositional techniques available. 12 min.
RepertoireAnalyse the development section of one mastery work: "How does Beethoven/Chopin/Schubert develop the main themes? Which of the five tools appear, and in which order? What is the harmonic trajectory? Where is the climax, and how has it been prepared?" This analysis feeds directly into the student's own compositional development. 13 min.
Weeks 11–12 · Sessions 21–24
Resolution, First Draft, and Revision
The recapitulation as transformation · The ending as completion · First draft played through · Another pianist reads from score · Final revised score
Phase B
▾
Sessions 21–22 Resolution and the complete first draft
ComposeThe recapitulation: "The theme is the same — but the listener who has been through the development is different. Play to that changed listener." The recapitulation is composed with this emotional transformation in mind: the same theme, in the same key, but carrying the weight of everything that has happened since it was first heard. 15 min.
ComposeThe ending — three options: (1) Clear, settled cadence — the answer to the question posed in bar 1; (2) Sustained final note that dissolves into silence — the argument is made, the silence completes it; (3) Open ending — the work ends, but the listener continues thinking about it. The student chooses deliberately, having considered all three, and defends the choice. 12 min.
ComposeComplete first draft: by end of session 22, the complete first draft exists. Play through from beginning to end without stopping. First impression: does it feel like a complete work? Does the emotional journey arrive where designed? Write three assessments immediately — they guide the revision. 20 min.
Sessions 23–24 Revision and the external ear
ComposeTeacher hears the first draft for the first time as a listener — not as someone who has discussed the process. Teacher provides one response — an experiential report: "What did I experience? Where did the emotional journey arrive as designed? Where did it lose me? What was the single most powerful moment?" 12 min.
ComposeAnother pianist reads the composition from score: teacher or a second pianist reads through the complete work. Every unclear notation produces a different result from the one intended. Student observes without intervening — the reading is the diagnostic. Revision targets identified from the discrepancies. 15 min.
ComposeThe final score: all notation ambiguities resolved. Dynamics fully specified. Pedalling marked where intention is specific. Tempo indication: not only a metronome marking but a character indication ("with urgency and clarity," "as if from a great distance"). Fingering added for three most technically demanding passages. 10 min.
Art"The compositional decisions you have made in Phase B will permanently change how you perform other composers' music — because you now understand what those decisions feel like from the inside. When you decided where the climax of your work should be, you made the same decision as a Beethoven sonata performer who shapes the development section." 8 min.

Sessions 25–26 The complete programme
RepertoireFirst full recital run-through: all five pieces (four mastery works + original composition) in programme order for a small audience. 50–75 minutes total. Sessions 27–30 Performance accumulation and deepening
Art"The performance that is exactly the same each time is a recording. The performance that is completely different each time is chaos. Artistry is the living space between these two." After 10–15 real-audience performances, the interpretation deepens — if the performer is listening. Each audience and hall is different. A performer genuinely present responds to these differences. 10 min.
ArtPerformance log analysis: three patterns identified. (1) What consistently improved? (Area of greatest current mastery.) (2) What consistently remained problematic? (Next technical or analytical target.) (3) What varied — sometimes better, sometimes worse? (Still consciously managed rather than automatic.) 15 min.
LegacyArchive recording session — Week 15: the complete programme recorded in one sitting, pre-performance ritual used. No editing. "In ten years, this recording will be more interesting than any you will make in the next twelve months. It is the most honest document of exactly who you are as a musician at this moment." 15 min.

Phase C — Teaching and Community

Weeks 17–24 · Sessions 33–48 · Teaching a real student from Stage 1 through at least Stage 3 over Stage 6. The second original composition. Community performance for non-concert audiences. Fifth mastery work. The full 60–90 minute programme takes shape.

Teacher's framing for Phase CTeaching a student through three stages of the curriculum is not an add-on — it is a primary component of Stage 6. The reason: teaching reveals the depth of understanding in the teacher in a way that nothing else can. Every concept the Stage 6 student struggles to explain to their student is a concept they do not yet fully understand themselves. Every moment when their student's playing suddenly improves is a moment when the teacher has found the right words, the right demonstration, or the right physical adjustment.

Week 17 · Sessions 33–34
The Teaching Relationship in Depth
The teaching diary · Diagnostic first lesson · The mirror effect · Teaching vs. mentoring
Phase C
▾
Sessions 33–34 Beginning to teach
LegacyThe teaching diary: from the first lesson through the end of Stage 6, a weekly record of: what was taught; what the student understood easily and what required more explanation; what the teacher could not explain clearly; what changed in the student's playing; what the act of teaching revealed about the teacher's own understanding. Reviewed every four weeks. Submitted as part of the gateway portfolio. 10 min.
LegacyThe diagnostic first lesson — four things revealed: (1) Physical habits — tension, posture, existing touch; (2) Musical instincts — ear, rhythmic sense, response to dynamics; (3) Reading level; (4) Motivational profile — why do they want to play? The first lesson is a diagnostic, not a teaching session. 15 min.
LegacyThe mirror effect: "The student you teach will reveal your own history as a student — the moments where you struggled, the concepts that came easily, the habits formed before you understood them. When your student collapses their knuckle on the fourth finger, you will remember collapsing yours — and understand the correction from the inside rather than the outside." 12 min.
LegacyTeaching vs. mentoring: teaching addresses skill deficits ("Your thumb crossing is late — begin the slide under on beat 3"). Mentoring addresses artistic orientation ("You are playing this passage as if you are trying to impress me. What if you played it as if you were telling me something true?"). The Stage 6 student provides both — and never confuses which is being offered at any moment. 13 min.
Week 19 · Sessions 37–38
Community Performance — Music Beyond the Concert Hall
School, hospital, community centre, or place of worship · 20–30 min programme · Spoken framing · What reaches community audiences
Phase C
▾
Sessions 37–38 Community performance
LegacyCommunity programme design: 20–30 minutes from the full repertoire across all six stages. Requirements: shorter pieces (3–8 minutes each), greater variety, at least one piece the audience will recognise or can engage with immediately. Spoken framing before each piece: max 60 words, warm, specific, human. Example: "This next piece was written by a composer who was completely deaf when he wrote it. What I want you to listen for is how it still sounds like someone who loves sound deeply." 15 min.
LegacyPost-performance review: "What reached this audience that would not have reached a concert audience? What did not reach them? What does this reveal about the essential qualities of music that communicates across all contexts?" The insights are reported in the teaching diary. 12 min.
Legacy"In a community setting, your credentials are invisible. The only thing that communicates is the music itself and the quality of your presence in it. This is, paradoxically, the most honest performance context available. What works here works for the right reasons." 10 min.
Why community performance matters at Stage 6A performing musician who only plays in concert halls reaches audiences who have already decided classical piano music is for them. A musician who plays in schools, hospitals, and community centres reaches people who have never made that decision. These audiences respond only to what genuinely moves them — there is no social contract requiring polite listening. Playing for these audiences is the most revealing performance context available.

2–4 min · Meaningfully contrasts the first · Compositional range · Both pieces performed as a pair · Shared compositional fingerprints
Phase C

Sessions 39–40 Second composition and compositional range
ComposeThe second composition must be meaningfully different from the first in at least two dimensions: duration, harmonic language, texture, emotional character, or formal structure. "The shortest pieces are often the hardest to compose. Every bar must contribute to the whole. Nothing can be filler." 10 min.
ComposeCompositional range and identity: "The most compelling compositional identities are those whose range is wide but whose voice is consistent — Schubert can write a tragedy, a comedy, and an intimate confession — and all three are unmistakably Schubert. Can you write something completely different from your first composition — and still sound like yourself?" 10 min.
ComposeBoth compositions performed as a pair: play composition 1, then (after the transition silence) composition 2. "What do they have in common that was not consciously planned? Those shared qualities are the compositional fingerprints — the sonic signature present in everything the student writes." 15 min.
ComposeAnother pianist reads the second composition from score. Notation revised. "Your two compositions are now the most personal documents in this entire portfolio — the only things here that did not exist before you wrote them." 15 min.

The Legacy Project and the Musical Philosophy Document
Legacy project: first concrete step taken · Musical philosophy: five specific statements · 800–1500 words · "Is this what you believe?"
Phase D
▾
Sessions 49–50 Legacy project and philosophy
LegacyLegacy project first implementation: whatever the project — recital series, teaching programme, composition collection — the first concrete step has been taken. "A legacy is not a single event — it is a sustained practice. The annual recital that happens every year for twenty years has more lasting impact than the once-in-a-lifetime festival." 15 min.
LegacyThe musical philosophy document — five specific statements: (1) What music is for — why it exists and why it matters; (2) What performance demands — of preparation, commitment, presence, and integrity; (3) The relationship between technical mastery and artistic freedom; (4) What the musician owes to students, audiences, composers, and the tradition they inhabit; (5) Their specific artistic identity — a declaration of artistic values that will guide all future decisions. 20 min.
LegacyTeacher reads the first draft (minimum 800 words) and asks three deepening questions: "Which statement is most surprising? Most personal? Most conventional — echoing received wisdom rather than personal experience?" The final document must contain convictions, not aspirations. 5 min.
The legacy project — examples and criteriaA free annual recital series. A music education programme for students who cannot afford private lessons. A collection of original compositions published for other pianists. The Stage 6 Gateway — What It Certifies

The Stage 6 gateway certifies artistic maturity — the integration of performance, composition, teaching, and community service into a coherent musical identity that is genuinely personal, genuinely committed, and genuinely sustainable. The gateway is not an examination. It is a presentation — a sharing of a year's musical work.

01

The Complete Programme — Artistry in Performance

All five mastery works and two original compositions performed across Sessions 70 and 71. Pre-performance ritual. No stopping. Teacher and invited guest receive these works as a complete artistic event.

Four evaluation criteria
□ Is there a recognisable artistic presence in this performance?
□ Technique invisible — all attention freed for the music
□ Interpretive commitments fully present — not decisions being executed, but music being lived
□ Programme feels like a unified artistic statement, not five works played in sequence
The standard
"A listener who attended this performance should be able to describe the performer's musical personality in two or three consistent terms."
Pass criterionA recognisable artistic presence. Technique invisible. Interpretive commitments lived, not executed. Programme felt as a single artistic statement.
02

Original Compositions — Compositional Identity Confirmed

Both original compositions performed and discussed in Session 69. Three questions asked after both are performed.

Three composition questions
1. "What does this work say that your mastery repertoire cannot say for you?"
2. "Where in the composition are you most recognisably yourself?"
3. "What would you compose next — and why?"
Pass criterionBoth compositions are genuine artistic statements, not exercises. The two together demonstrate compositional range. The three Stage 5 personal fingerprints are audible in at least one composition. The composer's relationship to their own work is that of an artist, not a student completing an assignment.

The lifelong practice programme is realistic, complete, and has been followed for at least four weeks. The student can describe exactly what they will practice tomorrow, next week, and next month — without being told.

Recommended Stage 6 Repertoire

Stage 6 mastery works are chosen by the student for personal meaning and genuine artistic ambition. These are examples — not prescriptions. The five chosen mastery works + two original compositions = the complete gateway programme (60–90 min).

Phase A · Beethoven
Beethoven late and middle sonatas
Op. 57 (Appassionata) · Op. 109, 110, or 111 · Op. 31 No. 2 (Tempest)
Technique invisible. Interpretive commitments consistently present. All four memory systems active under performance conditions. The Appassionata for students needing maximum technical challenge; the late sonatas for maximum interpretive depth.
Phase A · Chopin
Chopin major works
Ballade No. 1 in G minor · Ballade No. 3 · Scherzo No. 2 · Fantaisie Op. 49 · Barcarolle Op. 60
The Ballades demand the complete integration of narrative structure, Chopin's rubato, and technical mastery. The Barcarolle is the most intimate and harmonically rich. The Fantaisie the most free in form.
Phase A · Schubert
Schubert major works
Sonata D. 960 · Wanderer Fantasy D. 760 · Impromptus Op. 90 complete set
Schubert D. 960 is one of the longest and most demanding works in the repertoire — 40 minutes alone. Its flat-VI modulations, the famous trill in the first movement, and the ambiguity of the finale make it the definitive Schubert gateway work.
Phase A · Brahms / Debussy / Ravel
Late Romantic and early modern
Brahms Rhapsodies Op. 79, Intermezzo cycle Op. 118, Sonata No. 3 Op. 5 · Debussy Préludes Book I or II, Images · Ravel Gaspard de la Nuit, Sonatine
Ravel's Gaspard de la Nuit is among the technically most demanding pieces in the repertoire. Debussy's Images require mastery of overtone pedalling. Brahms Op. 5 is the most ambitious of his early works.
Phase A–B · 20th century
Major 20th-century works
Prokofiev Sonata No. 7 · Bartók Out of Doors · Shostakovich Preludes and Fugues
Prokofiev's Seventh Sonata is the most immediately powerful of the war sonatas. Bartók's Out of Doors contains the full range of percussive and lyrical approaches. Shostakovich's Preludes and Fugues (a complete cycle of 24) are the 20th century's answer to Bach's WTC.
Phase A · Chamber and ensemble
Ensemble engagement works
Schubert Lieder accompaniment · Violin sonata reductions · Piano duet: Schubert Fantasia D. 940, Brahms Hungarian Dances
The dynamic recalibration that ensemble work demands transfers permanently to solo playing. At least one chamber music session per week throughout Stage 6 is recommended. The day before an important solo performance session is the ideal timing for ensemble work.
Phase B–C · Original compositions
The student's own works — two compositions
Composition 1: 5–10 minutes, major work, one movement, harmonic and structural ambition comparable to the mastery repertoire · Composition 2: 2–4 minutes, meaningfully contrasting the first in at least two dimensions
The two compositions together must demonstrate compositional range while maintaining a consistent compositional identity. They are placed within the mastery programme — not as demonstrations of compositional technique, but as genuine artistic statements with equal standing alongside the major works. The student's fingerprints must be audible in at least one of the compositions.

Teacher Quick Reference — Stage 6

The most important phrases, principles, and structures for Stage 6. The teacher's role has become collegial — accompanying rather than directing.

🎯

Session Structure — Every Session, Stage 6

Every session includes

  • 15 min sight-reading: Grade 7–8, never repeated, reading log maintained
  • 10 min improvisation: using personal voice fingerprints, recorded monthly
  • 10 min technique warm-up: two Stage 4 techniques — these are now automatic tools
  • 20 min mastery programme: either full simulation or targeted repair — never neglected in any phase
  • Pre/post performance ritual before and after any complete run-through

The teacher's role in Stage 6

  • Collegial — no longer instructing, but accompanying and witnessing
  • Ask "Is this what you meant?" not "Here is what it should be"
  • The most important questions: "What does this moment mean to you? Is that audible in the music?"
  • The final stage of teaching is learning to trust the student completely
  • The teacher's most important task in Phase E: name the gift they see in the student that the student cannot yet see in themselves
⚠️

Common Stage 6 Challenges — What to Say

Performance and interpretation

  • Safe interpretation: "You are playing within the range of interpretation that no critic can fault. That is the enemy of memorability. What would you do if you were the only person who had ever understood this piece? Play that."
  • Technique still audible: "I can hear you thinking about the octave passage. The passage is there to serve the music — not to be executed. Play through it as if it isn't there."
  • Performance log not improving: "The reviews are not becoming more specific. A vague review means vague attention during the performance. In the next performance, choose one specific question and listen for its answer throughout."
  • Composition feels generic: "Play bars 8–12 of your composition. Now play the parallel passage in your gateway piece. Where are you in both of these? I should hear the same person."

Legacy and teaching

  • Philosophy document aspirational: "You wrote 'I believe technique should always serve music.' Does your practice reflect this belief? Or is there still practice time devoted to technique for its own sake? Describe what you actually do — not what you believe you should do."
  • Legacy project not sustainable: "Who will do this when you are not here? If the answer is no one, the project depends entirely on your continued presence. Design it so that it would continue even if you left."
  • Teaching diary too surface: "The entry for this week describes what happened. I want to know what it revealed. What did your student's difficulty with thumb crossings remind you of from your own learning? What does that remind you of in your current playing?"
  • Student not making progress: "What is the one thing your student most needs this week — not technically, but musically? If you could give them one gift at this lesson, what would it be? Design the whole lesson around giving that one thing."
🎭

Musical Language — Phrases That Define Stage 6

For artistry and presence

  • "A correct performance disappears from memory within 24 hours. A performance in which something was genuinely at stake is remembered for years."
  • "Play as if you are the only person who has ever understood this piece — and as if it is urgent that the audience understand it too."
  • "Play the breath, not the notes. The notes happen inside the breath."
  • "The pre-performance ritual is not ceremony — it is the transition from being a person who knows how to play, to being the music."
  • "At performance number 35, the programme is no longer something you are learning. It is something you are inhabiting."

For legacy and the musical life

  • "Legacy is not what happens after you stop playing. It is what you build while you are still playing."
  • "A musician who gives only to the community without developing their artistry eventually has nothing to give. A musician who develops only their artistry without giving it to a community eventually plays for themselves alone — which is practice, not performance."
  • "The curriculum ends here. The music does not."
  • "There is no correct answer. There is only your answer. Speak it with the same conviction you played with. And then go and make it true."
