01
What artistry means — and what Stage 6 demands
Artistry is the state in which technique has become invisible and all attention flows into musical expression. Stage 6 defines what a lifetime of musical practice looks like — and begins building it. Six pillars. Thirty-six weeks. No ceiling.
Phase A
The goal of all technical practice is to make itself unnecessary. The pianist who is still thinking about technique while performing has not yet reached artistry. The one who has forgotten technique entirely — because it has become the body — has arrived.
The Stage 6 principle
Teacher’s framing for Stage 6

Stage 6 is unlike the previous five stages in one fundamental way: there is no ceiling. Each earlier stage had a defined gateway that confirmed a specific level of attainment. Stage 6 has a gateway too — but the gateway does not mark completion. It marks the beginning of an artistic life that extends indefinitely beyond the curriculum.

Three words in Stage 6’s title are carefully chosen. Artistry — the state in which all previously learned skills operate as an integrated whole in service of musical expression. And — the conjunction that insists on both things simultaneously. Legacy — the lasting mark left on the musical community: in performances remembered, students transformed, compositions that live in other pianists’ hands, and communities whose relationship with music has been permanently changed.

Pillar 1
Mastery Repertoire
Five major works at the highest technical and musical level the student can sustain — representing the full integration of all six stages’ skills simultaneously.
Pillar 2
Original Voice in Composition
Two extended original works: one of 5–10 minutes and one of 2–4 minutes. The student’s compositional voice developed to the point of recognisability.
Pillar 3
Teaching and Mentoring
Taking a real student from Stage 1 through at least Stage 3 over Stage 6. The deepest form of musical understanding is what can be given to someone else.
Pillar 4
Community and Contribution
Regular performance for real audiences. Collaborative music-making. A sustained legacy project contributing to the musical community beyond the private lesson.
Pillar 5
Self-Direction
The Stage 6 student becomes their own teacher — designing their practice, diagnosing their weaknesses, setting long-term artistic goals, and sustaining the discipline to pursue them without external accountability.
Pillar 6
The Lifelong Practice
Establishing the daily disciplines — in technique, sight-reading, improvisation, ear training — that will sustain musical growth for life. Not the curriculum’s end but its foundation.

The Stage 6 learning standardTechnique and interpretation as one act
  • ArtAt Stage 6, the sequence “learn notes → add technique → add interpretation” is collapsed. Every section is learned from bar one with technical accuracy, harmonic understanding, and interpretive intention simultaneously. A passage cannot be “technically correct” while “musically uninflected” — the musical inflection IS the technique. The arm weight that produces a singing legato is not independent of the musical choice: it is the musical choice, physically realised.10 min
  • ArtInterpretive position before the first note: before playing the mastery work, the student states their interpretive intention in three sentences. "This work is about ___. The journey it takes the listener on is ___. My specific contribution to the existing interpretations of this work is ___." The third sentence is the most important — it is what makes this performance irreplaceable rather than merely correct.10 min
  • ArtInterpretive courage: many technically accomplished performers play safely — within the range of interpretation clearly defensible, that no critic can fault. This safety is the enemy of memorability. A memorable interpretation commits to a specific, personal position that involves genuine artistic risk. "Play as if you are the only person who has ever understood this piece — and as if it is urgent that the audience understand it too."10 min
  • AdvancedFive specific interpretive commitments: for each mastery work, identify five interpretive decisions that are distinctly your own. Each is: (1) named; (2) justified in musical terms; and (3) consistently present in every performance. These five commitments are your artistic signature on the work — the place where your voice and the composer’s voice meet.10 min

Advanced memorisation — inhabiting a work
The difference between knowing a piece and inhabiting it. A pianist inhabits a work when the analytical memory, aural memory, and muscle memory are so redundantly active that no single point of failure can derail a performance.
Phase A
Sessions 3–4Memory at Stage 6 depth
  • AdvancedRandom start test — Stage 6 version: teacher names any bar in the mastery work. Student begins playing immediately in tempo. Teacher then interrupts with a harmonic question — "What chord is in bar 40?" — while the student continues playing. Then "What key are you in now?" All questions answered without stopping. Passed when analytical memory operates simultaneously with performance without either interfering with the other.15 min
  • AdvancedTransposition memorisation: play a fully memorised section transposed down a semitone. This eliminates muscle memory entirely — leaving only aural and analytical memory to guide the performance. If these two systems are fully active, transposition is possible (imperfectly, but continuously) within two to three weeks on any given section. If it is impossible, the analytical memory is not yet fully active.12 min
  • EarAural memory: student leaves the piano, faces away, and sings the mastery work — all voices, not just the melody. Inner voices, bass line, and harmonic changes all represented in the singing. Where the singing becomes uncertain is precisely where the performance is most vulnerable. These moments are the specific memorisation targets for the next week.13 min
  • ArtThe performance without preparation: teacher asks the student to perform a section immediately upon sitting at the piano, without any warm-up. "In a real performance, the first note is the first note. This reveals whether the piece is inhabitable at a moment’s notice — or whether it requires a preparatory ritual. The goal: the piece is always there, always complete, always ready."10 min
  • ArtSecond mastery work introduced in session 4: should contrast the first in at least two dimensions (era, character, technical demand). The programme arc of the two together is the first Phase A artistic decision. First play-through, three-sentence interpretive position, timeline estimate.10 min

03
The performance mindset — playing for real audiences consistently
Stage 6 performance is not an event — it is a discipline. Minimum two real-audience performances per month throughout Stage 6. The pre-performance ritual. The post-performance review. Working with adrenaline, not against it.
Phase A
Why regular performance is a technical discipline

The physiological changes during performance — elevated heart rate, increased adrenaline, heightened sensory awareness — are not problems to be overcome. They are the natural preparation of the human organism for a high-stakes activity. A performer who has performed regularly for years learns to work with these changes: using the additional energy for greater physical commitment, the heightened awareness for more intense musical listening. This learning only happens through repeated exposure. Stage 6 requires a minimum of two real-audience performances per month throughout its entire duration.

Sessions 5–6Performance ritual and post-performance review
  • ArtThe pre-performance ritual: every performance begins with a consistent ritual. (1) Physical warm-up: 10 minutes of scales and technical exercises at the performance venue — not at home. (2) Score review without playing: 5 minutes reading through the score mentally, confirming analytical memory for the opening of each piece. (3) The silence: 2 minutes — sit with the piano, settle the body, find physical stillness, hear the programme’s opening in the inner ear. (4) The intention: one sentence stated before sitting at the instrument: "Today this programme is about ___."12 min
  • ArtThe post-performance review: written within 24 hours of every performance. Three questions: (1) Where was the intention fully present in the music? (2) Where did the intention retreat — and what caused the retreat? (3) What would I do differently in the next performance of this programme? The review is diagnostic, not self-critical. The performer who can answer precisely is developing the self-knowledge that makes each performance better than the last.10 min
  • ArtThird mastery work introduced in session 6: the third work completes the Phase A programme. Three works in development simultaneously means managing 40–60 minutes of music in parallel — the most demanding time-management challenge of Stage 6. Programme arc designed: which piece opens (establishes the world), which is the centrepiece (most substantial), which closes (leaves the final impression)?15 min
  • ArtBreath as the anchor: the most effective in-performance technique for managing the performance state is breath. A deliberate complete out-breath before the first note resets the nervous system’s baseline. "Play the breath, not the notes. The notes happen inside the breath." Practice this explicitly: in-breath before the phrase begins, out-breath through the phrase’s arc, natural pause at the phrase end before the next breath begins.8 min
  • ArtFirst Stage 6 performance: arrange a small-audience performance within the first four weeks — friends, family, or a local community. The performance is informal, but the pre-performance ritual and post-performance review are used exactly as in a formal concert. "The first performance of Stage 6 begins your performance log. At the end of Stage 6, this log will contain 35–72 entries — the most detailed account of your artistic development that exists."5 min

04
Interpretive depth — the performance that changes the listener
The difference between a correct performance and a memorable one. What the listener actually experiences. The score-free performance and the trust it requires. Comparative listening as a tool for deepening interpretation.
Phase A
Sessions 7–8What changes the listener
  • Art"A correct performance disappears from memory within 24 hours. A performance in which the performer was genuinely present — in which something was genuinely at stake — is remembered for years. The difference is not technical. It is human." What makes a performance genuinely present: complete commitment to the musical intention, knowledge so deep that attention is entirely inside the music, and the willingness to be vulnerable — to let the music matter, visibly.10 min
  • ArtWhat the score cannot tell you: the score specifies pitches, rhythms, dynamics, and articulation. It does not specify: the exact character of a pianissimo (distant? intimate? fragile? menacing?); the precise rate of a ritardando; the emotional weight carried into a chord; or the silence before the first note. All of these are the performer’s contribution. "A score is a set of instructions for a performance, not the performance itself."10 min
  • EarComparative listening: listen to three recordings of the same passage from one mastery work by different performers. "What does each performer add that the score does not specify? What do they omit that the score requires? Which interpretation most closely resembles yours — and which most challenges yours?" The interpreter who challenges your interpretation is the most valuable — they reveal assumptions you have been making invisibly.12 min
  • AdvancedScore-free performance: when a work is genuinely inhabited — all four memory systems fully active — the score is redundant. Performing without it is not a risk. It is simply acknowledging what you already know. Practice performing the most fully-learned mastery work section score-free, exploring where the eyes go: the keyboard (deepens physical engagement with the touch), the space above (creates a visual focus that enhances concentration), or closed (intensifies the inner ear’s activity). Which produces the best musical result?18 min

05
Chamber music and collaborative playing
Playing with other musicians as accompanist, duo partner, or ensemble member. The relational listening that only ensemble playing develops. What ensemble playing gives back to solo playing.
Phase A
Sessions 9–10Ensemble disciplines
  • ArtAccompaniment at Stage 6: accompanying a singer or instrumentalist in a Schubert Lied, violin sonata, or concerto reduction. At Stage 6, accompaniment is a full artistic engagement — the accompanist makes interpretive decisions as significant as the soloist’s, simply in service of the soloist’s musical line. "The great accompanist is the soloist’s musical shadow — always present, always ready to catch."15 min
  • ArtThe dynamic recalibration: in ensemble, the piano is almost always too loud — not because the pianist plays loudly, but because the piano’s sustain accumulates while other instruments’ sounds decay. The pianist should use the dynamic that feels uncomfortably soft. When that level feels natural, the balance is probably correct. This recalibration is one of the most valuable byproducts of regular ensemble playing and transfers permanently to solo playing.10 min
  • ArtWhat ensemble gives back: after a chamber music session, the solo playing changes for one to three days. Inner voices become more audible, harmonic rhythm more alive, melodic line more conversational. This is the result of relational listening being temporarily applied to solo playing. Stage 6 schedules ensemble sessions deliberately the day before important solo performance work to exploit this effect.10 min
  • ArtPiano duet: two pianists at one piano creates the most transparent ensemble — every rhythmic and dynamic decision is immediately audible in the other player’s response. Begin with a Schubert Fantasia or Brahms Hungarian Dance. "In four-hand playing, you cannot hide. Every habit — of rushing, of dynamic imbalance — is instantly heard by your partner. This honesty is the most efficient performance feedback available."10 min
  • ArtProgramme design: with three mastery works now in development, the programme arc is designed. "A recital programme is a composition in three movements. The relationship between the pieces — in key, character, era, duration, and emotional content — is as intentional as the relationship between the movements of a symphony. You do not choose pieces and then arrange them. You compose a programme."5 min

09
Extended composition — the major original work begins
The 5–10 minute original work as an artistic statement equivalent in ambition to the mastery repertoire. Compositional seed. Form map. Harmonic planning. The first 16 bars composed.
Phase B
Teacher’s framing for Phase B

The major compositional work of Phase B is not a demonstration of compositional technique. It is the most direct statement of the student’s musical identity available — more direct than any interpretation of someone else’s music, because it involves no inherited constraints. The work must be genuine: it must say something the student actually wants to say, in a language authentically their own, in a form that serves the content rather than constraining it. The teacher’s role in Phase B is not to evaluate or improve the composition — it is to ask questions that help the student articulate and realise their own intention. "Is this what you meant to say?" is the only relevant question.

Sessions 17–18Compositional process at scale
  • ComposeThe compositional seed: from improvisation recordings made in weeks 1–8, identify the single most compelling musical idea — the idea most strongly carrying the three Stage 5 fingerprints, the idea most wanted to develop. This is the seed of the major work. It may be melodic, harmonic, rhythmic, or textural. Short (4–8 bars) and feeling inevitable — as if it could not have been any other way.10 min
  • ComposeForm design before composing: four landmarks are the structural skeleton. (1) Beginning: character, key, texture, dynamic level. (2) Turning point: the moment the music commits to its journey. (3) Climax: highest tension — dynamically, harmonically, emotionally. (4) Resolution: the arrival that makes the whole journey feel inevitable. Everything else is flesh.12 min
  • ComposeHarmonic planning: before writing bar 5, decide the harmonic world of the entire work. What is the home key? Most distant key visited — and why? Which chromatic chord (diminished seventh, augmented sixth, Neapolitan) appears at or near the climax? What is the final harmonic gesture — resolution, irresolution, or open ending? These decisions are made consciously before composing begins — or confirmed consciously as they emerge.10 min
  • ComposeThe first 16 bars: compose, notate, and play. Then: "Is this what you meant? Is the character what you intended? Is the harmonic opening establishing the world you designed? Does the first phrase have a shape — a question that demands an answer?" Every bar at Stage 6 composition is intentional. Nothing is written because it “sounds all right” — everything is written because it is the next necessary thing in the argument the music is making.18 min

10
Development — five tools for thematic argument
Sequence, fragmentation, augmentation, diminution, and inversion. The development section as an argument, not a passage. Planning the climax backward. Analysing development in one mastery work.
Phase B
Sessions 19–20Thematic development in practice
  • ComposeThe five development tools: (1) Sequence — idea repeated one step higher or lower (creates momentum); (2) Fragmentation — only part of the idea repeated (creates urgency); (3) Augmentation — idea in longer note values (creates weight); (4) Diminution — idea in shorter note values (creates excitement); (5) Inversion — idea upside down (creates surprise). Apply all five to the compositional seed. Evaluate: which transformation most advances the emotional argument at this point?15 min
  • ComposePlanning the climax backward: the climax must be earned. Plan it specifically: which bar? Which key? Which chromatic chord? What dynamic? What texture? Then compose backward from the climax to the current development section — "What must happen before bar 67 for bar 67 to feel inevitable?" This backward planning is one of the most powerful compositional techniques available.12 min
  • RepertoireAnalyse the development section of one mastery work: "How does Beethoven (or Chopin, or Schubert) develop the main themes? Which of the five tools appear, and in which order? What is the harmonic trajectory? Where is the climax, and how has it been prepared?" This analysis feeds directly into the student’s own compositional development — not as a model to copy, but as evidence of how a master composer solves the same problem currently being faced.13 min
  • ComposeThe development’s first 32 bars: composed and notated this week using at least three of the five tools. Each tool chosen for what it does to the emotional argument at that specific moment — not for variety.10 min

11
Resolution and the complete first draft
The recapitulation and resolution. The ending as the completion of the emotional argument. The complete first draft played through without stopping. First-impression self-assessment.
Phase B
Sessions 21–22Resolution and the earned return
  • ComposeThe recapitulation: the return of the main theme in the home key after the development must feel like a return, not a repeat. "The theme is the same — but the listener who has been through the development is different. Play to that changed listener." The recapitulation is composed with this emotional transformation in mind: the same theme, in the same key, but carrying the weight of everything that has happened since it was first heard.15 min
  • ComposeThe ending: the last 8–16 bars are the most important compositional decision of Phase B. Three options: (1) Clear, settled cadence — the answer to the question posed in bar 1; (2) Sustained final note or chord that dissolves into silence — the argument is made, the silence completes it; (3) Open ending — the work ends, but the listener continues thinking about it. The student chooses deliberately, having considered all three, and defends the choice in musical and artistic terms.12 min
  • ComposeComplete first draft: by end of session 22, the complete first draft exists. Play through from beginning to end without stopping. First impression: does it feel like a complete work? Does the emotional journey arrive where designed? What is the most glaring gap between intention and result? Write these three assessments immediately — they guide the revision.20 min
  • Art"Composing a piece and interpreting it are not different activities. When you decided where the climax of your work should be, you were making the same decision as a Beethoven sonata performer who decides how to shape the development section. The compositional decisions you have made in Phase B will permanently change how you perform other composers’ music — because you now understand what those decisions feel like from the inside."3 min

Phase C
Why community performance matters at Stage 6

A performing musician who only plays in concert halls reaches audiences who have already decided classical piano music is for them. A musician who plays in schools, hospitals, and community centres reaches people who have never made that decision. These audiences respond only to what genuinely moves them — there is no social contract requiring polite listening. Playing for these audiences is the most revealing performance context available: when it works, it works for the most fundamental musical reasons, stripped of the scaffolding that supports conventional concert performance.
