The Stage 5 Gateway Programme

Unlike earlier stages, the Stage 5 gateway is a five-work recital programme: one substantial piece from each of the four major eras, plus an original composition by the student. Chosen for personal meaning, not technical safety.

The Stage 5 gateway question"Does your playing have a recognisable voice? Not perfect — not unprecedented — but consistent, personal, and unmistakably individual." A listener who heard only the performance should describe the performer's musical personality in two or three consistent terms.

Phase A — Era Mastery

Weeks 1–6 · Sessions 1–12 · Four eras as four physical languages. By Week 6 the student identifies any piece's era by ear and adjusts their entire physical and musical approach accordingly — before playing a note.

Week 1 · Sessions 1–2
Baroque — Non-Legato, Terraced Dynamics, Dance Character
Era identification test · Physical calibration on middle C · Non-legato vs. legato · Baroque ornament vocabulary · Dance characters as body knowledge
Phase A
▾
Session 1 Era identification and Baroque physical approach
EarEra identification test: teacher plays 10 short excerpts unseen. Student identifies era of each. Pass: 8/10. Where the student cannot identify reveals which era to prioritise in Phase A. 10 min.
ArtPhysical calibration — four eras on middle C: (1) Baroque: fingertip, non-legato, no pedal; (2) Classical: warmer, phrase-shaped, tiny pedal; (3) Romantic: full arm weight, immediate pedal, singing legato; (4) 20th century: precisely as marked. The era-reset must become instantaneous — a complete physical and mental shift. 10 min.
ArtNon-legato Baroque articulation: play a Bach Menuet with every note non-legato — full value but released before the next. Compare to legato version. "The non-legato speaks. The legato sings. Bach speaks." 10 min.
TheoryTerraced dynamics: forte and piano are structural step-changes, not gradual swells. "The Baroque crescendo is a step, not a slope." 8 min.
RepertoireBach French Suite or Handel Suite: full Baroque approach — non-legato, terraced dynamics, no pedal. Compare to harpsichord recording. 12 min.
ArtEra-switch exercise: teacher calls an era mid-phrase — student adjusts within one bar. If it takes longer, the physical language is still conscious rather than habitual. 5 min.
Session 2 Baroque ornament vocabulary and dance characters
AdvancedComplete ornament vocabulary from C.P.E. Bach's 1753 table: trill, mordent, turn, Schleifer (rising slide from a third below), Nachschlag (closing note after trill). Study five ornaments; each beginning from the upper note. Identify each in a Bach Invention. 15 min.
TheoryBaroque dance characters: Allemande (moderate 4/4, dignified walk); Courante (flowing 3, running); Sarabande (slow 3/4, emphasis beat 2, solemn); Gigue (fast compound, jig). These are physical characters felt in the body — not tempo markings on a page. 8 min.
EarDance identification: teacher plays four pieces without showing the score. Student identifies the dance form by rhythmic character alone. 8 min.
RepertoirePerform one complete Baroque Suite movement with the correct dance character physically embodied. Teacher evaluates: "Does this feel like a dance or a piano study?" 12 min.
Central premise of Phase AA pianist who plays Bach like Chopin and Chopin like Debussy is not playing any of them well. Each era has a distinct performance practice — conventions about articulation, tempo, ornamentation, dynamics, and pedalling that the music was written to exploit. These conventions are not restrictions; they are the grammar of each era's musical language.

Week 2 · Sessions 3–4
Classical — Clarity, Wit, and the Conversational Phrase
Slur-gap articulation · Alberti bass at 40% · Haydn's wit · Mozart as hardest composer to play well · Beethoven: Classical form, Romantic content
Phase A
▾
Sessions 3–4 Mozart, Haydn, and Classical discipline
ArtSlur-gap articulation: slurs are short (2–4 notes), staccato dots crisp, adjacent slurs separated by a tiny gap. "The slur-gap version speaks. The legato version sings. Mozart speaks." 10 min.
TheoryAlberti bass rule: LH should be 40% of the RH melody dynamic. Practice any Mozart Sonata with LH at pp throughout regardless of RH dynamic. The result is usually the correct balance. 8 min.
EarHaydn's wit: identify every structural surprise before performing — deceptive cadences, sudden subito pianos, key ambiguities. "Haydn is a comedian — play the jokes." 8 min.
RepertoireMozart K.331 or K.545: full Classical performance — slur-gap, 40% Alberti bass, minimal pedal, restrained dynamics. Compare to Mitsuko Uchida or Robert Levin recording. "What do they do that you did not? What do they omit that you added?" 14 min.
ArtBeethoven Op. 2: "Play it as if Mozart — until the moment it stops being Mozart. Then let it be Beethoven." Finding that exact transition point is the interpretive work of the week. 15 min.
Why Classical is harder than RomanticClassical music punishes generous gestures — every note is exposed. A blurred ornament in Schubert is forgivable; in Mozart it sounds crude. The performer's job is not to add expression but to reveal the expression already written.
Week 3 · Sessions 5–6
Romantic — Four Composers, Four Distinct Voices
Schubert's flat-VI · Chopin's LH-steady rubato · Schumann's literary characters · Brahms's three dynamic levels · Liszt as poet
Phase A
▾
Sessions 5–6 Four Romantic voices
ArtSchubert's flat-VI: the move from major to the key a minor third above (C→Eb) creates unexpected warmth. At every flat-VI arrival: slow fractionally, give the chord slightly more weight and pedal, let the new key settle. Never rush past it. 8 min.
ArtChopin's rubato: the LH is the conductor and maintains tempo; the RH is the singer and deviates freely. Practice explicitly: LH strict time, RH completely free. The LH never adjusts. 8 min.
ArtSchumann's literary references: read the title before every practice session. "The Happy Farmer" has a completely different body from "Träumerei." 6 min.
ArtBrahms's three-level voice: primary melody (top), counter-melody of near-equal importance (inner), harmonic foundation (bass). All three heard in proportion — three simultaneous dynamic levels in one hand. 6 min.
RepertoirePlay one excerpt from each of the four Romantic composers. For each: announce the composer and adjust the entire physical and emotional approach. Teacher evaluates: does each excerpt sound written by a different person? 12 min.
Week 4 · Sessions 7–8
20th Century — Five Composers, Five Sonic Worlds
Debussy: overtone pedalling · Ravel: mechanical precision · Bartók: percussion instrument · Prokofiev: sardonic and lyrical · No single performance practice
Phase A
▾
Sessions 7–8 Four 20th-century voices
SoundDebussy — overtone pedalling: press before the first note, change slowly with half-pedal releases, let adjacent harmonies blur intentionally. The blur IS the sound. 8 min.
SoundRavel — mechanical precision with warmth: absolute rhythmic precision, very clean finger work, minimal pedal, every inner voice audible. "A Swiss watchmaker who happens to be French." 6 min.
SoundBartók — the percussion instrument: dry, non-pedalled, sharp arm-weight attacks. No portato. The Mikrokosmos studies are the curriculum. 6 min.
SoundProkofiev — sardonic wit and lyrical warmth: dry/mechanical in ironic passages; warm and singing in lyrical ones. "Prokofiev is funny — but his slow movements will break your heart. Play both extremes fully." 6 min.
RepertoirePlay one excerpt from each composer. State the sonic intention before playing. Teacher evaluates: does the stated intention match the heard result? 14 min.
The 20th century has no single performance practiceEvery major 20th-century composer requires individual research. The physical approach must be derived from the specific sonic goal of each composer — not from a generic "modern" style.

Phase B — Improvisation

Weeks 7–12 · Sessions 13–24 · The direct voice. One rule: never stop. From black-key pentatonic through modal, blues, jazz harmony, and Classical-style improvisation. Five minutes every lesson and home practice session from Week 6 forward — no exceptions.

The one rule of improvisation trainingThere are no wrong notes in the practice context — only notes that have not yet been followed by the right continuation. The teacher never says "that was wrong" during an improvisation. Only: "keep going."
Weeks 7–8 · Sessions 13–16
Pentatonic Freedom and Blues
Black keys — no wrong notes · Shape: question-answer, call-response · A minor pentatonic + blue note · 12-bar blues frame · Emotional authenticity test
Phase B
▾
Sessions 13–14 Black keys and pentatonic shape
ImproviseBlack-key rule: play only F#, G#, A#, C#, D#. Any combination sounds beautiful — no dissonances possible. Teacher plays a steady bass in the low register. Student improvises in RH for 3 minutes continuously. If the student stops: "Keep going — there are no wrong notes here." 8 min.
ImproviseShape the melody: begin softly in the middle register, rise to the highest notes (climax), descend and soften back. "A piece of music is a shape in time. You already know what a good shape is — now make the shape yourself." 8 min.
ImproviseCall and response: teacher plays a 2-bar phrase. Student responds with their own 2-bar phrase. Then reverse. "Every piece of music is a conversation. Improvisation makes the conversation explicit." 8 min.
Art"Every composer was an improviser first. Bach improvised fugues at the organ. Chopin improvised Nocturnes before writing them down. The notation is a record of an improvisation — not the improvisation itself." 8 min.
Sessions 15–16 12-bar blues and emotional depth
ImproviseA minor pentatonic (A–C–D–E–G): improvise a 4-minute piece with three chapters: melancholic opening, building urgency, resolution or resignation. "This is not an exercise. This is a piece of music with your name on it." 8 min.
ImproviseThe blue note: add Eb to A minor pentatonic. "The blue note contains both the pain and the resolution in a single note." 8 min.
Theory12-bar blues in C: C7 (4 bars) → F7 (2) → C7 (2) → G7 (1) → F7 (1) → C7 (2). LH plays chord 3rds and 7ths; RH improvises blues pentatonic. Four cycles = a complete performed piece with a four-section arc. 10 min.
ImproviseEmotional authenticity test: play the same 8-bar phrase three times — thinking about something happy, sad, then angry. Record all three. "Are they different? If yes, emotional state is a deliberate performance tool." 14 min.
Weeks 9–10 · Sessions 17–20
Modal Improvisation and Jazz Harmony
Dorian · Mixolydian · Phrygian · ii–V–I and walking bass · First jazz standard · Swing from listening not calculation
Phase B
▾
Sessions 17–18 Three primary modes
ImproviseD Dorian: improvise 5 minutes over sustained D bass. The raised sixth (B natural) gives Dorian its "melancholy but not defeated" quality. "This is the sound of medieval plainchant, Celtic folk, and John Coltrane." 8 min.
ImproviseG Mixolydian: the flat seventh creates an open, powerful quality — a major scale refusing to commit to leading-tone resolution. "This is the sound of Celtic folk, rock and roll, and most pop music of the last 70 years." 8 min.
ImproviseE Phrygian: the flat second (F immediately above E) creates a distinctly Spanish or ancient quality. "Play with a held E bass — this is the sound of flamenco and Gregorian chant." 8 min.
ImproviseMode-switching: improvise a continuous piece moving through three modes. The last note of one mode becomes the first note of the next — no gap, no reset, just a change of harmonic character. 8 min.
Sessions 19–20 ii–V–I and the jazz standard
Theoryii–V–I in C: Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7. Voice the 3rd and 7th of each chord in the LH (the colour tones). RH improvises C major pentatonic over I, G blues pentatonic over V. Cycle eight times. 10 min.
ImproviseWalking bass with melody: LH walks four-beat bass line; RH improvises. Practice LH alone for 8 minutes first. When combined, the walking bass must be completely automatic. 12 min.
RepertoireFirst jazz standard: "Autumn Leaves" or "All The Things You Are." Learn melody from lead sheet. Play melody once, then improvise over chord changes. 13 min.
SoundSwing rhythm: felt in the body, not calculated. Listen to Erroll Garner, Oscar Peterson, or Bill Evans for 5 minutes before playing. 10 min.

Arrangement — The Five Elements and Liszt's Method
Five elements that must survive · Texture map · Reharmonisation · First arrangement draft
Phase C
▾
Sessions 25–26 Arrangement fundamentals
Art"A transcription reproduces in another medium — fidelity is the goal. An arrangement transforms — a new version that lives naturally in the new medium. Liszt was the greatest arranger of the 19th century. Phase C teaches you to do the same." 8 min.
TheoryFive elements that must survive: (1) melody; (2) harmonic language; (3) rhythmic character; (4) emotional character; (5) climax structure. Any element lost makes the arrangement unrecognisable or unsatisfying. 10 min.

Stage 5 Gateway Assessment — Session 64

Six components. The decisive question: does this playing have a recognisable voice? Not perfect — not unprecedented — but consistent, personal, and unmistakably individual.

The Stage 5 Gateway — What It Tests

The Stage 5 gateway is an identity assessment. The student who passes has found their place within the tradition they have inherited — not a new musical language, but their own voice within the existing one.

01

Era Performance Mastery

Teacher plays opening 8 bars of each gateway piece in the wrong era style. Student diagnoses and corrects verbally and physically within 10 seconds per piece. Then: teacher calls an era at random; student plays any 4-bar passage in that style without preparation.

Recommended Stage 5 Repertoire

Organised by phase. The gateway programme is chosen by the student for personal meaning — one substantial work per era plus an original composition.

Phase A · Baroque
Non-legato, terraced dynamics, dance character
Bach French Suites · WTC Preludes · Handel Suites · Scarlatti Sonatas
Non-legato, terraced dynamics, ornaments from upper note. Each Baroque Suite movement has a distinct dance character that must be embodied, not just counted.
Phase A · Classical
Slur-gap, 40% Alberti, Haydn wit
Mozart K.331, K.545, K.570 · Haydn Hob. XVI:37 · Beethoven Op. 2, Op. 13
Slur-gap articulation throughout. 40% Alberti bass. Haydn surprises performed as jokes. Beethoven: find the exact bar where Classical form becomes Romantic content.
Phase A · Romantic
Four composers, four distinct voices
Schubert Op. 90 · Chopin Nocturnes · Schumann Kinderszenen · Brahms Op. 118 · Liszt Consolations
Each composer is a different person with a different body. Schubert: flat-VI lean. Chopin: LH-steady rubato. Schumann: read the title before every practice. Brahms: three simultaneous dynamic levels.
Phase A · 20th Century
Composer-specific approach
Debussy Arabesques · Ravel Sonatine · Bartók Mikrokosmos IV–VI · Prokofiev Visions Fugitives
No generic "modern" approach. Debussy: blur IS the sound. Ravel: precision is warmth. Bartók: percussion instrument. Prokofiev: sardonic and lyrical in the same piece.
Phase B · Improvisation models
Jazz standards and Classical improv vehicles
"Autumn Leaves" · "All the Things You Are" · Schubert Lieder for Classical cadenzas · Haydn Sonata fermatas
Jazz standards: learn melody by ear, improvise over chord changes. Classical improv: cadenzas grown organically from the piece's material. Baroque preludes: arpeggiated I–ii7–V7–I in continuous 16th notes.
Phase C · Arrangement models
Liszt, Bach-Busoni, and contemporary
Liszt Schubert transcriptions · Bach-Busoni Chaconne · Brahms arrangements of own songs
Five elements must survive: melody, harmonic language, rhythmic character, emotional character, climax structure. Liszt's method: create something that lives naturally at the piano.
Phases D–E · Gateway programme (student's choice)
One substantial work per era + original composition
Baroque: Bach WTC Prelude & Fugue, French Suite · Classical: Beethoven Bagatelle, Haydn or Mozart Sonata movement · Romantic: Schubert Impromptu, Chopin Nocturne, Brahms Intermezzo · 20th C: Debussy Arabesque, Ravel Sonatine, Bartók Mikrokosmos V–VI · Composition: 32–48 bars by the student
Chosen for personal meaning, not technical safety. Each piece must be one the student genuinely wants to perform. The programme's arc — how the five works relate — is itself an artistic decision the student makes and defends in the gateway.

Teacher Quick Reference — Stage 5

The teacher's role has shifted from instructor to collaborator. These cheat sheets reflect that shift.

🎯

Session Opening — Every Session, Stage 5

Always begin with

  • Era-reset ritual: 30 seconds of silence, then first gateway piece — the era must be present from note one
  • 5 minutes improvisation: no theme, no preparation — whatever comes. Teacher never corrects during these 5 minutes
  • Reading log check: is the daily sight-reading habit maintained at Grade 7?
  • One Stage 4 technical warm-up: octaves, double notes, trills, or polyrhythm — background now, not work

The teacher's role in Stage 5

  • Collaborator, not instructor — the student's emerging voice is reflected, questioned, deepened — not redirected
  • Ask "Is this you?" more than "Is this correct?" — correctness was Stage 4; identity is Stage 5
  • When the student's interpretation surprises you — say so. That surprise is data about where their voice is
  • Specific feedback only: never "play with more feeling" — always "what does this chord mean to you, and how will you communicate that?"
⚠️

Common Stage 5 Challenges — What to Say

Era and style challenges

  • Bach played like Chopin: "Play this with the lid closed — imagine you are playing a harpsichord. You cannot control the volume after the key goes down. Only the rhythm and articulation are yours." Then open the lid and maintain that restraint.
  • Mozart too Romantic: "Every note you add — dynamics, pedal, rubato — ask: did Mozart write this? If not, remove it. Trust the notes."
  • Debussy too clean: "Press the pedal before you play the first note. Now play. The blur is already there before the melody arrives. That blur is where Debussy lives."
  • Chopin rubato with LH following: "LH only — four bars at strict tempo. Now RH only — same four bars, completely free. Now together, but LH does not know the RH exists."

Improvisation and voice challenges

  • Student stops during improvisation: "Keep going. Whatever just happened — follow it. There are no wrong notes, only notes not yet continued."
  • Improvisation has no shape: "Before you start: decide three things — where you will begin (register, dynamic), where the climax will be, and how it will end. Then play."
  • Voice statement too generic: "You wrote 'I love expressive music.' So does everyone. Name a specific thing your hands always do that no one told them to."
  • Fingerprints not audible in composition: "Play bars 12–16 of your composition and then bars 12–16 of your Romantic gateway piece. Point to where the same person made both."
🎭

Musical Language — Phrases That Work in Stage 5

For era mastery

  • "Bach speaks. Mozart speaks. Chopin sings. Debussy shimmers. Which one are you about to be?"
  • "Haydn is a comedian — play the jokes."
  • "The Baroque crescendo is a step, not a slope."
  • "Debussy: the blur IS the sound — it is not a mistake."
  • "Play it as if Mozart — until the moment it stops being Mozart."

For voice and identity

  • "What does this chord mean to you? Play it so I feel that."
  • "Every composition is an improvisation that got written down."
  • "The notation is a record of an improvisation — not the improvisation itself."
  • "Your fingerprints must be heard in the music — not just in the performance."
  • "Stage 6 begins with the question: now that you know who you are, what will you make of it?"
📝

What Changes in Stage 6

Stage 6 is Artistry and Legacy. The student who passes Stage 5 has found their voice. Stage 6 asks what to do with it — over a lifetime. Not adding more skills or deepening more knowledge, but contributing: performing, composing, teaching, and contributing to the musical community in ways that outlast the individual performing career.

The Stage 6 gateway certifies artistic maturity, not just technical accomplishment — a deliberate philosophical distinction in the LUBENO framework. The student who arrives at Stage 6 does not ask "What should I do next?" They ask "What must I do?" That imperative is the beginning of artistry.
