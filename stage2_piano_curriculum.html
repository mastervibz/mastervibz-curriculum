Coordination — Hands and Reading

01
The inner-singing method — why it works
The foundational technique for all HT coordination. Before any HT piece is attempted, the student must own the inner-singing drill.
Phase A
The central problem of Stage 2

Hand independence is not a mechanical problem — it is an attention problem. When both hands play simultaneously, the brain defaults to treating them as one unit and synchronises them. The result: rhythmic lockstep, the left hand copying the right hand's dynamics, and the inability to differentiate two musical lines.

The inner-singing method is the most effective solution known: the student plays one hand while singing the other. The voice bridges the two hemispheres of brain activity required. Once the student can do this fluently, HT coordination follows naturally — because the musical content of each hand has been internalised separately before being combined.

Session 1 — The singing-while-playing drill
First 20 min Inner singing: RH plays, LH sung
  • CoordTake the simplest piece from Stage 1 with both hands. Student plays the RH while speaking/singing the LH note names aloud. Start at a very slow tempo — the voice must genuinely name the note, not mumble.8 min
  • CoordNow reverse: LH plays, student sings/speaks the RH note names. Same slow tempo. This direction is usually harder — notice which hand the student is "weaker" at leading mentally.8 min
  • EarFinal step: play both hands together. The student will notice HT is easier than expected — because both lines have been fully internalised. Teacher confirms: "This is always how we prepare a new piece."4 min
Next 15 min New short HT piece — full process
  • EarTeacher plays the new piece in full. Student listens with eyes closed and describes what they hear: "Which hand has the melody? Does the LH have a pattern or does it move freely?" Listening analysis before any playing.5 min
  • RepertoireStudent reads and plays RH alone, singing along. Then LH alone, singing along. Then RH while singing LH. Process takes time — this is the work.7 min
  • SoundHT attempt. Set one rule: if the singing stops, the hands stop. The voice is load-bearing — not decorative. First HT run at whatever tempo allows continuous voice.3 min
Session 2 — Rhythmic independence: different values HT
First 20 min Long note in one hand, moving in the other
  • CoordExercise: LH holds a whole note (C, four counts) while RH plays C–D–E–F in quarter notes. The LH must not "pulse" with the RH — it holds, genuinely still. Student taps foot for pulse.8 min
  • CoordReverse: RH holds whole note while LH plays a four-quarter pattern. Listen: does the sustained hand keep ringing? Does it resist the urge to accent with the other hand?7 min
  • EarTeacher plays both versions — one where both hands accent together (wrong) and one where they're independent. Student identifies which is which by ear. "Your ear is better at this than your hands — let the ear lead the correction."5 min
Next 15 min Dotted notes and the held-plus-moving pattern
  • KeysIntroduce dotted half note (3 counts) as a notation concept. Clap-and-count first: "ta-a-a." Then play on a single note. Teacher counts aloud — student holds for exactly three beats.5 min
  • RepertoireNew piece featuring dotted half notes in one hand vs. quarter notes in the other. Apply full inner-singing preparation before any HT attempt. This piece is the week's home practice target.10 min
Watch for — Week 1
  • Student mouthing the note names silently rather than singing audibly — the voice must be heard; silent mouthing bypasses the auditory feedback loop that makes the drill work
  • Hands rushing to HT before each hand is individually secure — enforce the process: HS RH, HS LH, RH play + LH sing, LH play + RH sing, then HT
  • LH thumping on long held notes — the sustained hand tends to accent sympathetically; ask student to let the held note simply ring, as if it's forgotten
Home practice — Week 1 (20 min/day)
8 min — Dotted-half piece: full inner-singing preparation before any HT. Log which direction (RH play/LH sing vs. LH play/RH sing) is harder — report to teacher next session.
7 min — Long-note independence exercise: LH whole note + RH quarters, then reverse. Foot taps pulse throughout.
5 min — Stage 1 piece review: play any Stage 1 piece to consolidate tone quality and legato. "Don't forget where you came from."
02
Position shifts and thumb crossings
The hand must learn to travel. Thumb-under crossings for both hands. The five-finger position expands.
Phase A
Teacher's framing for this week

The five-finger position of Stage 1 was a necessary limitation — it gave the student a home. Now we teach the hand to move. Thumb crossings are the mechanism that allows the hand to travel across the keyboard seamlessly. They feel awkward for every beginner. The correction is always the same: the thumb must slide under the palm invisibly — the wrist stays level and the hand does not rock.

Session 3 — Thumb crossing: the C major scale begins
First 20 min The thumb-under mechanism
  • BodyDemonstrate on the fallboard (off keys): fingers 1–2–3 tap, then thumb slides under to arrive at the position of finger 4's note before that finger lifts. The thumb is already in place — it does not swing up to meet the key. This is the key mechanical insight.5 min
  • BodyRH only: C–D–E, thumb crosses to F. Stop. Reset. Repeat 8 times. The crossing must be invisible — no wrist lurch, no shoulder dip. "A sleeping cat walks across your hand and feels nothing."8 min
  • EarListen test: teacher plays C–D–E–F with visible wrist rock vs. smooth thumb crossing. Student identifies the difference by ear — the rocky version produces an accent or unevenness on F. The ear hears what the eye misses.7 min
Next 15 min C major scale — one octave, RH
  • SoundIntroduce the complete one-octave C major scale: C–D–E (cross) F–G–A–B–C. Sing it as do-re-mi first. Every note the same tone quality — no accent on the thumb crossing. This is the test.5 min
  • SoundPlay descending: C–B–A–G (finger 3 crosses over thumb to F)–E–D–C. The over-crossing is a different challenge — finger 3 reaches over without collapsing the wrist inward. Practice this direction separately.5 min
  • RepertoireScale as music: play C major scale ascending with a crescendo (soft start, louder at the top), descending with a diminuendo (loud at top, soft return). The scale is not an exercise — it is a melodic arc. Sing along.5 min
Session 4 — LH position shifts and the G position
First 20 min G position and lateral movement
  • KeysIntroduce G position: RH thumb on G, five fingers spanning G–A–B–C–D. LH fifth finger on G below, spanning G–A–B–C–D. This is the first position shift away from C. Play the five notes in both hands. Same weight, same tone quality.6 min
  • BodyLateral arm movement drill: play C position five-note scale slowly, then without lifting the wrist, slide the whole arm one position to the right. Arrive at G position. The arm travels — the hand shape stays identical. "The hand is a passenger. The arm drives."8 min
  • RepertoireShort piece using C position for 4 bars, then G position for 4 bars. The shift must be smooth — no pause, no re-positioning fidget. The hand simply arrives at the new home.6 min
Next 15 min HT piece with position changes
  • CoordIntroduce a piece where RH shifts position mid-piece while LH stays in C position. Apply full inner-singing preparation. The challenge: the singing must account for the RH's new note set after the shift.10 min
  • EarHarmonic listening: when both hands are in C position together, hum a single note that fits (C, E, or G). When RH shifts to G, which notes in the LH still fit? This is the earliest harmonic awareness — not theory, just ear.5 min
Watch for — Week 2
  • Wrist lurching inward during finger-3 over-crossing on descending scale — the over-cross must happen with the wrist staying neutral; correct by slowing to half tempo and watching the wrist
  • Position shifts done with a visible pause or "look-down" — the hand should know where it's going before the phrase ends; practice the arrival separately, eyes closed
  • Accent on the note after the thumb crossing — the thumb note is never louder than its neighbours; teacher should be able to close eyes and not hear where the crossing occurs
Home practice — Week 2 (20 min/day)
5 min — C major scale RH, ascending and descending, with dynamic arc. Eyes closed — listen for evenness at the crossing point.
8 min — HT piece with position change: full process (HS each, then cross-singing, then HT).
7 min — G position five-finger exercise: LH and RH separately, then together in unison. Identical tone quality in both hands.
03
Reading rhythmic complexity — ties, rests, 3/4 time
The pulse subdivides. Silence becomes musical. Waltz time introduces a new metric feel.
Phase A
Teacher's framing for this week

Rests are not pauses in the music — they are active silence. The student must feel a rest as a held breath, not as an absence of action. A rest that is not felt in the body will always be rushed or skipped. Ties are the opposite problem: students routinely re-attack a tied note. These two elements reveal whether the student truly hears notation or is just reading symbols.

Session 5 — Ties, rests, and the silent pulse
First 20 min Rests as active silence
  • EarTeacher claps a 4-beat rhythm with a rest on beat 3: clap–clap–(silence)–clap. Student echoes. Then student must tap foot on beat 3 to keep the pulse during the rest. "The pulse never stops — only the sound does."5 min
  • KeysQuarter rest on the piano: play C–D–(rest)–E–F. The rest hand lifts — but the foot pulse continues. Student should feel a physical impulse on beat 3 even with no sound. Teacher listens: is the E on beat 4 perfectly in time?8 min
  • RepertoirePiece with quarter and half rests in both hands (at different moments). The coordination challenge: RH rests while LH plays, and vice versa. Inner-singing covers the resting hand's silence.7 min
Next 15 min Ties — no re-attack, continuous ring
  • KeysDefine the tie physically: play C (quarter), tie to C (half note). The finger stays depressed — the key never comes back up. Teacher places a hand over the student's to feel: no bounce on the barline. Three counts total, one press.5 min
  • EarTie vs. slur listening test: teacher plays two identical note pairs — one tied (one long note), one slurred (two connected notes). Student identifies. The tie produces a single sound; the slur produces two. Ear must distinguish before notation explains.5 min
  • RepertoireNew piece featuring ties across barlines. Student marks every tie in pencil before playing — circle the second note of each tie as a reminder: "this note is silent — I already pressed it."5 min
Session 6 — 3/4 time: the waltz feel
First 15 min Three-in-a-bar as a physical sensation
  • EarPlay a recording of a waltz (Strauss or Chopin — anything in 3). Student sways or steps to feel ONE-two-three, ONE-two-three. The ONE is heavy; 2 and 3 are light. Body owns the feel before notation is shown.4 min
  • KeysSimple 3/4 exercise: LH plays one note on beat 1, RH plays two notes on beats 2 and 3. The ONE must have more weight — not by force, but by intention. "Beat 1 is where you land; beats 2 and 3 are where you float."6 min
  • RepertoireFirst 3/4 piece: a simple waltz. Mark the bar lines in pencil. Count aloud (one-two-three) for first four bars before playing. Let the body sway slightly — metric feel must be physical, not just numerical.5 min
Next 20 min Waltz with LH accompaniment pattern
  • CoordClassic waltz pattern: LH plays a bass note on beat 1, then a chord (or two notes) on beats 2 and 3. This is the first time the LH has a repeating accompaniment pattern — not a melody. Inner-singing: play LH, sing RH melody.10 min
  • SoundBalance: the RH melody must be louder than the LH. But the LH beat 1 (bass note) must be slightly fuller than beats 2–3. Three levels of dynamic in the LH alone. Listen for this balance — it is the characteristic waltz sound.6 min
  • EarSing only the melody while playing LH accompaniment. If the student can sing the melody freely while the LH pattern runs underneath — they own the piece. That is the standard.4 min
Watch for — Week 3
  • All three beats in 3/4 played with equal weight — the waltz lilt requires a heavier beat 1; ask student to "land" on 1 and "float" on 2 and 3
  • Re-attacking tied notes — this is universal; marking the tied note in pencil is the most reliable correction; the eye must be reminded before the hand can be retrained
  • LH accompaniment drowning the RH melody — this happens when the LH is closer to the student physically and gets more arm weight by default; consciously reduce LH weight by 30%
Home practice — Week 3 (20 min/day)
5 min — Waltz LH pattern alone (bass note + two chord notes) in 3/4 time. Count aloud. Foot taps beat 1 only.
10 min — Waltz piece HT: inner-singing preparation first, then full piece. Sing the melody while playing — if melody singing stops, the hands stop.
5 min — Rest and tie exercise: write a 4-bar rhythm from dictation (teacher provides at lesson), clap it at home, then play on single note with tied notes marked.
04
The LH as an equal voice — bass lines and simple chords
The left hand stops being an accompanist and starts being a musician. Bass lines with character. Two-note chords introduced.
Phase A
Teacher's framing for this week

Most beginner methods treat the left hand as subordinate — it holds chords, taps a bass note, stays out of the way. This method does not. From week 4 onwards, the student learns to give the LH its own musical identity: a bass line that has direction, weight, and character. This pays off enormously in Stage 3 when they encounter music that genuinely requires two independent musical voices.

Session 7 — LH melodic bass lines
First 20 min Bass line with direction and weight
  • EarTeacher plays the same piece with two different LH approaches: one where the LH is flat and mechanical, one where the bass line has shape (crescendo toward a peak, slight weight on strong beats). Student describes what they hear. This is the standard.4 min
  • RepertoirePiece where LH has a moving bass line (not repeated notes). Student prepares LH alone first — add a dynamic shape to the bass line independently. "If someone only heard your LH, would it be musical on its own?"10 min
  • CoordHT combination: RH holds a long note while LH bass line moves beneath. The long RH note should ring effortlessly — the student's attention is fully on the LH. This is the reverse of the usual attention pattern.6 min
Next 15 min Two-note chords — LH and RH
  • BodyTwo-note chord technique: both notes must drop simultaneously with arm weight — no rolling, no one finger arriving before the other. Drop both fingers from a small height above the key. Listen: both notes sound at exactly the same instant.5 min
  • TheoryName two-note intervals in chords: C+E (a third), C+G (a fifth). Students plays each on LH and identifies by ear: thirds sound close and warm; fifths sound open and hollow. No theory labels yet — just aural character.5 min
  • RepertoirePiece with two-note LH chords on beats 1 and 3. The chord must be even — not louder on the bottom note or the top. Check with teacher: "Cover my ears and play — I should not be able to guess which note is on top."5 min
Session 8 — Combining bass notes and chords in one hand
Full session Alberti bass and oom-pah patterns
  • TheoryIntroduce the oom-pah: LH plays a single bass note (oom) on beat 1, then a two-note chord (pah) on beat 2. This is the most common LH accompaniment pattern in beginner-intermediate repertoire. It requires a position shift within the LH between beats 1 and 2.8 min
  • BodyThe LH shift: 5th finger on bass note, then thumb and 3rd finger on chord. The shift must be seamless — arm weight transfers without audible gap. Practice LH alone until the pattern is automatic, then add RH.8 min
  • CoordHT with oom-pah: this is the first genuinely complex coordination moment — the LH has two different physical tasks per bar. Inner singing: play LH oom-pah, sing RH melody. Confirm melody is singing freely before attempting HT.7 min
  • SoundBalance check: in the finished HT version, the RH melody should be the dominant voice. The oom (bass note) should be audible but subordinate. The pah (chord) should be the softest element. Three levels in one bar — all from arm weight variation, never from tension.7 min
Watch for — Week 4
  • Uneven two-note chords — students consistently play the bottom note louder; ask them to deliberately play the top note louder for one bar, then find the even middle point
  • The oom-pah pattern making the LH rigid — the shift between bass note and chord must stay relaxed; stiffness in the LH wrist is the main cause of unevenness
  • LH oom-pah overpowering the RH melody — this is the defining imbalance of intermediate playing; address it directly and early
Home practice — Week 4 (20–25 min/day)
5 min — LH oom-pah pattern alone: one chord pattern, four bars, until it's automatic. No looking at the hand.
10 min — HT piece with oom-pah: inner-singing preparation mandatory. Then HT with attention on RH melody dominance.
5 min — C major scale, both hands in contrary motion (RH goes up, LH goes down from middle C simultaneously). This is preparation for weeks 5–6.
05
Eighth notes, dotted rhythms, and sight-reading habits
Rhythm doubles in complexity. Sight-reading is introduced as a daily discipline — 10 minutes every session, always slightly below current repertoire level.
Phase A
The sight-reading rule — introduce it here, keep it forever

Sight-reading is the skill most intermediate pianists wish they had developed earlier. Begin it now, in Stage 2, as a fixed part of every lesson: 10 minutes at the start, on material one full level below the student's current repertoire. The rule is iron-clad: play through once, no stopping, no correction. Speed is irrelevant. The point is to keep the eyes moving and the music continuous.

The reason sight-reading material must be below current level: if the student is struggling to decode notes, they cannot also maintain pulse, phrasing, and continuity. Easy material is the only way to practice the skill of reading (not the skill of playing difficult music).

Session 9 — Eighth notes and the subdivided pulse
First 10 min First sight-reading session
  • KeysTeacher selects a simple, fresh piece (Stage 1 level — five-finger, C position). Student scans it silently for 30 seconds before playing: "What's the time signature? Where are the rests? Is there a position change?" Then: one continuous play-through. No stopping.5 min
  • EarAfter the play-through: "What was the hardest moment? Not to fix — just to notice." Self-analysis is part of the sight-reading discipline. Teacher confirms or adds one observation. Move on — never drill the sight-reading piece.5 min
Next 25 min Eighth notes — two per beat
  • EarClap eighth notes: "ta-ta ta-ta ta-ta ta-ta" (eight per bar in 4/4). Teacher and student clap together. Then student taps foot on the quarter beat while clapping eighths — two levels of pulse simultaneously in the body.5 min
  • KeysPlay pairs of eighth notes on single pitches: C–C D–D E–E F–F. Each pair is one beat. The two notes of a pair must be exactly equal — not one short, one long. Listen: the pair sounds like a single beat with two impulses.8 min
  • RepertoireNew piece featuring eighth notes in the RH against quarter notes in the LH. This is the first true polyrhythmic challenge — 2:1 in each hand simultaneously. Inner-singing: play LH quarters while saying "ta-ta" for each RH eighth pair.7 min
  • SoundArticulation of eighth notes: pairs should be slightly lighter than quarters — they move faster and need less arm weight per note. Compare the feel to the quarter-note approach. "Eighths are more like brushing, quarters more like pressing."5 min
Session 10 — Dotted quarter + eighth rhythm
First 10 min Sight-reading (daily discipline)
  • KeysNew sight-reading piece — same level as last session, different material. Scan, then play through. One chance. Teacher records any notes about fluency, note accuracy, pulse maintenance. This log is the sight-reading progress record.10 min
Next 25 min Dotted rhythm — long-short feel
  • EarClap the dotted quarter + eighth: "ta-a-ta ta-a-ta." The long note is 3/4 of a beat, the short note 1/4. Listen to familiar songs: "Greensleeves," "I've Been Working on the Railroad." Student identifies the dotted feel by ear before reading it.6 min
  • KeysPlay dotted quarter + eighth on single pitches. The short note arrives exactly on the half-beat subdivision — not earlier, not later. Teacher counts "1-and-2-and" and marks where the short note falls: "and." Precision here prevents a lifetime of sloppy dotted rhythms.8 min
  • RepertoireA well-known march or song in dotted rhythm. Sing it first — the body already knows the rhythm even if the notation is new. Playing follows the singing.7 min
  • EarCommon error demonstration: teacher plays the dotted rhythm "swung" (too uneven, jazz feel) vs. precise (classical notation). Student identifies which matches the written score. The ear is trained on the difference.4 min
Watch for — Week 5
  • Sight-reading used as a performance opportunity — student slows down at hard passages, corrects errors, repeats bars; stop immediately and reset: one play-through, continuous, no going back
  • Dotted rhythms played as equal eighths (swung) — the short note must arrive precisely on the half-beat; counting "1-and" with the teacher is the reliable fix
  • Eighth note pairs rushing — the student tends to speed up where notes are denser; a physical cue (slight lean back on eighth-note bars) can help
Home practice — Week 5 (25 min/day)
10 min — Sight-reading at home: student reads one new, simple piece daily (not the lesson piece). One play-through only. Circle any note they couldn't read in time and check it after.
10 min — Dotted rhythm repertoire piece: full preparation process, then HT at a tempo that keeps the dotted rhythm precise.
5 min — Eighth-note exercise: C–D–E–F–G in eighth notes, RH alone, then LH alone, then HT in unison. Listen for even pairs throughout.
06
The C major scale HT and scales as musical vocabulary
Both hands play the C major scale together. The scale is treated as a musical entity — not as an exercise to endure but as a tool to own.
Phase A
Teacher's framing for this week

Scales are taught badly in most piano methods — as mechanical drills divorced from music. This method treats scales as musical vocabulary: patterns that appear in actual pieces and that the hand must own in order to play fluently. The C major scale HT is a genuine coordination challenge because the thumb crossings in each hand happen at different moments. This asymmetry is the technical core of this week.

Session 11 — C major scale HT: the asymmetric crossing
First 25 min Where the thumbs cross — and why it's different
  • BodyMap the crossings: RH thumb crosses under after E (onto F). LH thumb crosses under after G (onto A) ascending. These happen at different points in the scale — the hands are not mirror images. Student marks the crossing notes on the score.6 min
  • CoordSlow HT at ♩=40: one note per beat, teacher counts. At the crossing point, the arriving thumb of each hand moves independently of the other. Do not let one hand dictate the other's rhythm. Stop and identify the exact beat where each crossing occurs.10 min
  • SoundEvenness test: teacher closes eyes, student plays. Teacher must not be able to identify any crossing point by sound. Accents at the crossing = not ready for tempo increase. Silence = ready to move tempo up by ♩=5.9 min
Next 10 min Scale as melody — with shape
  • EarOnce technically passable: play the C major scale with a full musical arc — crescendo ascending to the top C, then a real diminuendo descending. Sing the scale while playing. "A scale is eight notes of music, not eight notes of exercise."5 min
  • RepertoireFind the scale pattern in a real piece: teacher highlights a scale run in a method book piece. Student identifies: "This is the same pattern we just practiced." The muscle memory transfers because the context is recognised.5 min
Session 12 — Sight-reading + scale in G major introduced
First 10 min Sight-reading (progressive)
  • KeysSight-reading piece now includes eighth notes — the level has advanced by one notch from weeks 5. Student scans (30 sec), identifies rhythm patterns before playing. One play-through continuous.10 min
Next 25 min G major scale and the F-sharp
  • TheoryWhy G major has one sharp: teacher plays the G major scale using all white keys. "Does that sound right?" Student hears the wrong note (F natural feels like a step too low). Then play F-sharp: "Now it sounds like a proper scale." The ear discovers the sharps — the rule follows the sound.6 min
  • KeysG major scale RH: G–A–B–C–D–E–F#–G. The F# is a black key — the thumb crosses under after B (not after E as in C major). New crossing point, new muscle memory. Practice the B–F# segment specifically.10 min
  • RepertoireShort piece in G major (one sharp in the key signature). Student identifies the key from the key signature before playing. "Every time you see one sharp, the home note is G and F is always sharp."9 min
Watch for — Week 6
  • C major scale HT rushed before evenness is achieved — the test is always the teacher's closed-eyes listening; crossing accents mean the tempo target has not been reached yet
  • F-sharp forgotten mid-piece — student plays F natural by habit; remedy: circle every F in the piece before playing and say "sharp" as each one is approached
  • Sight-reading quality plateauing — if the student is not improving week-on-week, the material is too hard; drop the level further
Home practice — Week 6 (25 min/day)
5 min — C major scale HT daily, ♩=50, listening for any crossing accent. Raise tempo by 5 only when the teacher cannot hear the crossing.
5 min — G major scale RH, paying attention to the new crossing point (after B). Sing the scale while playing.
10 min — G major piece: inner-singing process, then HT. Mark every F# before starting.
5 min — Sight-reading: one new simple piece, one play-through only.
07
Phase A consolidation — milestone check
No new material. Every skill from weeks 1–6 is deepened, tested, and performed. The milestone check determines readiness for Phase B.
Phase A
Why a full consolidation week matters

Seven weeks of new material have accumulated. Without a consolidation week, the skills exist in isolation — practiced separately, never integrated. Week 7 forces integration: the student must draw on inner singing, position shifts, scale knowledge, rhythmic vocabulary, and sight-reading simultaneously in a single performance. This reveals which pillars are genuinely owned and which are still fragile under pressure.

Session 13 — Integration performance
Full session Three-piece programme + milestone check
  • RepertoireStudent performs three pieces from phases A (weeks 1–6) in sequence: one 4/4 piece, one 3/4 piece, one piece in G major. No stopping. Teacher takes written notes — not corrections during performance.15 min
  • KeysScale check: C major and G major, HT, both directions. Teacher listens with eyes closed for any crossing accent. Student receives pass/continue feedback on each scale.8 min
  • CoordInner-singing drill on demand: teacher opens to any piece the student has learned and asks them to play the LH while singing the RH. No preparation — immediate response. This tests whether the skill is genuinely owned or only practiced in sequence.7 min
Phase A Milestone Check
Not a formal gateway — a diagnostic snapshot. Areas that fail continue as targeted work in Phase B alongside new material.
M1
HT coordination
Student plays a full piece HT with different rhythmic values in each hand. The hands sound genuinely independent — not synchronised. Inner-singing can be demonstrated on demand.
M2
Scales
C major and G major, HT, both directions at ♩=60 minimum. No audible accent at thumb crossings. Played with a dynamic arc — not flat.
M3
Rhythmic reading
Student correctly identifies and performs quarter, half, dotted half, whole, and eighth note patterns on sight. Ties and rests are handled correctly.
M4
Sight-reading
Student sight-reads a fresh piece at Stage 1 level in a single continuous play-through with no stops. Pulse maintained throughout.
Session 14 — Targeted repair + Phase B preview
Full session Fix one thing, preview the next
  • CoordBased on milestone check: address the single most significant gap with targeted exercises. Not a broad review — one specific skill, worked deeply for 15 minutes.15 min
  • TheoryPhase B preview: introduce the concept of three-note chords (triads). Teacher plays C–E–G together. "You've played C and E, you've played C and G — now all three." Let the student hear how the triad sounds before naming it. Curiosity first, theory second.10 min
  • RepertoireAssign Phase B opening piece (triads in LH, melody in RH) for first reading at home. No instruction yet — just exploration. "What do you notice? What's hard? What feels natural?" Report back next session.5 min
Home practice — Week 7 (25 min/day)
10 min — Three-piece programme: perform all three pieces in sequence with full intention. No stopping. Record yourself if possible — the recording is honest in a way the internal ear is not.
10 min — Targeted repair from milestone check: whatever the weakest area, focus practice entirely here.
5 min — Explore Phase B opening piece freely. No process, no method — just listening to what the hands find naturally.
08
Triads — the harmonic vocabulary begins
Three-note chords in both hands. The major triad as a physical and aural unit. Root, first and second inversion introduced by ear before theory.
Phase B
Teacher's framing for Phase B

Phase B is where reading becomes genuinely fluent and harmony begins to make sense to the student. The three goals of Phase B: (1) triads become physically and aurally automatic; (2) the student reads and plays at sight from the grand staff without decoding individual notes; (3) key signatures up to two sharps and two flats are internalised. By the end of week 14, the student should be able to open a new simple piece and read through it — imperfectly but continuously — at first sight.

Session 15 — The major triad: sound before theory
First 20 min C, G and F major triads by ear
  • EarTeacher plays three chords: C major (C–E–G), a minor-sounding chord, a dissonant cluster. Student arranges from "most settled" to "least settled." The major triad is the most stable sound in Western harmony — the ear knows this before the theory says it.4 min
  • TheoryMajor triad structure: root, third above, fifth above. Not as an interval formula — as physical finger placement. RH: thumb–3rd finger–5th finger on C–E–G. The span should feel comfortable, not stretched. Play C, G, and F major triads in this position.8 min
  • SoundTriad evenness: all three notes must arrive simultaneously. Drop test: raise the hand 2cm above the keys and let it fall as a unit. No leading finger, no lagging finger. "The chord is one sound, not three sounds close together."8 min
Next 15 min Triads in LH + melody in RH
  • CoordFirst piece using LH triads: C major triad on beat 1 of every bar, RH plays a simple melody. Inner-singing: play LH triads, sing RH melody. The LH triad is a single sustained event — the RH melody is the changing element.10 min
  • EarHarmonic listening: as the RH melody moves over the C major LH triad, which melody notes sound "inside" the chord (consonant) and which sound "outside" (adding tension)? Student points at the score. No labels — just aural identification of consonance and dissonance.5 min
Watch for — Week 8
  • Uneven triad — one finger (usually the 5th) arriving late; the drop technique is the fix, not individual finger targeting
  • LH triad too loud against the RH melody — the chord is harmonic support; it should be present but not dominant; aim for 60% of the RH volume
  • Student not yet fluent in Phase A skills — if inner-singing is still unreliable, slow the Phase B pace and continue Phase A repairs alongside new material

Triad inversions and chord progressions by ear
Root, first and second inversion. The I–V progression heard and played. Harmonic rhythm introduced.
Phase B
Teacher's framing for this week

Inversions are taught by ear first: teacher plays C major in root, first inversion, second inversion. Student arranges from "most settled" to "most unstable." Root is most stable; second inversion is least. The student already knows this — the ear knows. Theory labels follow the aural recognition, never precede it. The I–V chord change is the most fundamental harmonic motion in Western music and should be introduced here as an emotional experience (tension and resolution) before any analysis.

Session 17 — Inversions as different weights of the same chord
First 20 min Inversion voice-leading — smooth movement
  • EarCompare: moving from C major root to G major root (big jump in LH) vs. C major root to G major first inversion (smooth voice-leading, small movement). Which sounds more connected? "Inversions exist to make chord changes graceful."6 min
  • TheoryRoot position: C–E–G. First inversion: E–G–C (C moves up an octave). Second inversion: G–C–E. Play all three, listen to how the sound shifts from settled to floating. Identify each by its "feel" — not by calculation.8 min
  • RepertoirePiece using smooth inversion changes in LH: C major root → G major first inversion → C major. The LH barely moves — yet the harmony changes. Student listens for the harmonic shift while the fingers do minimal work.6 min

Teacher's framing for this week

Key signatures should feel like orientation, not memorisation. The ear already knows what a major scale sounds like. F and B-flat major — the flat keys begin
Minor seconds become flats. The black key topology changes. The ear distinguishes flat-key colour from sharp-key colour.
Phase B
Teacher's framing for this week

Flat keys have a different harmonic colour from sharp keys — slightly warmer, slightly rounder. This is not imagination; it is a consequence of the overtone series and the equal temperament tuning of the modern piano. The student's ear will notice it when asked to compare. Use this as an introduction to the concept that key is not just a technical label — it carries character. F major is warm and pastoral; B-flat major is rich and full. These associations are not rules but accumulated musical experience.

Sessions 21–22 F major (1 flat) and B-flat major (2 flats)

  • KeysF major scale: RH thumb crosses under after A (to Bb). Bb is a black key — the thumb slides under to a black key, which feels different from crossing to a white key. Practice the A–Bb segment slowly with attention to the different thumb position.8 min

Minor keys — the parallel minor and its emotional landscape
A minor introduced as C major's relative. The minor scale as a different emotional language. Ear training: major vs. minor.
Phase B
Teacher's framing for this week

Minor is not sad — it is complex. This is an important distinction for the student's musical development. Minor keys carry gravity, depth, mystery, nobility, longing — a far richer emotional range than the popular cliché of "minor = sad." The teacher should play examples of minor-key music that express different emotional states: a minor march (powerful, not sad), a minor lullaby (tender, not gloomy), a minor dance (energetic, even joyful). The ear should discover the range before any playing begins.

Sessions 23–24 A natural minor and the relative major connection
  • EarTeacher plays C major scale, then A natural minor scale (A–B–C–D–E–F–G–A — same notes, different starting point). "Same notes — but does it feel the same?" Watch for — Week 12

Sight-reading at Stage 2 level — the fluency threshold
Sight-reading material advances to match current repertoire complexity. Scanning technique deepened. Predictive reading introduced.
Phase B
The fluency threshold

By week 13, sight-reading should be advancing from "decoding notes" to "reading patterns." The difference is significant: a decoder looks at individual notes and translates them one at a time. A pattern reader sees a group of notes, recognises it (a scale fragment, a broken chord, a familiar rhythmic figure) and plays the group as a unit. Pattern reading is 3–4× faster than decoding and is the skill that separates functional readers from exceptional sight-readers. This week begins the deliberate shift from decoding to pattern recognition.

Sessions 25–26 Pattern reading and eyes-ahead technique
  • KeysEyes-ahead drill: student covers the measure they're playing with a piece of paper, forcing the eye to look one bar ahead at all times. Start with a very simple piece. This feels impossible at first — that is the point. The discomfort is the training.8 min
  • KeysPattern identification drill: teacher writes 6 four-note groups on paper (scale fragment up, broken chord, step-skip-step, repeated notes, etc.). Student names the pattern and plays it on demand. "Don't read the notes — read the shape."8 min
  • RepertoireSight-read a piece specifically chosen to contain familiar patterns (scale fragments, triad outlines, oom-pah figures). Before playing: "Find all the scale passages. Find all the triad outlines. Now you know the hard bits — the rest is easy." Label them in pencil. Then play.14 min
Ear training Predictive hearing — what comes next?
  • EarTeacher plays a 3-bar phrase and stops. Student sings what they think the fourth bar will be. Any answer that is musically plausible passes — there is no single right answer. The goal is that the student has a musical expectation. Harmonic instinct is being built.8 min
  • EarHarmonic prediction: teacher plays I chord, then asks: "Is the next chord going away from home or coming back?" Student answers before teacher plays. Then teacher plays and student confirms. The ear is learning the grammar of tonal music.7 min
Watch for — Week 13
  • Eyes-ahead drill causing panic — normalise it: "Everyone finds this impossible at first. The discomfort is the work." Start with 2-bar pieces if needed and expand gradually
  • Pattern reading limited to scale passages — students often recognise scales but not broken chords or rhythmic patterns; include all pattern types in the identification drill

The first multi-page piece — learning strategy
A longer, more complex piece than anything attempted before. Structural learning replaces bar-by-bar memorisation. The student learns how to learn a hard piece.
Phase C
Teacher's framing for Phase C

The central skill of Phase C is structural learning — approaching a multi-page piece not as a sequence of bars but as an architecture. Every piece has a form: verse and chorus, A and B sections, a development and a return. Understanding the form before learning the notes reduces the learning time by half and produces far more musically intelligent performances. The student who learns by structure never loses their place; the student who learns bar-by-bar always does.

Sessions 29–30 Form analysis before note learning
  • RepertoireAssign a multi-page piece (a simple sonatina movement or a longer song arrangement). First task — before playing a single note: map the form. "Where does section A end? Where does section B begin? Is there a return to A? Mark these with brackets in pencil."8 min
  • TheoryHarmonic skeleton: identify the home key, the main departure points (where does it move away from home?), and the return. "You now know the story before the details. The notes fill in the story you already understand."7 min
  • RepertoireLearn section A only this week — not the whole piece. Mastery of one section before adding the next. "A building is not built by laying all the bricks at once and hoping they stay."15 min
Technique The problem-bar method
  • CoordProblem-bar method: when a specific bar or passage fails consistently, isolate it. Play it 10 times at a very slow tempo (where it can be played correctly). Then play the bar before and the problem bar. Then two bars before. Gradually expand outward. Never start a practice session from the beginning of the piece to reach the problem bar — that wastes most of the session.15 min
  • EarThe "zoom-out" test: after isolating and fixing a difficult passage, play the full section in tempo. The fixed passage must now sound as natural as the easy passages. If it still sounds different, the isolation tempo was not slow enough.5 min
Watch for — Week 15
  • Student trying to learn the whole multi-page piece in one week — enforce the section-by-section rule; partial mastery of the full piece is not the goal; complete mastery of each section is
  • Problem-bar work done at the wrong tempo — if the student makes any error during the 10 isolation repetitions, the tempo is still too fast; reduce further

Phase C
Resilience — the skill no method book teaches

A musician who stops when they make an error has not learned to perform — they have only learned to practice. Performance requires a fundamentally different mental state: the error is noted and left behind; the music continues. This must be explicitly taught. The student should understand: in a performance, stopping is the only unrecoverable error. A wrong note is a bump in the road; stopping is a wall.

Session 40 — The Stage 2 Gateway
Stage 2 Gateway Assessment
Four components. The central criterion: can the student play HT and read at sight? Partial passes move specific pillars forward into Stage 3 while continuing targeted work on weak areas.
01
HT coordination — inner singing on demand
Teacher names any piece from Stage 2. Student plays one hand while singing the other, without preparation. Then plays both hands together. Pass criterion: the inner singing is genuinely audible and continuous; the HT playing reflects independent voices (melody audibly louder than accompaniment; rhythmic values in each hand are distinctly different where the score requires it).
02
Sight-reading — reading music, not decoding notation
Teacher presents a fresh piece at Stage 2 level (two-page, simple HT, key signature of up to two sharps or flats). Student scans for 60 seconds, then plays one continuous read-through. Pass criterion: pulse is maintained throughout, eyes move ahead of the playing hand, patterns are recognised (not decoded note-by-note), no stopping or restarting. Imperfect accuracy is acceptable — musical continuity is not negotiable.
03
Scales and key signatures
Four scales at random from the six major keys and A minor learned in Stage 2. HT, both directions. Pass criterion: no audible accent at any thumb crossing; each scale played with a dynamic arc (not flat); key signatures correctly recalled before playing. 3 of 4 scales passing to this standard is acceptable.
04
Programme performance — musical and structural ownership
Student performs both Stage 2 pieces in programme sequence with a deliberate pause between them. Teacher listens for: balance (RH melody dominant), musical intention (each piece has a distinct character), harmonic awareness (chord changes are shaped, not just noted), and structural security (student can start from any section without resetting to the beginning). The decisive question: does this student make musical decisions, or do they only play notes? Pass criterion: at least one clear musical decision is evident in each piece.

After the gateway — what changes in Stage 3

Stage 3 is where the student stops being a learner and starts being a musician. The coordination and reading tools are now in place. Stage 3 uses them in service of real musical goals: scales as vocabulary rather than exercises, harmonic understanding that enables interpretation, and repertoire that has genuine artistic weight. The student who passes the Stage 2 gateway has earned the right to be treated as a musician. That shift in relationship — from teacher-guiding to artist-consulting — is the first thing that changes.
