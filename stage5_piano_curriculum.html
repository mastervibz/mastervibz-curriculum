01
The four eras — four different languages
Baroque, Classical, Romantic, and 20th-century music require genuinely different physical approaches, harmonic vocabularies, and interpretive frameworks. Stage 5 begins by establishing these differences as the foundational map.
Phase A
Central premise of Phase A

A pianist who plays Bach like Chopin and Chopin like Debussy is not playing any of them well. Each era has a distinct performance practice — conventions about articulation, tempo, ornamentation, dynamics, and pedalling that the music was written to exploit. These conventions are not restrictions; they are the grammar of each era's musical language. Phase A maps all four eras as practical performance guides. By week 6, the student identifies any piece's era by ear and adjusts their entire physical and musical approach accordingly before playing a note.

Era 1
Baroque
c.1600–1750 · Bach, Handel, Scarlatti, Couperin
Non-legato default. Terraced dynamics (no gradual swells). Ornaments are structural, not decorative. No sustain pedal. Tempo is dance-derived and steady. The harpsichord is the model instrument.

02
Classical era — clarity, wit, and the conversational phrase
Mozart as the hardest composer to play well. Slur-gap articulation. Alberti bass at 40% volume. Haydn’s wit as an interpretive challenge. Early Beethoven: Classical form, Romantic content.
Phase A
Why Classical is harder than Romantic for advanced players

Most technically advanced students find Romantic music easier to perform convincingly. Romantic music rewards generous physical gestures and rich emotional expression. Classical music punishes them — every note is exposed. A blurred ornament in Schubert is forgivable; the same ornament in Mozart sounds crude. Classical style demands precision, transparency, and interpretive humility. The music speaks for itself when played cleanly. The performer’s job is not to add expression but to reveal the expression already written.

Sessions 3–4Mozart, Haydn, and Classical discipline
  • ArtSlur-gap articulation: slurs are short (2–4 notes), staccato dots crisp, and adjacent slurs are separated by a tiny gap. This slur-gap-slur pattern is the most characteristic Classical articulation. Play a Mozart theme this way vs. fully legato. “The slur-gap version speaks. The legato version sings. Mozart speaks.”10 min
  • TheoryAlberti bass rule: the LH Alberti bass should be 40% of the RH melody dynamic — almost inaudible beneath the melody. Practice any Mozart Sonata with LH at pp throughout regardless of RH dynamic. The result is usually the correct balance.8 min
  • EarHaydn’s wit: Haydn violates expectations constantly — a phrase ending on vi instead of I (deceptive cadence), a sudden subito piano after fortissimo. Identify every structural surprise in a Haydn movement before performing. Then lean into each surprise with the performance gesture of the unexpected. “Haydn is a comedian — play the jokes.”8 min
  • RepertoireMozart K.331 Theme and Variations or K.545 Sonata: full Classical performance practice — slur-gap articulation, 40% Alberti bass, minimal pedal, restrained dynamics. Compare to a recording by Mitsuko Uchida or Robert Levin. “What do they do that you did not? What do they omit that you added?”14 min
  • ArtBeethoven Op. 2 Sonatas are Classical in form but Romantic in emotional intensity. Play them “as if Mozart — until the moment it stops being Mozart. Then let it be Beethoven.” Finding that exact transition point in the score is the interpretive work of the week.15 min

03
Romantic era — singing tone, harmonic colour, personal narrative
Schubert’s flat-VI magic. Chopin’s LH-steady rubato. Schumann’s literary references. Brahms’s three-level voice. Four composers, four distinct physical and emotional approaches.
Phase A
Sessions 5–6Four Romantic voices and gateway piece analysis
  • ArtSchubert’s flat-VI: the sudden move from major to the key a minor third above (C→Eb, E→G) creates unexpected warmth. At every flat-VI arrival: slow fractionally, give the chord slightly more weight and pedal, let the new key settle before moving on. The magical effect depends on not rushing past it.8 min
  • ArtChopin’s rubato: the LH is the conductor and maintains tempo; the RH is the singer and may deviate freely. Practice explicitly: LH strict time, RH melody with any rhythmic freedom the phrase demands. The LH never adjusts. This creates the characteristic floating melody of a Chopin Nocturne.8 min
  • ArtSchumann’s literary references: read the title or subtitle before every practice session — it sets the physical and emotional state from bar one. “The Happy Farmer” has a completely different body from “Träumerei.” Different people, different bodies, different times of day.6 min
  • ArtBrahms’s three-level voice: in the Intermezzo Op. 118 No. 2, the top voice sings the primary melody, the inner voice carries a counter-melody of near-equal importance, and the bass provides harmonic foundation. All three must be heard in proportion — three simultaneous dynamic levels in one hand.6 min
  • RepertoirePlay one excerpt from each of the four Romantic composers. For each: announce the composer and adjust the entire physical and emotional approach accordingly. Teacher evaluates: does each excerpt sound as if written by a different person?12 min
  • ArtLiszt’s Consolations or Liebesträume: intimate, harmonically adventurous, deeply personal. “Liszt is not a circus act. He is a poet who commands the full piano. The technical display exists to recreate an orchestra, not to impress.”10 min
Gateway pieceRomantic piece structural analysis + section A
  • RepertoireComplete structural analysis of the Romantic gateway piece: form (ABA, binary, through-composed), key areas, every modulation point, all secondary dominants and chromatic chords, phrase shapes, dynamic map. Mark every flat-VI, deceptive cadence, and Neapolitan in the score. These harmonic events are the performance events.15 min
  • RepertoireLearn Section A using structural method: HS each, inner singing, HT at 50% target tempo. Five consecutive correct repetitions rule applies to every difficult passage. Era-reset ritual performed before starting — 30 seconds to establish the Romantic physical state.15 min

04
20th century — five composers, five sonic worlds
Debussy’s overtone pedalling. Ravel’s jewel-box precision. Bartók’s percussive piano. Prokofiev’s sardonic wit. Each composer is a separate study — there is no unified “modern” performance practice.
Phase A
The 20th century has no single performance practice

Every major 20th-century composer requires individual research into their own writings, their own recordings where available, and the critical literature about their work. What unites 20th-century piano music is the expanded use of the instrument’s full sonic range — the overtone resonances (Debussy), the percussive qualities (Bartók, Prokofiev), the mechanical precision (Ravel), the extremes of register (Messiaen). In each case, the physical approach must be derived from the specific sonic goal of each composer, not from a generic “modern” style.

Sessions 7–8Four 20th-century voices and 20th-century gateway piece
  • SoundDebussy — overtone pedalling: press the pedal before the first note (pre-pedalling), change slowly with half-pedal releases, let adjacent harmonies blur intentionally. The pedal in Debussy is for harmonic colour, not harmonic clarity — the blur IS the sound.8 min
  • SoundRavel — mechanical precision with warmth: absolute rhythmic precision, very clean finger work, minimal pedal, every inner voice audible. “Ravel is a Swiss watchmaker who happens to be French. Precision first; warmth second, but always present.”6 min
  • SoundBartók — the percussion instrument: dry, non-pedalled, sharp arm-weight attacks. No portato, no singing legato. The sound should be clean, hard-edged, and rhythmically absolute. The Mikrokosmos studies are the curriculum.6 min
  • SoundProkofiev — sardonic wit and lyrical warmth: dry/mechanical/percussive in the ironic passages; warm and singing in the lyrical passages. The contrast is the character. “Prokofiev is funny — but his slow movements will break your heart. Play both extremes fully.”6 min
  • RepertoirePlay one excerpt from each composer. For each: state the sonic intention before playing. Teacher evaluates whether the stated intention matches the heard result.14 min
  • Repertoire20th-century gateway piece structural analysis: form, key areas (if tonal), modal or pitch-class organisation (if post-tonal), the composer’s own performance markings (pedal, articulation, dynamics) treated as precise instructions. Begin Section A learning.10 min

This analysis typically takes 15–20 minutes at Stage 5 speed.20 min
  • RepertoireSection A of Baroque piece: play with full Baroque physical approach (non-legato default, terraced dynamics, no pedal, ornaments from upper note). Era-reset ritual before starting. Aim for the piece to sound identifiably Baroque from bar one.15 min

05
All four gateway pieces in parallel — programme design and note writing
The four gateway pieces are chosen, analysed, and Section A begun for each. The programme note is written. The parallel learning structure and era-reset ritual become habits.
Phase A
Gateway programme architecture

Unlike earlier stages where the gateway was two contrasting pieces, the Stage 5 gateway is a four-work recital programme — one from each era. The programme is chosen for stylistic breadth and personal meaning, not technical safety. Each piece must be one the student genuinely wants to perform. The programme’s arc (how the four pieces relate in key, character, and emotional journey) is itself an artistic decision the student makes and defends in the gateway.

Sessions 9–10Programme note, parallel learning, and Classical gateway piece

Phase B
The one rule of improvisation training

There are no wrong notes in the practice context — only notes that have not yet been followed by the right continuation. The student's job is not to avoid mistakes but to follow whatever happens and make it musical. This rule must be returned to whenever the student stops, apologises, or self-edits.

Session 64 — The Stage 5 Gateway
Stage 5 Gateway Assessment
Six components. The decisive question throughout: does this playing have a recognisable voice? Not perfect — not unprecedented — but consistent, personal, and unmistakably individual.
01
Era performance mastery
Teacher plays opening 8 bars of each gateway piece in the wrong era style. Student diagnoses and corrects verbally and physically within 10 seconds per piece. Then: teacher calls an era at random; student plays any 4-bar passage in that style without preparation. Pass: all four diagnoses accurate; era switch made within one bar, maintained for 8 bars; each era audibly distinct from the others.
02
Improvisation on demand
Teacher specifies: a mode, a character (two words), and a harmonic event (a specific chord type from Stage 4). Student improvises a 2–3 minute piece incorporating all three. Pass: modal character aurally identifiable; character description matches the mood; specified harmonic event present, correctly voiced, and given dramatic weight; the improvisation has a shape — it is a piece, not a fragment.
03
Arrangement and teaching demonstration
Part A — Arrangement: one arrangement performed, three creative decisions explained with musical reasoning. Pass: arrangement is a complete, self-sufficient piano piece. Part B — Teaching: teacher simulates a common error in a Stage 1–3 concept. Student diagnoses the error by name and corrects it through physical demonstration within 3 minutes. Pass: error correctly diagnosed; correction made physically; measurable improvement produced.
04
Original composition — performed and discussed
Student performs the 32–48 bar composition. After: teacher asks (1) "What did you want to say?" (2) "Did it say it?" (3) "What did you discover about your voice in writing it?" Pass: the composition is a complete musical statement with clear idea, development, climax, and resolution. All three questions answered honestly and specifically. The teacher can identify the three personal fingerprints aurally — they must be heard, not just claimed.
05
Complete gateway programme — era depth and personal voice
Voice statement read aloud. Then the complete programme performed (four era pieces + original composition). No stopping. Teacher evaluates: (1) each era piece audibly distinct in performance practice; (2) Stage 4 advanced techniques invisible — background operations; (3) harmonic events communicated — chromatic chords, modulations, phrase high points all given physical weight; (4) memory systems active — random starting point test administered mid-gateway; (5) composition placed with artistic logic. The decisive criterion: a listener who heard only the performance could describe the performer's musical personality in two or three consistent terms. If these terms align with the voice statement — the voice has been found.
06
The Stage 6 question
After the programme: "Stage 6 is Artistry and Legacy. What do you want to contribute? Not technically — but artistically and humanly. What music do you want to bring into the world that is not yet there? Whose musical life do you want to change — as a performer, as a teacher, as a composer? The answer to this question is the beginning of Stage 6."

After the gateway — what Stage 6 brings

Stage 6 is Artistry and Legacy. The student who passes the Stage 5 gateway has found their voice. Stage 6 asks what to do with it — over a lifetime. Performing, composing, teaching, and contributing to the musical community in ways that outlast the individual performing career.
