Technical Exercises

20 core exercises covering all four pillars: Body, Ear, Keys, and Sound. Sequenced from Week 1 through Week 8. Click any exercise to expand full instructions.

Body Posture, Tone & Arm Weight
B-01 The Arm-Weight Drop
BodySound
Week 1 ▾
1Lift the forearm to 45°. Let it fall freely — no muscle assistance — onto a single key (Middle C). Observe: the key goes down, the sound rings, the arm rests.
2Repeat 8 times on the same key. Listen for consistency. If any note sounds different, ask: "Was my arm actually free, or did I help it?"
3Move to D, E, F, G — one drop per key. Each must sound equally resonant. The tone changes with the key, not the technique.
4Three dynamic levels: arm drops from low height (pp), medium height (mp), high height (f). Same relaxed arm — only launch height changes.
The arm should feel "heavy" going down, completely relaxed after landing. If the student tenses up after the note, the exercise needs resetting.
B-02 Weight Transfer — Rolling Between Notes
BodySound
Week 2 ▾
1Place RH in five-finger position (C–G). Play C with arm weight. While C is ringing, slowly roll the weight across to D. C releases exactly when D sounds.
2Continue rolling: D→E→F→G. The hand should feel like water poured from one vessel to another — continuous, no gaps, no blur.
3Roll back down: G→F→E→D→C. The hand weight must travel "uphill" — slightly more active, but still relaxed.
4Sing each note as the weight arrives. Voice must reach the note a fraction before the finger does.
Watch the wrist: it should remain level or slightly elevated throughout. If it dips below the hand on each note, the student is "poking" rather than rolling.
B-03 Staccato Bounce
BodySound
Week 5 ▾
1Begin with the arm-weight drop (B-01) on Middle C. After the note sounds, allow the arm to "rebound" upward — like a ball bouncing, not a controlled lift.
2The rebound creates the staccato: the note is short not because you "cut" it, but because the arm naturally rises. The motion is initiated from the forearm, not the finger.
3Practice C–D–E–D–C with staccato. Listen: each note should sound like a crisp raindrop — clean attack, immediate release, no buzzing.
4Compare: play C legato, then C staccato. The tone colour changes; the relaxation in the arm does not.
"Drop and bounce, not poke." If the student jabs with the fingertip, the sound will be sharp and thin. The arm's natural weight always produces a rounder staccato tone.
B-04 Posture Reset — The Benchmark Check
Body
All weeks ▾
1Bench height: Student sits — thighs parallel to floor, elbows slightly above key level. Adjust before every session.
2Hand shape: "Hold a small orange." Fingers gently curved. Knuckle arch does not collapse when pressing. Thumb plays on its outer edge — never flat.
3Shoulder test: Ask student to shrug shoulders to ears, hold 2 seconds, release. Whatever position the shoulders fall to is where they should stay during playing.
4Wrist level: Not higher than the knuckles, not lower than the keys. Level is neutral. "Wrist is a bridge — not a peak, not a valley."
Use this check at the start of every session and whenever you hear tone quality deteriorate mid-lesson. Tension is almost always the cause of poor tone.
Ear Singing, Intervals & Pitch
E-01 Voice Leads Hand — Inner Singing Protocol
Ear
Week 1+ ▾
1Teacher plays C. Student sings it. Then sings D before finding it on the keyboard. The sequence is always: hear → sing → play.
2If the student cannot sing the next note before playing, they are not ready to play it. Slow down or return to listening only.
3Apply to every scale, every piece, every exercise: voice arrives at each note a fraction before the finger. No exceptions.
If the singing stops, the hands stop. The voice is the real musician — the hands are only servants.
E-02 Pitch Matching — Call and Echo
Ear
Weeks 1–4 ▾
1Teacher plays a single note (C–G range only). Student sings it back — without first looking at or touching the keyboard.
2Once sung accurately, student finds the note on the keyboard — then plays it, checking whether the pitch matches their voice.
3Run 5 notes per session. Progress criteria: 5/5 correct pitches, found without hesitation, within 5 seconds each.
4Harder version (Week 4+): Teacher plays the note, student sings it back, then sings the note one step above. Only then plays both on the keyboard.
Students who struggle to match pitch at all may need the teacher to sing unison with them until the habit forms. Never embarrass — frame it as "our voices finding the note together."
E-03 Step or Skip — Blind Interval Identification
EarKeys
Weeks 5–8 ▾
1Teacher sits beside student. Teacher plays two notes sequentially, shielding the keyboard. Student answers: "step" or "skip."
2Round 1: direction only — "up" or "down." Round 2: both — "skip up," "step down." Round 3 (Week 6+): add quantity — "small skip (3rd)" or "big skip (5th)."
3Run 10 pairs per session. Scoring: 8/10 is pass for gateway readiness.
4Melody version: Teacher plays a 4-note melody. Student transcribes as a sequence of step/skip directions: e.g. "step up, skip down, step up."
Start with widely contrasting intervals (2nd vs 5th) before narrowing. A student who confuses steps and small skips is not ready for gateway ear assessment.
E-04 Melody Echo — 3-Note Singing
Ear
Weeks 5–8 ▾
1Teacher sings (not plays) a 3-note melody using solfège or neutral syllable "la." Student listens, then sings it back exactly.
2Student then finds the starting note on the keyboard without help. Plays the full melody from memory.
3Advance to 4-note melody in Week 7. Gateway check uses 3-note melody.
The student who can sing it back has already understood the music. The keyboard is just the translation.
Keys Keyboard Geography & Staff Reading
K-01 Black Key Anchors — C and F
Keys
Week 1 ▾
1Eyes closed. Student places their right thumb anywhere they think is a C. Open eyes — check. Repeat 5 times without rhythm pressure.
2Teach the rule: C is the white key immediately to the left of any group of TWO black keys. F is the white key immediately to the left of any group of THREE black keys.
3Speed drill: teacher calls "C!" — student touches the nearest C within 2 seconds. Rotate through all C's on the keyboard. Then F. Then both mixed.
4Advance: teacher calls note names at random — C G D A E B F — student plays each in any octave within 3 seconds. No looking at the hand.
The goal is instantaneous visual pattern recognition, not counting from C. A student who still counts from C in Week 4 needs additional daily drill at home — 2 minutes of "name and find" with note flashcards.
K-02 Staff Reading — Line and Space Drill
Keys
Weeks 3–8 ▾
1Teacher draws (or prints) a grand staff with 5 notes marked — 3 treble, 2 bass. Student names each note aloud, then plays it on the keyboard.
2Use landmark notes first: Middle C (ledger below treble), E (first line treble), G (second line treble), B (middle line bass), G (second line from bottom bass).
3Advance from landmarks to all 5-position notes by Week 5. Gateway check requires 4/5 notes correct across both clefs.
4Step-skip reading drill: Show a note on staff. Ask "one step up — what note is that?" then "a skip up?" The student answers before you point.
Home practice tool: Teacher writes 5 random notes on manuscript paper at end of each lesson. Student names and plays them at home. Takes 3 minutes and dramatically accelerates note-reading fluency.
K-03 The Rapid Name Drill (Gateway Prep)
Keys
Weeks 7–8 ▾
1Teacher calls 5 note names in rapid succession (one every 2 seconds). Student finds each anywhere on the keyboard without hesitation.
2Second round: Teacher points to 5 notes on the grand staff. Student names each and immediately plays it. No "thinking time" — the name should arrive with the recognition.
3Gateway standard: 5/5 keyboard locations correct; 4/5 staff notes correctly named and played.
If the student is still slow at Week 7, add flashcard practice daily. The 2-second rule is non-negotiable for gateway — it signals genuine fluency vs. counting.
Sound Expression & Musical Intention
S-01 Character Game — Same Notes, Different Story
SoundEar
Week 3+ ▾
1Student plays C–D–E–D–C. Teacher asks: "Play it as a lullaby." Student plays. Teacher: "Now play it as a fanfare." Student plays. The notes don't change — only the intention does.
2Compare the two versions out loud: what changed? Dynamic? Tempo? Articulation? The student names the musical tools they instinctively used.
3Extend: "Play it as if you're saying goodbye." "Play it as if you're arriving home." Musical intention must precede physical action.
This exercise is not about correctness. There is no wrong answer. The goal is for the student to discover that they already have musical instincts — they just need permission to use them.
S-02 The One Beautiful Note
SoundBody
All weeks ▾
1Student plays a single note (teacher chooses, mid-register). Student listens to the full decay until silence.
2Teacher asks: "What did you hear? Was it a sound you'd want to hear more of?" Student answers before teacher comments.
3Play it again, aiming for a "better" version of that same note. The student is the judge. Teacher only asks: "Was that better or the same?"
Use this as the opening of every session — before scales, before repertoire. It calibrates the ear and reminds both teacher and student that tone quality is the foundation of all musical expression.
S-03 Pedal by Ear — Clean vs. Muddy
SoundEar
Week 6 ▾
1Teacher demonstrates: play a C major chord slowly (C–E–G) without pedal. Then with pedal. Student describes the difference: "room" vs. "outdoors" or "wet" vs. "dry."
2Student tries: holds pedal, plays C, then D. "Does that sound clean or blurred?" The student's own ear gives the rule.
3Rule derived by the student (not taught): "Change the pedal when the sound blurs." This is the only pedal rule needed in Stage 1.
4Apply to Piece 07 (Bell Tower): hold pedal on LH C throughout. Student decides — does it improve the piece?
Never give pedal rules before this exercise. The student who discovers the rule through their ears owns it permanently. The student who is told the rule will forget it.

Weekly & Mid-Stage Evaluation

Evaluation is embedded in every session — not reserved for Week 8. These tools help the teacher track progress weekly and identify which pillar needs attention before the gateway.

The Four Pillars — Stage 1 Evaluation Framework

Every evaluation in Stage 1 tests one or more of four pillars: Body (physical mechanics), Ear (aural perception and pitch), Keys (keyboard geography and staff reading), and Sound (musical expression and intention). A student may pass one pillar before others. Progress is always reported by pillar, never as a single score.

Pillar 01 — Body

Posture, Arm Weight & Tone

ObservedBench height correct; shoulders released; wrist level; curved finger shape; no collapsed knuckles.
Tone testStudent plays a single note with arm weight. Tone is resonant, not brittle. Arm relaxes after contact.
Dynamic testStudent plays p, mp, and f on same note. Dynamic changes are produced by arm speed — no added tension visible.
Pass criterionOne tone the teacher would "want to hear more of," produced with visible physical relaxation at all three dynamic levels.
If not passingReturn to Exercise B-01 (Arm Drop) and B-04 (Posture Reset) daily. Check bench height first — most tension originates from poor seating position.
Pillar 02 — Ear

Pitch, Intervals & Singing

Pitch testTeacher plays 5 notes (C–G range). Student sings each back accurately. Pass: 5/5.
Interval testTeacher plays 5 two-note intervals. Student identifies each as "step up/down" or "skip up/down." Pass: 4/5.
Melody echoTeacher sings a 3-note melody. Student sings it back, then finds it on keyboard. Pass: accurate sing-back and location.
Pass criterion4/5 intervals correct; melody sung back accurately and located on keyboard without hesitation.
If not passingAdd E-02 (Pitch Matching) and E-04 (Melody Echo) to every session. Check whether student sings along during playing — if the voice has stopped, the ear has stopped. Restore singing-first protocol before anything else.
Pillar 03 — Keys

Keyboard Geography & Staff Reading

Geography testTeacher calls 5 note names rapidly. Student finds each anywhere on keyboard within 2 seconds. Pass: 5/5.
Staff readingTeacher points to 5 notes on grand staff (3 treble, 2 bass). Student names each and plays it. Pass: 4/5.
Step-skipOn staff: teacher points to a note, asks "one step up?" — student names the next note without counting. Pass: 4/5 without counting.
Pass criterion5/5 keyboard locations; 4/5 staff notes named and played correctly without counting from a landmark.
If not passingAssign K-02 home worksheet — 5 notes written on manuscript, named and played daily. Add K-01 speed drill (call and find) to every session opening. Students who still count from C in Week 5 need daily home flashcard practice — 2 minutes per day closes the gap by Week 7.
Pillar 04 — Sound

Musical Intention & Expression

PerformanceStudent performs their chosen piece, one complete run-through without stopping. Teacher listens for legato connection, pulse stability, and dynamic shape.
Intention testTeacher asks: "What is this piece about?" Student answers before playing. Does the playing match the stated intention?
The key questionDoes this performance make the teacher want to hear the next piece?
Pass criterionAt least one moment of genuine musical intention — a dynamic choice, a phrasing decision, a moment of listening — that could not have been accidental.
If not passingThis is rarely about technique — it is usually about permission. Use S-01 (Character Game) and S-02 (One Beautiful Note) to restore musical ownership. Ask: "What do you want me to feel when you play this?" Then: "Play it so I feel that."

Weekly Progress Checklists

Use these at the end of each week's second session to confirm readiness to progress.

Week 1

Body foundation and first tone

  • Student can find Middle C without counting from any starting point
  • Arm-weight drop produces a resonant tone (no jabbing, no tension visible after contact)
  • Student can name all 7 white note names when called by teacher
  • Posture check: bench height, shoulder release, curved hand shape
  • Student can play a single note and describe what they heard (self-assessment begun)
Week 2

Five-finger position and singing protocol

  • RH five-finger position correct; LH five-finger position correct (both hands tested separately)
  • Weight transfer C–D–E produces even legato — no audible gap between notes
  • Student can find "Mary Had a Little Lamb" by ear with only "It starts on E" as a hint
  • Singing leads playing — voice arrives before each note on C–D–E exercises
  • All 7 notes found on keyboard within 3 seconds when called (no hesitation on F, B, or A)
Week 3

Legato, dynamics, and first notation

  • True legato demonstrated: C–G ascending/descending with no audible gap or blur
  • Student can identify p and f by ear (teacher plays, student names dynamic level)
  • Grand staff introduced: student names treble landmarks (Middle C, E, G) and bass landmarks (B, G)
  • First written piece: student reads note names aloud before playing each note
  • Dynamic shape applied to Mary Had a Little Lamb — student commits to one interpretation
Week 4

Rhythm, pulse, and alternating hands

  • Student can maintain pulse while teacher plays a different rhythm (pulse independence)
  • Quarter and half note distinction demonstrated by clapping and playing
  • Quarter rest: student continues internal pulse through silence — "the pulse never stops"
  • Alternating RH/LH on keyboard — no hesitation at hand-switch point
  • Two-bar piece with alternating hands performed with steady pulse and legato touch
Week 5

Staccato, phrase shaping, and interval ear

  • Staccato produced by "drop and bounce" — no finger-jabbing audible
  • Mixed articulation (legato steps, staccato skips) demonstrated on a short exercise
  • Student identifies question vs. answer phrase by ear before playing any piece
  • Step-skip identification: 8/10 correct on blind interval test (E-03)
  • Staff reading: student can navigate from any given note one step or skip up/down on the page
Week 6

Hands together and sustain pedal

  • First HT piece performed at slow tempo with steady pulse — no correction on the fly
  • Student can play RH while singing LH notes (cross-hand inner singing drill)
  • Pedal experiment: student knows the "change pedal when it blurs" rule — derived from their own ear
  • Posture maintained during HT playing — shoulder alignment has not collapsed under coordination demand
  • First full performance with both hands: student plays without stopping; teacher does not correct mid-performance
Week 7

Interpretation and mock gateway

  • Three pieces chosen for programme — each has a stated character, title, or story

Teacher Quick Reference

Printable one-page cheat sheets for common teaching moments, error patterns, and the exact words to use.

🎯

Session Opening — Every Session, Every Week

Always begin with

  • One Beautiful Note (Exercise S-02) — 90 seconds, calibrates the ear
  • Posture Reset (B-04) — bench, shoulders, wrist, hand shape
  • Ask: "What do you want to work on today?" — student ownership

Never begin with

  • Running through last week's piece from bar 1 without intention
  • Scales mechanically as a "warm-up" — no purpose stated
  • Correcting something before the student has played anything
⚠️

Common Error Patterns — What to Say and Do

Error → Response

  • Playing before singing: "Stop. Sing it first. Now play." Every time without exception.
  • Tension after note: "Drop the arm, let it rest there. Play the next note only when the arm is asleep."
  • Collapsed knuckles: "Hold a small orange. Now press. Does the orange drop? No. Keep the shape."
  • Rushing: "The pulse belongs to everyone. Walk it — don't chase it."
  • Stopping mid-piece: "In a performance, the only mistake is stopping. Keep going. Always."

Error → Response

  • Loud = tension: "Louder means faster arm, not harder pressing. Same drop, higher launch point."
  • Staccato = jabbing: "Drop and bounce. Like a rubber ball — the floor does the work."
  • Flat thumb: "Thumb plays on its outer side — the edge, not the nail. Try now."
  • 4th/5th finger legato gap: "Did you hear a space? Close your eyes. Listen again. Your ear knows."
  • Reading without hearing: "Before you play this bar, sing it. If you can't sing it, you can't play it."
🗓️

Phase Gate — What Must Be True Before Phase B Begins (Week 5)

  • All 7 note names found on keyboard within 2 seconds (no counting from C or F)
  • C–G five-finger scale played with audible legato in both hands separately
  • Quarter and half note distinction demonstrated by clapping and keyboard
  • Student sings before playing — consistently, not occasionally
  • Grand staff landmarks: Middle C, E, G treble; B and G bass — all named without prompting
  • One piece performed from memory with a stated musical intention
  • Arm-weight drop produces resonant tone at p and f — no tension visible
  • Pitch matching: 4/5 notes echoed accurately after teacher plays
If not all are met by end of Week 4Extend Phase A by one session — target the weakest pillar specifically. Do not begin Phase B until the student can demonstrate tone quality and five-finger legato. Everything in Phase B builds on these two foundations.
🎭

Musical Language — Phrases That Work in Stage 1

For tone and body

  • "Each note is a stone dropped into still water."
  • "Your arm is heavy. Let it fall."
  • "Would you want to hear that note if it were the only one in the room?"
  • "Play it so I want to hear the next note."
  • "The key goes down; the arm goes to sleep."

For expression and pulse

  • "What is this piece about? Tell me before you play."
  • "The pulse never stops — even in silence, even between pieces."
  • "If the singing stops, the hands stop."
  • "The voice is the real musician. The hands are only servants."
  • "Quiet does not mean small. The softest note can fill a room."
📋

Home Practice — What to Assign and How to Say It

Effective assignments

  • 5 minutes arm-weight drop on each note C–G, singing along (not "practice scales")
  • 5 notes written on manuscript paper at end of lesson — named and played at home daily
  • Play today's piece twice: once for accuracy, once for feeling. Which was better?
  • Listen to one piece of music and describe it in 3 words. Play something that sounds like those words.

Ineffective assignments

  • "Practice more" — no duration, no target
  • "Go over everything" — student will skip the hard parts
  • Assigning a passage before the student can sing it — they will practice errors
  • More than 3 separate tasks — compliance drops sharply after 3 items
