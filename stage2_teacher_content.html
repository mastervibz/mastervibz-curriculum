The Central Problem of Stage 2

Hand independence is not a mechanical problem — it is an attention problem. When both hands play simultaneously, the brain defaults to treating them as one unit and synchronises them. The result: rhythmic lockstep, the left hand copying the right hand's dynamics, and the inability to differentiate two musical lines.

The inner-singing method is the most effective solution: the student plays one hand while singing the other. The voice bridges the two hemispheres of brain activity required. Once the student can do this fluently, HT coordination follows naturally — because the musical content of each hand has been internalised separately before being combined.

Non-negotiable ruleIf the singing stops, the hands stop. The voice is load-bearing — not decorative. This rule applies from Session 1 to Session 40. Never relax it.

The Sight-Reading Discipline — Begin Week 5, Keep Forever

From Week 5 onwards, every lesson begins with 10 minutes of sight-reading on material one full level below the student's current repertoire. The rules are iron-clad:

  • Play through once — no stopping, no correction, no repeat
  • Material must be below current level — easy enough to practice reading, not struggling with notes
  • After: one observation only ("What was the hardest moment?") — then move on. Never drill the sight-reading piece
  • The skill being practiced is continuity, not accuracy
Sight-reading used as a performance opportunity — slowing at hard passages, correcting errors — must be stopped immediately. Reset: one play-through, continuous, no going back.

The Standard Preparation Process for Every New HT Piece

This process is never skipped, never shortened, never done out of order. A student who skips any step is practicing the wrong thing.

1
Teacher plays — student listens with eyes closed
2
RH alone — student sings along
3
LH alone — student sings along
4
RH plays, LH sung aloud (then swap)
5
HT — singing continues under the breath

Session 1 The singing-while-playing drill
CoordTake a Stage 1 piece with both hands. Student plays RH while speaking/singing the LH note names aloud. Slow tempo — the voice must genuinely name each note, not mumble. 8 min.
CoordReverse: LH plays, student sings the RH note names. Same slow tempo. Notice which direction is harder — this reveals which hand the student "leads" mentally. 8 min.
EarPlay both hands together. Student notices HT is easier than expected — because both lines were fully internalised. Teacher confirms: "This is always how we prepare a new piece." 4 min.
EarTeacher plays new HT piece in full. Student listens with eyes closed: "Which hand has the melody? Does the LH have a pattern or move freely?" Listening analysis before any playing. 5 min.
RepertoireStudent reads and plays RH alone singing along, then LH alone singing along, then RH while singing LH. Full inner-singing process. 7 min.
SoundHT attempt. One rule: if the singing stops, the hands stop. The voice is load-bearing. First HT run at whatever tempo allows continuous voice. 3 min.
Teacher framingThe inner-singing method works because the voice bridges the two hemispheres of brain activity required for hand independence. A student who can sing one hand while playing the other has already solved the coordination problem. The hands just need time to confirm what the brain already knows.
Session 2 Rhythmic independence: different values HT
CoordLH holds a whole note (C, four counts) while RH plays C–D–E–F in quarter notes. The LH must not "pulse" with the RH — it holds, genuinely still. Student taps foot for pulse. 8 min.
CoordReverse: RH holds whole note while LH plays a four-quarter pattern. Does the sustained hand keep ringing? Does it resist the urge to accent with the other hand? 7 min.
EarTeacher plays both versions — one where both hands accent together (wrong) and one where they're independent. Student identifies by ear. "Your ear is better at this than your hands — let the ear lead the correction." 5 min.
KeysIntroduce dotted half note (3 counts). Clap-and-count: "ta-a-a." Then play on single note. Teacher counts aloud — student holds for exactly three beats. 5 min.
RepertoireNew piece featuring dotted half notes in one hand vs. quarter notes in the other. Full inner-singing preparation before any HT attempt. This is week's home practice target. 10 min.
Watch for — Week 1
  • Student mouthing note names silently rather than singing audibly — the voice must be heard; silent mouthing bypasses the auditory feedback loop
  • Hands rushing to HT before each hand is individually secure — enforce the full process
  • LH thumping on long held notes — the sustained hand tends to accent sympathetically; ask it to "simply ring, as if forgotten"
Home practice — Week 1 (20 min/day)
  • 8 min — Dotted-half piece: full inner-singing preparation before any HT. Log which direction (RH play/LH sing vs. LH play/RH sing) is harder — report to teacher next session
  • 7 min — Long-note independence: LH whole note + RH quarters, then reverse. Foot taps pulse throughout
  • 5 min — Stage 1 piece review: play any Stage 1 piece to consolidate tone quality and legato. "Don't forget where you came from."
Week 2 · Sessions 3–4
Position Shifts and Thumb Crossings
C major scale begins · G position introduced · First position-shift HT piece
Phase A
▾
Session 3 Thumb crossing: the C major scale begins
BodyDemonstrate on fallboard (off keys): fingers 1–2–3 tap, then thumb slides under to arrive at finger 4's note position before that finger lifts. The thumb is already in place — it does not swing up to meet the key. 5 min.
BodyRH only: C–D–E, thumb crosses to F. Stop. Reset. Repeat 8 times. The crossing must be invisible — no wrist lurch, no shoulder dip. "A sleeping cat walks across your hand and feels nothing." 8 min.
EarListen test: teacher plays C–D–E–F with visible wrist rock vs. smooth thumb crossing. Student identifies difference by ear — the rocky version produces an accent on F. The ear hears what the eye misses. 7 min.
SoundOne-octave C major scale RH: C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C. Sing it as do-re-mi first. Every note the same tone quality — no accent on the thumb crossing. Played with crescendo ascending, diminuendo descending. 15 min.
Session 4 LH position shifts and the G position
KeysG position: RH thumb on G, five fingers spanning G–A–B–C–D. LH fifth finger on G below, spanning G–A–B–C–D. This is the first position shift from C. Same weight, same tone quality. 6 min.
BodyLateral arm movement drill: C position five-note scale slowly, then without lifting the wrist, slide the whole arm one position to the right to G position. "The hand is a passenger. The arm drives." 8 min.
RepertoireShort piece: C position for 4 bars, then G position for 4 bars. The shift must be smooth — no pause, no re-positioning fidget. 6 min.
CoordHT piece where RH shifts position mid-piece while LH stays in C. Full inner-singing preparation. Challenge: the singing must account for the RH's new note set after the shift. 10 min.
EarHarmonic listening: when both hands are in C position, hum a note that fits (C, E, or G). When RH shifts to G, which LH notes still fit? Earliest harmonic awareness — not theory, just ear. 5 min.
Watch for — Week 2
  • Wrist lurching inward during finger-3 over-crossing on descending scale — correct by slowing to half tempo and watching the wrist
  • Position shifts done with a visible pause or "look-down" — the hand should know where it's going before the phrase ends; practice the arrival separately, eyes closed
  • Accent on the note after the thumb crossing — the thumb note is never louder than its neighbours
Home practice — Week 2 (20 min/day)
  • 5 min — C major scale RH, ascending and descending, with dynamic arc. Eyes closed — listen for evenness at the crossing point
  • 8 min — HT piece with position change: full process (HS each, cross-singing, then HT)
  • 7 min — G position five-finger exercise: LH and RH separately, then together in unison. Identical tone quality in both hands
Week 3 · Sessions 5–6
Ties, Rests, and 3/4 Time
Silent pulse maintained through rests · Ties vs. slurs by ear · Waltz feel — ONE-two-three as physical sensation
Phase A
▾
Session 5 Ties, rests, and the silent pulse
EarTeacher claps 4-beat rhythm with rest on beat 3: clap–clap–(silence)–clap. Student echoes. Then student taps foot on beat 3 during the rest. "The pulse never stops — only the sound does." 5 min.
KeysQuarter rest on piano: play C–D–(rest)–E–F. The rest hand lifts — but foot pulse continues. Student feels a physical impulse on beat 3 even with no sound. 8 min.
RepertoirePiece with quarter and half rests in both hands at different moments. Coordination challenge: RH rests while LH plays, and vice versa. Inner-singing covers the resting hand's silence. 7 min.
KeysTie: play C (quarter), tie to C (half note). Finger stays depressed — the key never comes back up. Teacher places a hand over the student's to feel: no bounce on the barline. Three counts total, one press. 5 min.
EarTie vs. slur listening: teacher plays — one tied (one long sound), one slurred (two connected notes). Student identifies. Tie = one sound; slur = two. Ear must distinguish before notation explains. 5 min.
RepertoirePiece featuring ties across barlines. Student circles the second note of each tie in pencil: "This note is silent — I already pressed it." 5 min.
Session 6 3/4 time: the waltz feel
EarPlay a waltz recording (Strauss or similar). Student sways or steps to feel ONE-two-three, ONE-two-three. ONE is heavy; 2 and 3 are light. Body owns the feel before notation is shown. 4 min.
Keys3/4 exercise: LH plays one note on beat 1, RH plays two notes on beats 2 and 3. ONE has more weight — by intention, not force. "Beat 1 is where you land; beats 2 and 3 are where you float." 6 min.
RepertoireFirst 3/4 piece: a simple waltz. Mark bar lines in pencil. Count aloud for first four bars before playing. Body sways slightly. Metric feel must be physical, not just numerical. 5 min.
CoordClassic waltz pattern: LH bass note on beat 1, then chord on beats 2 and 3. First time LH has a repeating accompaniment pattern — not a melody. Inner-singing: play LH, sing RH melody. 10 min.
SoundBalance: RH melody must be louder than LH. But LH beat 1 (bass note) slightly fuller than beats 2–3. Three dynamic levels in the LH alone. This is the characteristic waltz sound. 6 min.
Watch for — Week 3
  • All three beats in 3/4 played with equal weight — the waltz lilt requires heavier beat 1; ask student to "land" on 1 and "float" on 2 and 3
  • Re-attacking tied notes — mark the tied note in pencil every time; the eye must be reminded before the hand can be retrained
  • LH accompaniment drowning the RH melody — reduce LH weight by 30% consciously
Home practice — Week 3 (20 min/day)
  • 5 min — Waltz LH pattern alone (bass note + two chord notes) in 3/4. Count aloud. Foot taps beat 1 only
  • 10 min — Waltz piece HT: inner-singing preparation first, then full piece. If melody singing stops, hands stop
  • 5 min — Rest and tie exercise: clap a 4-bar rhythm (teacher provides), then play on single note with tied notes marked
Week 4 · Sessions 7–8
The LH as an Equal Voice
Bass lines with direction · Two-note chords · Oom-pah pattern · Balance between voices
Phase A
▾
Session 7 LH melodic bass lines and two-note chords
EarTeacher plays same piece with two LH approaches: flat/mechanical vs. shaped bass line (crescendo toward peak, slight weight on strong beats). "If someone only heard your LH, would it be musical on its own?" 4 min.
RepertoirePiece where LH has a moving bass line. Student prepares LH alone first with its own dynamic shape. Then HT: RH holds a long note while LH bass line moves beneath. Attention fully on LH. 16 min.
BodyTwo-note chord technique: both notes must drop simultaneously with arm weight. No rolling. Drop test: raise hand 2cm and let fall as unit. Both notes sound at exactly the same instant. 5 min.
EarName two-note intervals: C+E (a third, warm and close), C+G (a fifth, open and hollow). Student plays each and identifies by aural character. No theory labels — just ear. 5 min.
RepertoirePiece with two-note LH chords on beats 1 and 3. Chord must be even — not louder on bottom or top. Check: teacher covers ears — "I should not be able to guess which note is on top." 5 min.
Session 8 Combining bass notes and chords — the oom-pah pattern
TheoryOom-pah: LH plays a single bass note (oom) on beat 1, then a two-note chord (pah) on beat 2. Most common LH accompaniment pattern in beginner-intermediate repertoire. Requires a position shift within the LH. 8 min.
BodyLH shift: 5th finger on bass note, then thumb and 3rd finger on chord. The shift must be seamless — arm weight transfers without audible gap. Practice LH alone until the pattern is automatic. 8 min.
CoordHT with oom-pah: LH has two different physical tasks per bar — the first genuinely complex coordination moment. Inner-singing: play LH oom-pah, sing RH melody. Confirm melody is singing freely before HT. 7 min.
SoundBalance check: RH melody is dominant. The oom (bass) is audible but subordinate. The pah (chord) is the softest element. Three dynamic levels in one bar — all from arm weight variation, never tension. 7 min.
Teacher framing for this weekMost beginner methods treat the left hand as subordinate. This method does not. From week 4, the student learns to give the LH its own musical identity — a bass line that has direction, weight, and character. This pays off enormously in Stage 3 when they encounter music that genuinely requires two independent musical voices.
Watch for — Week 4
  • Uneven two-note chords — students consistently play the bottom note louder; ask them to deliberately play the top note louder for one bar, then find the even middle point
  • The oom-pah pattern making the LH rigid — the shift must stay relaxed; stiffness in the LH wrist is the main cause of unevenness
  • LH oom-pah overpowering the RH melody — address it directly and early; this is the defining imbalance of intermediate playing
Home practice — Week 4 (20–25 min/day)
  • 5 min — LH oom-pah pattern alone: one chord pattern, four bars, until automatic. No looking at the hand
  • 10 min — HT piece with oom-pah: inner-singing preparation mandatory. Attention on RH melody dominance
  • 5 min — C major scale, both hands in contrary motion (RH up, LH down from middle C simultaneously)
Week 5 · Sessions 9–10
Eighth Notes, Dotted Rhythms, Sight-Reading Begins
Two eighth notes = one beat · Dotted quarter + eighth · Sight-reading introduced as daily discipline
Phase A
▾
Session 9 Eighth notes and the subdivided pulse
KeysFirst sight-reading session (10 min): Teacher selects a simple fresh piece (Stage 1 level). Student scans silently 30 sec: "What's the time signature? Where are the rests? Any position change?" Then: one continuous play-through. No stopping. After: "What was the hardest moment?" One observation only — move on.
EarClap eighth notes: "ta-ta ta-ta ta-ta ta-ta." Then student taps foot on the quarter beat while clapping eighths — two levels of pulse simultaneously in the body. 5 min.
KeysPlay pairs of eighth notes on single pitches: C–C D–D E–E F–F. Each pair is one beat. The two notes must be exactly equal — not one short, one long. 8 min.
RepertoirePiece with eighth notes in RH against quarter notes in LH: first true polyrhythmic challenge — 2:1 in each hand simultaneously. Inner-singing: play LH quarters while saying "ta-ta" for each RH eighth pair. 7 min.
SoundArticulation of eighth notes: pairs slightly lighter than quarters — they move faster and need less arm weight per note. "Eighths are more like brushing, quarters more like pressing." 5 min.
Session 10 Dotted quarter + eighth rhythm
KeysSight-reading (10 min daily discipline): new piece, same level as last session. Scan, then play through. One chance. Teacher logs fluency, note accuracy, pulse maintenance.
EarClap dotted quarter + eighth: "ta-a-ta ta-a-ta." Listen to familiar songs: "Greensleeves," "I've Been Working on the Railroad." Student identifies the dotted feel by ear before reading it. 6 min.
KeysPlay dotted quarter + eighth on single pitches. The short note arrives exactly on the half-beat subdivision. Teacher counts "1-and-2-and" — the short note falls on "and." Precision here prevents a lifetime of sloppy dotted rhythms. 8 min.
RepertoireA well-known march or song in dotted rhythm. Sing it first — the body already knows the rhythm. Playing follows the singing. 7 min.
EarCommon error demonstration: teacher plays the dotted rhythm "swung" (too uneven, jazz feel) vs. precise (classical notation). Student identifies which matches the written score. 4 min.
Watch for — Week 5
  • Sight-reading used as a performance opportunity — student slows at hard passages, corrects errors, repeats bars; stop immediately and reset: one play-through, continuous, no going back
  • Dotted rhythms played as equal eighths (swung) — the short note must arrive precisely on the half-beat; counting "1-and" with the teacher is the fix
  • Eighth note pairs rushing — a physical cue (slight lean back on eighth-note bars) can help
Home practice — Week 5 (25 min/day)
  • 10 min — Sight-reading at home: one new simple piece daily. One play-through only. Circle any note they couldn't read in time and check it after
  • 10 min — Dotted rhythm repertoire piece: full preparation process, then HT at a tempo that keeps the dotted rhythm precise
  • 5 min — Eighth-note exercise: C–D–E–F–G in eighth notes, RH alone, then LH alone, then HT in unison

C Major Scale HT and G Major Introduced

Phase A

Session 11 C major scale HT: the asymmetric crossing
BodyMap the crossings: RH thumb crosses under after E (onto F). LH thumb crosses under after G (onto A) ascending. These happen at different points — the hands are not mirror images. Student marks the crossing notes on the score. 6 min.
CoordSlow HT at ♩=40: one note per beat, teacher counts. At the crossing points, each hand moves independently — do not let one hand dictate the other's rhythm. Stop and identify the exact beat where each crossing occurs. 10 min.
SoundEvenness test: teacher closes eyes, student plays. Teacher must not be able to identify any crossing point by sound. Accents at crossing = not ready for tempo increase. Silence = move up by ♩=5. 9 min.
EarScale as melody: play C major with full musical arc — crescendo ascending, diminuendo descending. Sing the scale while playing. "A scale is eight notes of music, not eight notes of exercise." 5 min.
RepertoireFind the scale pattern in a real piece: teacher highlights a scale run in a method book piece. Student identifies: "This is the same pattern we just practiced." 5 min.
Session 12 G major scale and the F-sharp
KeysSight-reading (10 min): piece now includes eighth notes — level advanced. Student scans (30 sec), identifies rhythm patterns before playing. One continuous play-through.
TheoryWhy G major has one sharp: teacher plays the G major scale using all white keys. "Does that sound right?" Student hears the wrong note (F natural feels a step too low). Then F-sharp: "Now it sounds like a proper scale." The ear discovers the sharps — the rule follows the sound. 6 min.
KeysG major scale RH: G–A–B–C–D–E–F#–G. The F# is a black key — thumb crosses under after B (not after E as in C major). New crossing point, new muscle memory. Practice the B–F# segment specifically. 10 min.
RepertoireShort piece in G major (one sharp key signature). Student identifies the key before playing. "Every time you see one sharp, the home note is G and F is always sharp." 9 min.
Watch for — Week 6
  • C major scale HT rushed before evenness is achieved — always test with teacher's closed eyes; crossing accents mean the tempo target is not reached
  • F-sharp forgotten mid-piece — circle every F in the piece before playing and say "sharp" as each one is approached
  • Sight-reading quality plateauing — if not improving week-on-week, the material is too hard; drop the level further
Home practice — Week 6 (25 min/day)
  • 5 min — C major scale HT daily at ♩=50. Raise tempo by 5 only when teacher cannot hear the crossing
  • 5 min — G major scale RH, paying attention to the new crossing point (after B). Sing the scale while playing
  • 10 min — G major piece: inner-singing process, then HT. Mark every F# before starting
  • 5 min — Sight-reading: one new simple piece, one play-through only

Week 8 · Sessions 15–16
Triads — The Harmonic Vocabulary Begins
C, G, F major triads · Sound before theory · Even chord drop technique · LH triads + RH melody balance
Phase B
▾
Session 15 The major triad: sound before theory
EarTeacher plays three chords: C major (C–E–G), a minor-sounding chord, a dissonant cluster. Student arranges from "most settled" to "least settled." The major triad is the most stable sound in Western harmony — the ear knows this before the theory says it. 4 min.
TheoryMajor triad structure: root, third above, fifth above. Not as an interval formula — as physical finger placement. RH: thumb–3rd–5th on C–E–G. Play C, G, and F major triads in this position. 8 min.
SoundTriad evenness: all three notes must arrive simultaneously. Drop test: raise hand 2cm and let fall as a unit. No leading finger, no lagging finger. "The chord is one sound, not three sounds close together." 8 min.
CoordFirst piece using LH triads: C major triad on beat 1 of every bar, RH plays a simple melody. Inner-singing: play LH triads, sing RH melody. LH triad is a single sustained event; RH melody is the changing element. 10 min.
EarHarmonic listening: as the RH melody moves over the C major LH triad, which melody notes sound "inside" the chord (consonant) and which sound "outside" (adding tension)? No labels — just aural identification. 5 min.
Watch for — Week 8
  • Uneven triad — one finger (usually the 5th) arriving late; the drop technique is the fix, not individual finger targeting
  • LH triad too loud against RH melody — chord is harmonic support; aim for 60% of the RH volume
  • Student not yet fluent in Phase A skills — if inner-singing is still unreliable, slow the Phase B pace and continue Phase A repairs alongside
Week 9 · Sessions 17–18
Triad Inversions and I–V Progression by Ear
Root, first and second inversion by feel · Voice-leading for smooth chord movement · I–V as tension and resolution · Harmonic rhythm
Phase B
▾
Session 17 Inversions as different weights of the same chord
EarCompare: moving from C major root to G major root (big jump) vs. C major root to G major first inversion (smooth voice-leading, small movement). "Inversions exist to make chord changes graceful." 6 min.
TheoryRoot position: C–E–G. First inversion: E–G–C. Second inversion: G–C–E. Play all three, listen to how the sound shifts from settled to floating. Identify each by its "feel" — not by calculation. 8 min.
RepertoirePiece using smooth inversion changes: C major root → G major first inversion → C major. The LH barely moves — yet the harmony changes. Student listens for the harmonic shift while the fingers do minimal work. 6 min.
EarI–V progression: teacher plays four bars of C major, then one bar of G major, then returns to C. "What did that G bar feel like?" Students describe: unstable, pulling, wanting to return. "That pulling feeling is what makes music move." 6 min.
RepertoireFolk melody harmonised with I and V chords only. Student matches LH chords by ear: "Does this bar feel like home (I) or away (V)?" Before reading the harmony, hear it. 9 min.
Teacher framingInversions are taught by ear first — teacher plays root, first inversion, second inversion, student arranges from "most settled" to "most unstable." Root is most stable; second inversion is least. The student already knows this — the ear knows. Theory labels follow the aural recognition, never precede it.
Watch for — Week 9
  • Inversions learned as patterns rather than sounds — test by playing an inversion and asking the student to name it by ear without looking; if they can only identify visually, the ear hasn't caught up
  • Harmonic rhythm ignored — student changes chords wherever convenient rather than on the correct beat; mark harmonic change points explicitly

Scale Programme — Stage 2

Six major keys and A minor are introduced across the 20 weeks. Each scale is introduced by ear — the student identifies why the sharp or flat is needed before being told the rule. All scales are learned HT with a dynamic arc. By the gateway, the student plays four at random without preparation.

The Scale Standard — Stage 2

A scale is passed when: the teacher closes their eyes and cannot identify any thumb crossing by sound; the scale is played with a deliberate dynamic arc (not flat); the key signature is recalled correctly before playing; both directions are played HT at ♩=60 minimum. Any accent at a crossing point means the scale is not yet passed — regardless of speed.

How to Introduce Every New Scale

1
Play using all white keys — "Does that sound right?"
2
Student hears the wrong note — ear discovers the problem
3
Play corrected note — "Now it sounds like a proper scale"
4
Rule is named after the ear has confirmed it — never before

Scales as Musical Vocabulary — Not as Exercises

Scales are taught badly in most piano methods — as mechanical drills divorced from music. This method treats scales as musical vocabulary: patterns that appear in actual pieces and that the hand must own in order to play fluently.

Each scale is always played with a dynamic arc — crescendo ascending, diminuendo descending — and the student is expected to sing along. The teacher regularly points to scale passages in repertoire: "This is the same pattern. Your hands already know it."

Nigerian repertoire adaptation

Where Western method book pieces are unavailable or unfamiliar, these substitutions work well at Stage 2 level. The pedagogical goal (inner singing, position shifts, triad harmony) does not change — only the melodic material.

  • Phase A folk songs → "Kini Ife" or "Siyahamba" arranged for simple HT in C position
  • Phase B triad pieces → Highlife chord patterns (I–IV–V) in simple 4/4 HT arrangement
  • Phase C character piece → Original composition by student based on a personal experience or image — teacher provides the harmonic framework, student invents the melody

Stage 2 Gateway Assessment

Session 40 · Four components. The central criterion: can the student play HT and read at sight? Partial passes move specific pillars forward into Stage 3 while targeted work continues on weak areas. The gateway is a diagnostic conversation, not an examination.

The Stage 2 Gateway Question

Does this student make musical decisions, or do they only play notes? A student who passes the Stage 2 gateway has earned the right to be treated as a musician. That shift in relationship — from teacher-guiding to artist-consulting — is the first thing that changes in Stage 3.

01

HT Coordination — Inner Singing on Demand

Teacher names any piece from Stage 2. Student plays one hand while singing the other, without preparation. Then plays both hands together. No warm-up. No specific piece chosen in advance.

Required
□ Inner singing is genuinely audible and continuous
□ HT playing reflects independent voices
□ Melody audibly louder than accompaniment
□ Rhythmic values in each hand distinctly different where score requires
Distinction
□ Inner singing continues seamlessly through position changes
□ HT can be demonstrated on any piece, not just prepared ones
□ The two voices have genuinely different characters
Pass criterionInner singing is audible and continuous. HT playing reflects independent voices — melody louder than accompaniment; rhythmic independence genuine where required by the score.
02

Sight-Reading — Reading Music, Not Decoding Notation

Teacher presents a fresh piece at Stage 2 level (two-page, simple HT, key signature of up to two sharps or flats). Student scans for 60 seconds, then plays one continuous read-through. No second attempt.

Required
□ Pulse maintained throughout — no stops
□ Eyes move ahead of the playing hand
□ Patterns recognised, not decoded note-by-note
□ Key signature applied correctly
Important note
Imperfect note accuracy is acceptable. Musical continuity is not negotiable. A student who plays every note correctly but stops twice has not passed. A student with some wrong notes but continuous pulse has passed.
Pass criterionPulse maintained throughout. No stopping or restarting. Eyes ahead. Patterns recognised. Musical continuity is the only non-negotiable criterion.

Teacher Quick Reference — Stage 2

Print these and keep them on the music stand. The most common teaching moments in Stage 2, and exactly what to say.

🎯

Session Opening — Every Session

Always begin with

  • One beautiful note (S-02 from Stage 1 — still applies): calibrate the ear before anything else
  • Posture reset: bench, shoulders, wrist, hand shape — 60 seconds
  • Scale of the week: one scale, HT, with dynamic arc, eyes closed listening test
  • From Week 5: sight-reading (10 min, one play-through, no correction, no repeat)

Never begin with

  • Running through last session's piece from bar 1 without intention
  • Scales mechanically with no dynamic shape — "exercise mode" is musical death
  • Correcting anything before the student has played something
  • Sight-reading on material at or above the student's current level
⚠️

Common Stage 2 Error Patterns — What to Say

Coordination errors

  • Both hands rushing to HT: "Stop. RH sings the LH first. Aloud — I must hear you."
  • LH louder than RH melody: "Play the LH at 40% of what you just played. Now add RH. Which voice do you hear?"
  • Rhythmic lockstep (hands move as one): "Slow to half tempo. Play LH only, tap RH rhythm on your knee. They are two different people."
  • Sustained hand pulsing with moving hand: "The held hand is asleep. Only the moving hand is awake. Play again."

Reading and scale errors

  • Sight-reading with corrections: "One chance only. Start again from bar 1. No matter what happens — keep going."
  • Key signature forgotten mid-piece: "Before you play: name every accidental in this key. Now circle every one in the piece. Then play."
  • Thumb crossing audible: "Eyes closed. Tell me which note I can hear as different. That is the one you're tensing for."
  • Triad uneven: "Drop your hand from 3cm above the keys. Don't aim any finger — let gravity decide. Land. Listen."
  • Inversion identified visually not aurally: "I'll play it with the fallboard down. You tell me: settled, floating, or neutral?"
📋

Phase Gate — What Must Be True Before Phase B (Week 8)

  • Inner-singing can be demonstrated on demand on any Phase A piece without preparation
  • C major scale HT with no audible crossing accent at ♩=60
  • G major scale RH with F# consistently applied
  • Waltz 3/4 feel genuinely physical — beat 1 heavier than 2 and 3 without being told
  • Dotted rhythms precise — short note arrives exactly on the half-beat
  • Oom-pah pattern automatic in LH — no looking at hand, no hesitation at shift
  • Sight-reading: one complete play-through of a Stage 1 piece with no stops
  • Ties and rests handled correctly — no re-attack, no rushed silence
If not all met by end of Week 7Extend Phase A for two additional sessions — target the weakest milestone specifically. Phase B triads build directly on the inner-singing foundation. A student who is not fluent at inner-singing will not achieve genuine harmonic hearing in Phase B.
🎭

Musical Language — Phrases That Work in Stage 2

For coordination

  • "If the singing stops, the hands stop. Always."
  • "The hand is a passenger. The arm drives."
  • "A sleeping cat walks across your wrist and feels nothing."
  • "These are two different people having a conversation — not one person talking to themselves."
  • "The held note is asleep. Let it ring and forget about it."

For harmony and reading

  • "Does that sound like home, or away?"
  • "That pulling feeling is what makes music move. The V chord is the engine."
  • "Is this student reading music or decoding notation? That is the sight-reading question."
  • "A scale is eight notes of music, not eight notes of exercise."
  • "The chord is one sound. Not three sounds close together."
📝

What Changes in Stage 3

Stage 3 is where the student stops being a learner and starts being a musician. The coordination and reading tools are now in place. Stage 3 uses them in service of real musical goals: scales as vocabulary rather than exercises, harmonic understanding that enables interpretation, and repertoire that has genuine artistic weight.

The student who passes the Stage 2 gateway has earned the right to be treated as a musician. That shift in relationship — from teacher-guiding to artist-consulting — is the first thing that changes.
