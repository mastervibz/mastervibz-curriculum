Piano from Beginner to Mastery

Stage 1 — Foundation
Body and Sound

A complete 8-week session-by-session curriculum. Two sessions per week. Four pillars running in parallel from day one.

Duration8 weeks
Sessions16 lessons
Lesson length30–45 min
Daily practice15–20 min
GatewayEnd of week 8
01
First contact — body before notes
The student meets the instrument. No reading. No pieces. Pure sensation and listening.
Phase A
Teacher's framing for this week

Resist every urge to start playing melodies. Week 1 is about calibration — of the body, the ear, and the relationship to sound. A student who arrives expecting to play songs needs to understand that learning to produce one beautiful tone is a genuine achievement. Frame it that way.

Session 1 — The instrument and the body
First 15 min Setup and anatomy
  • Body Set bench height: elbow level with keys, forearm roughly parallel to floor. Student sits on front half of bench, feet flat. 5 min
  • Body Arm swing test: student lets arm hang from shoulder, swings loose from elbow. Teacher checks for locked joint. "Remember this feeling — this is the arm we bring to the piano." 5 min
  • Keys Explore the keyboard freely — no instruction. Student presses any key they wish. Teacher asks: "Which sounds higher? Lower? Which feels softer to press?" 5 min
Next 20 min The arm-weight drop
  • Sound Teach the arm-weight drop: raise forearm, feel complete looseness, lower onto middle C using gravity only. Listen. Repeat 5× on each hand separately. 8 min
  • Ear Compare: one tone produced with weight drop vs. one poked hard with a finger. Ask: "Which rings longer? Which sounds rounder?" Student must listen before answering. 5 min
  • Sound Weight drop on three different notes (low, middle, high register). Same technique — different sound. "The piano changes; your body doesn't." 7 min
Session 2 — Listening and landmarks
First 15 min Ear — high / low / contour
  • Ear Teacher plays 5 short musical excerpts (varied register). Student points: left hand toward bass end, right toward treble. No wrong answers — just listening. 5 min
  • Ear Contour game: teacher plays a 3-note phrase. Student draws the contour in the air (hand goes up, down, or stays). Repeat with 8 different phrases. 5 min
  • Ear Pitch matching: teacher sings a note (middle register, comfortable for student). Student sings back. Do not correct pitch with piano — use voice to demonstrate. 6 notes total. 5 min
Next 20 min Black key landmarks + C position
  • Keys Two-black and three-black clusters: student places both hands over the keyboard, eyes closed, and finds every two-black group by touch. Teacher counts aloud: 8 groups across full keyboard. 6 min
  • Keys Introduce C: "The white key immediately left of the two-black group." Find all C's across the keyboard, eyes open, then eyes closed. 6 min
  • Sound Play every C from low to high with arm-weight drop, right hand. Listen to how the same technique produces different sounds in different registers. "It's still you — the piano just resonates differently." 8 min
Watch for — Week 1
  • Locked elbows during the arm-weight drop — arm must hang free from shoulder, not from the elbow
  • Pressing rather than dropping — if they tense the forearm before the key, the sound will be clipped; demonstrate the difference audibly
  • Student sitting too far back on the bench — this collapses the posture and forces forward lean
  • Pitch matching avoidance (especially adult learners) — normalise singing immediately; if embarrassment is present, sing together
Home practice — Week 1 (15 min/day)
5 min — Arm-weight drops on middle C, 10× each hand. Focus on the ring of the tone, not the motion.
5 min — Find all C's across the keyboard from memory, eyes closed.
5 min — Listen to one piece of music (any genre) and draw the contour of the melody on paper as it plays.
02
Hand shape, note names, weight transfer
The five-finger position is introduced. All seven white note names learned. Weight begins to move.
Phase A
Teacher's framing for this week

The five-finger position is not a cage — it's a launching pad. Be explicit about this. The student should understand that these five notes are a temporary home, not a permanent limit. Introduce it as "the place we return to while we're learning to move."

Session 3 — Five-finger position and D, E
First 15 min Hand shape + position
  • Body The curved finger: student holds an imaginary small orange. Fingers gently curved, thumb on its side. Place on keys — knuckles must not collapse inward when pressing. Check each finger individually. 5 min
  • Keys Right hand five-finger position: thumb on middle C, fingers on D, E, F, G. Name each finger's note aloud as it presses. "Thumb is C. Second finger is D. Third is E." Repeat 3×. 5 min
  • Keys Left hand five-finger position: fifth finger on the C below middle C, ascending to G. Same naming exercise. Both hands know their home positions by end of session. 5 min
Next 20 min Weight transfer — C to D to E
  • Sound Weight transfer exercise: from C, roll the arm weight to D, then to E. No lifting between notes — the hand should feel like water moving. Listen for evenness. Each note should ring equally long. 8 min
  • Ear Sing C–D–E as you play. "Do–Re–Mi." The voice must lead — play only after the inner ear has the note ready. If the student can't sing the next note before playing, slow down. 7 min
  • Sound C–D–E–D–C, right hand only. Play it forward and backward. Teacher gives a tempo with finger tapping. No rushing — each note gets full weight and full ring. 5 min
Session 4 — All seven note names + singing leads playing
First 15 min F, G, A, B — complete the alphabet
  • Keys Three-black cluster anchors F and B. Teach F (white key left of three-black), G, A, B as a group. Student finds each note across three octaves. Teacher calls the name; student finds without hesitation. 7 min
  • Keys Musical alphabet quiz: teacher calls a note name, student plays it anywhere on the keyboard within 3 seconds. Run through all 7 names, then randomise. Speed is secondary to accuracy. 5 min
  • Ear Solfège in five-finger range: sing C–D–E–F–G ascending and descending. Teacher models, student echoes. Then student leads while playing, voice slightly ahead of the hand. 3 min
Next 20 min First five-note piece (by ear)
  • Ear Teacher plays "Mary Had a Little Lamb" (C position, right hand). Student listens with eyes closed, then draws the contour. Teacher plays again — student sings along. No keyboard yet. 5 min
  • Repertoire Student finds the piece by ear. Teacher says only: "It starts on E." Student experiments. Teacher confirms each correct note with a nod. Piece should be found within 10–12 attempts. This is discovery, not instruction. 10 min
  • Sound Once found: play it with weight transfer, singing along. "Every note you play should already be in your voice before your finger moves." 5 min
Watch for — Week 2
  • Collapsed knuckles — particularly on the third and fourth fingers; gently lift the knuckle arch with a finger if needed
  • Thumb playing flat (nail side down) — correct immediately; the thumb plays on its outer edge to allow free wrist movement
  • Playing before singing — if the hand moves before the voice, stop and restart from singing first
  • Rushing during weight transfer — each note needs a full, settled moment; think "pour" not "skip"
Home practice — Week 2 (15 min/day)
5 min — Weight transfer C–D–E–D–C, both hands separately, singing along. Voice leads.
5 min — Play "Mary Had a Little Lamb" by ear, singing simultaneously. If the singing stops, the hands stop.
5 min — Note name flash: write all 7 note names on paper, shuffle, point to each and play the note on the keyboard without hesitation.
03
Legato, dynamics, and the grand staff
Connected touch is taught as a physical sensation. Reading begins — five notes only.
Phase A
Teacher's framing for this week

Legato is the default articulation of the piano. Before staccato, before accent, before any other touch — the student must own connected sound. This week the physical sensation of legato should become habitual: finger-to-finger transfer, no gap, no blur. Notation is introduced only as a label for something the student already knows by ear.

Session 5 — True legato and piano / forte
First 20 min Legato — no gap, no blur
  • Sound Define legato physically: "The outgoing note releases the instant the incoming finger makes contact — not before, not after." Demonstrate on C–D–E with teacher playing one hand, student placing their hand on top to feel the motion. 6 min
  • Sound Legato C–D–E–F–G ascending and descending, right hand. Pause after each note to confirm legato connection. Teacher listens for: no audible gap between notes, no double-sounding from a blurred change. 8 min
  • Ear Sing the five-note scale while playing. Test: if the student closes their eyes and hears gaps, there are gaps. The ear is the judge — not the teacher, not the eye. 6 min
Next 15 min Piano and forte — weight controls volume
  • Sound Same technique (arm weight), different speed. Slow arm drop = soft sound. Faster drop = louder sound. Demonstrate 3 levels on middle C — no change in tension, only speed of approach. 5 min
  • Ear Character game: teacher asks "play C–D–E as if it's a lullaby" vs. "play it as if it's a fanfare." Student chooses the dynamic — teacher listens. No right answer — musical instinct is what's being built. 5 min
  • Repertoire Apply to "Mary Had a Little Lamb": play it once softly, once loudly. Then play it making a dynamic shape — what does the student think feels most musical? Commit to it. 5 min
Session 6 — The grand staff introduced
First 15 min Staff = keyboard picture
  • Keys Show the grand staff as a vertical picture of the keyboard: "Higher on the staff = higher on the keyboard." Middle C on a ledger line between treble and bass. Student traces the connection: note on page → key on keyboard. 5 min
  • Keys Introduce treble clef: C, D, E, F, G — learn their positions on the staff (spaces and lines). Use landmark notes only: middle C (ledger line below), E (first line), G (second line). Point, name, play. 5 min
  • Keys Bass clef landmarks: middle C (ledger line above), B (third line from top), G (second line from bottom). Student points to each on the page, names it, finds it on the keyboard. 5 min
Next 20 min First written piece
  • Ear Before opening the music: teacher plays the first piece from the method book. Student listens and sings along. They must know what it sounds like before reading it. 3 min
  • Keys Open the music. Student reads each note aloud (naming it) before playing. First pass is a reading exercise, not a performance. Teacher may prompt: "What note is that?" not "Play it." 10 min
  • Repertoire Second pass: play with legato and a single chosen dynamic. Sing along. The transition from reading to playing should feel like recognising an old friend, not decoding a new puzzle. 7 min
Watch for — Week 3
  • Reading without hearing — student plays written notes correctly but produces no musical shape; address with singing before every phrase
  • Legato gaps on the 4th and 5th fingers — these weaker fingers release early; teacher should listen closely and ask "did you hear a space there?"
  • Forcing louder dynamics by pressing harder — remind that louder = faster arm approach, same relaxed weight
Home practice — Week 3 (15 min/day)
5 min — Legato C–G–C (both hands separately), singing. Judge quality by ear: "Would I want to listen to this?"
5 min — First written piece: read notes aloud, then play with legato and a dynamic choice.
5 min — Staff reading: teacher writes 5 random notes on manuscript paper for student to name and play at home.
04
Rhythm, pulse, and two-hand coordination begins
Basic note values introduced. Alternating-hand pieces. The physical sense of pulse is established.
Phase A
Teacher's framing for this week

Rhythm is not taught from notation in this method — it is felt first, then named. Begin this week by having the student walk a steady pulse while clapping a pattern. The body must own the beat before the page does. Two-hand coordination is introduced through alternating (not simultaneous) playing — hands do not yet play together.

Session 7 — Pulse, quarter, and half notes
First 15 min Pulse as a physical reality
  • Ear Walk the room to a steady clap from the teacher. Then reverse: student claps while teacher walks. The pulse belongs to everyone — it is not inside the music, it is the music. 4 min
  • Ear At the keyboard: tap the pulse on the fallboard (closed key cover) while teacher plays a piece. Student must maintain pulse even when the music pauses — "the pulse never stops, even in silence." 4 min
  • Keys Introduce quarter note = one pulse. Half note = two pulses. Student claps: one clap for quarter, hold for half. Then play on middle C: quarter, quarter, half. Listen for the held note ringing for two full counts. 7 min
Next 20 min First rhythmic piece
  • Repertoire Introduce a simple 4-bar piece using C, D, E in quarter and half notes, right hand. Student sings the rhythm ("ta ta ta-a") before touching the keyboard. This naming of rhythm is not solfège — it's rhythmic syllables. 8 min
  • Sound Play the piece: right hand, legato, steady pulse. Teacher taps the pulse throughout. If the student loses the pulse, stop and re-establish it — do not "catch up" by speeding. 7 min
  • Body Body internalization: student taps foot (or nods head) to the pulse while playing. "The pulse lives in your body, not in your hands." 5 min
Session 8 — Alternating hands, left hand on the staff
First 15 min Left hand reading — bass clef C position
  • Keys Review bass clef landmarks: middle C (ledger line above staff), B, A, G, F in the bass clef C position (LH 5th finger on C below middle C). Read and play each note. 7 min
  • Sound Left hand five-note scale, C to G, legato, with singing. Note that the left hand tends to produce heavier tone — the student should calibrate to match the right hand's quality, not just match the notes. 8 min
Next 20 min Alternating hand piece
  • Repertoire Introduce an alternating-hand piece: right hand plays bars 1–2, left hand plays bars 3–4, repeating. No simultaneous notes yet. The handoff itself must be smooth — no pulse interruption at the change. 10 min
  • Ear Sing the entire piece through both hands — the melody does not stop when the hand changes. The student's voice creates continuity that the hands then follow. 5 min
  • Sound Dynamic assignment: right hand = piano, left hand = forte. Then reverse. Notice how character changes. "The notes are the same — only the weight changes. But it's a completely different piece." 5 min
Watch for — Week 4
  • Rushing at the hand changeover — the incoming hand often arrives early; make the student freeze at the bar line and breathe before the change
  • Ignoring held note duration — a half note must be allowed to ring for two full counts; students often lift early
  • Left hand sounding thumpy or uncontrolled — the LH naturally drops with more weight; require parity of tone quality between hands
Home practice — Week 4 (15 min/day)
5 min — Rhythmic piece: right hand alone, singing rhythm syllables. Foot taps the pulse throughout.
5 min — Alternating-hand piece: read aloud first (name every note), then play with legato and a dynamic shape.
5 min — Staff reading: identify 8 random notes written by teacher, name them, play them, sing them.
05
Staccato, phrasing, and intervals by ear
A second touch introduced. Musical sentences begin. Ear training moves from pitch to interval recognition.
Phase B
Teacher's framing for this week

Phase B begins. The student now has a foundation — body position, weight drop, legato, basic reading, and a feel for pulse. The work becomes more musical. Staccato is introduced not as a technical skill but as a character choice: some musical ideas need to breathe between notes. Intervals by ear are the ear-training leap that pays off enormously in Stage 2.

Session 9 — Staccato touch and musical character
First 15 min Staccato — released weight
  • Sound Staccato is arm weight quickly released — not a jab. "Drop and bounce." The hand drops onto the key with arm weight and immediately rebounds. The sound is clean, not scratchy. Demonstrate on middle C, student imitates. 5 min
  • Sound C–D–E staccato, right hand. Compare to legato: teacher plays each — "which sounds like raindrops? Which sounds like a river?" Student assigns character, then plays their own version. 5 min
  • Repertoire Mixed articulation exercise: play C quarter legato, D quarter legato, E half staccato. Listen — the half-note staccato should still feel like it had space to breathe, not like it was cut off by tension. 5 min
Next 20 min Musical phrases — question and answer
  • Ear Teacher plays two 2-bar phrases: first ends on a note that feels unfinished (an "away" note), second ends on C (home). Student identifies: "which felt like a question? which like an answer?" This is the foundation of phrase understanding. 6 min
  • Repertoire Introduce a piece with clear question-answer phrasing. Student labels the phrases before playing. Play the "question" phrase with a slight lean forward; the "answer" phrase with settling back and slowing slightly at the end. 8 min
  • Ear Sing only — not play. Sing the piece with the student, shaping the phrase in the voice: lean into the question, resolve the answer. Then play it the same way. 6 min
Session 10 — Steps and skips by ear
First 15 min Intervals — step vs. skip
  • Ear Teacher plays pairs of notes without showing the keyboard. Student answers: "step" (adjacent note) or "skip" (one note apart). 10 examples. Start wide (easy skips) then narrow to close calls. 6 min
  • Ear Add direction: "step up, skip down, step up." Teacher plays, student describes. This trains the ear-to-spatial-thinking connection that is the prerequisite for sight-reading. 5 min
  • Keys On the staff: point to a note, move up a step (next line or space), identify the new note. Then a skip. Student names each landing note. Staff geography and ear training merge. 4 min
Next 20 min Piece with steps and skips — reading
  • Repertoire New piece using both steps and skips (e.g. a piece spanning the full C position including skips from C to E, or from D to G). Student analyses before playing: label each interval movement above the staff. 8 min
  • Ear Sing the piece using interval names: "step up, step up, skip down, step up." Then play it. The analysis should make the playing effortless by comparison. 7 min
  • Sound Apply staccato to the skips, legato to the steps — as a musical experiment. "What story does this tell?" The student answers and commits to a performance. 5 min
Watch for — Week 5
  • Staccato produced by jabbing with the finger — remind: "drop and bounce," not "poke." The arm initiates, not the finger.
  • Phrases played as an even series of notes with no shape — stop and ask "where is the arrival point of this phrase?" Phrase shaping requires a destination.
  • Skips misread on the staff — students commonly read a skip as a step when tired; reinforce: "a skip always lands on the same type — line to line, space to space."
Home practice — Week 5 (15–20 min/day)
5 min — Staccato exercise: C–G staccato, both hands separately, listening for "clean bounce" tone.
7 min — New piece: read intervals aloud (step/skip + direction) before playing. Then play with a clear phrase shape.
5 min — Listen to a short melody (teacher chooses or student picks). Transcribe as "step up, step down, skip up" etc. in writing.
06
Simultaneous hands, sustain pedal introduced
Hands play together for the first time. The sustain pedal is learned by ear, never by rule.
Phase B
Teacher's framing for this week

Playing both hands together for the first time is the most psychologically significant event of Stage 1. Expect regression in tone quality, posture, and rhythm — coordination demand spikes, and previously automatic skills require conscious attention again. This is normal. Do not rush through it. The pedal is introduced as a listening experiment, not a technique rule.

Session 11 — Hands together, first pieces
First 20 min Layered preparation for HT
  • Body Inner singing exercise: student plays the right hand while singing the left hand's notes aloud. Then swap: play the left hand while singing the right. This is the most effective pre-HT coordination drill known. 7 min
  • Repertoire Choose a simple HT piece where one hand holds a single repeated note while the other plays a melody. This minimises coordination demand — one hand has a fixed task, one has creative space. 8 min
  • Sound Play HT at half the intended tempo. Teacher counts aloud. Stop at any point where hands lose synchrony — do not "correct on the fly." Reset, breathe, and restart the phrase. 5 min
Next 15 min Sustain pedal by ear
  • Ear Teacher plays a simple phrase with and without pedal. Student listens: "which sounds like a room, which sounds like outdoors?" The pedal creates resonance — it is a sound tool, not a "smoother." 4 min
  • Sound Student tries: play a C major chord slowly (C–E–G one at a time) without pedal. Then with pedal. Listen to the accumulation of sound. Press the pedal before the first note — release only when changing harmony. 6 min
  • Ear Muddy pedal experiment: play C then D with held pedal. "Does that sound beautiful or blurred?" The student's own ear gives the rule: change the pedal when the sound blurs. No other rule is needed yet. 5 min
Session 12 — First complete HT piece with expression
Full session Build and perform a complete piece
  • Repertoire Work on a complete HT piece (2 pages, both hands). Process: LH alone → RH alone → sing LH while playing RH → hands together at slow tempo → add a single expressive choice (one dynamic shape). 15 min
  • Ear Perform for the teacher: one full run-through with both hands. No stopping. Teacher listens only — does not correct mid-performance. This is the student's first experience of performing intention over correction. 8 min
  • Sound Feedback: teacher names one thing that worked musically and one specific technical target for home practice. Positive specificity + single correction. Not a list. 7 min
Watch for — Week 6
  • Posture collapsing when hands play together — HT demand diverts attention from physical awareness; periodically check shoulders and wrist alignment
  • Pedal held through harmonic changes causing blur — trust the ear rule entirely; no need for theory yet
  • Student becoming self-critical during performance — teach resilience: "if something goes wrong, keep going. Stopping is the only real mistake in a performance."
Home practice — Week 6 (20 min/day)
5 min — Singing exercise: RH plays while LH notes are sung. Then swap.
10 min — HT piece: slow tempo practice. One phrase at a time. Stop and reset if hands lose sync — do not correct on the fly.
5 min — Pedal experiment: student chooses any piece from weeks 1–4 and experiments with adding pedal. Judged by their own ear.
07
Consolidation, repertoire review, and interpretation
Everything learned is deepened. Three pieces in the repertoire. Each is interpreted, not just played.
Phase B
Teacher's framing for this week

Week 7 is intentionally not a week of new material. It is a deepening week. The student now has enough under their fingers to focus entirely on what they are saying musically. Introduce the concept of a "programme" — three pieces that the student can perform with intention. This is the preparation for the gateway, but more importantly, it is their first experience of owning a repertoire.

Session 13 — Interpretation and performing intention
First 20 min Interpreting three pieces
  • Repertoire Student selects their three best pieces from weeks 1–6. For each: what is this piece about? Give it a title (if it doesn't have one), a story, or a mood. The interpretation is the student's — teacher does not prescribe. 5 min
  • Ear For each piece: sing it first with the chosen character in the voice. Then play it. Does the playing match the singing? If not, what needs to change: dynamic, tempo, articulation? 10 min
  • Sound Perform all three pieces in sequence for the teacher. One breath between pieces. Teacher listens for: does each piece sound different? Does the student's playing reflect their stated intention? 5 min
Next 15 min Technical refinement — targeted
  • Body Full posture check: bench height, hand shape, wrist level, shoulder release. After 7 weeks, habits have formed — good and bad. Address the single most impactful correction per student. 5 min
  • Sound Tone refinement: play any piece very slowly, listening to every individual note. "Would I want to hear that note if it were the only note in the room?" Each note is an event, not a step on a path. 5 min
  • Ear Interval dictation: teacher plays 5 two-note intervals, student writes "step up," "skip down" etc. in a notebook. Grade: 4/5 is pass. These notes go home for review. 5 min
Session 14 — Gateway preparation
Full session Mock gateway run
  • Sound Gateway check 1 — tone: student plays a single note with arm weight. Teacher listens without comment, then asks "what did you hear?" Self-assessment before external feedback. 5 min
  • Ear Gateway check 2 — ear: 5 interval pairs (step/skip), 3 note-naming exercises on the staff, 1 pitch-matching exercise (sing back a note). 7 min
  • Keys Gateway check 3 — geography: teacher names 5 notes rapidly, student finds each in any octave within 2 seconds. Teacher points to 3 notes on staff, student names and plays. 5 min
  • Repertoire Gateway check 4 — performance: student plays their best piece from the repertoire. Teacher gives written feedback: one musical strength, one technical target, pass/hold decision for week 8. 13 min
Watch for — Week 7
  • Student playing pieces mechanically by week 7 — if the interpretation has been "learned" as a habit, the playing becomes routine; ask them to change one thing (dynamic, tempo) and play it again
  • Performance anxiety in the mock gateway — normalise it; frame the gateway as a conversation, not a test
  • Gaps in any of the four pillars now need targeted home practice before week 8; assign specific exercises, not just "practice more"
Home practice — Week 7 (20 min/day)
10 min — Three repertoire pieces in sequence, each with full intention. No stopping to correct — perform.
5 min — Arm-weight drop: return to the very first exercise. Has the tone quality grown? Compare to week 1 in memory.
5 min — Interval ear training: listen to any melody and transcribe as steps/skips. Self-check by playing it back.
08
Final polish and the Stage 1 Gateway
The last session is a celebration and formal assessment. The gateway is a diagnostic conversation, not an exam.
Phase B
Teacher's framing for this week

Session 15 is the final polish session. Session 16 is the gateway. Frame the gateway to the student as this: "I'm going to listen to you the way someone who loves music listens — for what moves me, not for what's wrong." A student who produces one moment of genuine musical intention in the gateway has passed its spirit, whatever the technical results show.

Session 15 — Polish and readiness
Full session Refinement across all four pillars
  • Body Full slow-motion posture check. Student demonstrates the arm-weight drop at three dynamic levels. Teacher verifies: same relaxed technique, only speed varies. No tension visible in shoulder, forearm, or wrist. 6 min
  • Ear Ear review: pitch matching (5 notes), interval identification (5 pairs), contour of a new 4-note melody heard once. This is not the gateway — it is preparation. No pressure. 7 min
  • Repertoire Play the gateway piece twice: once for technical focus (listen for every legato connection, every dynamic), once for musical intention (forget the technique, tell the story). Teacher compares the two: usually, the second is better. 12 min
Session 16 — The Stage 1 Gateway
Stage 1 Gateway Assessment
Four components. Diagnostic, not punitive. A student who does not pass one pillar may begin Stage 2 work in the passing pillars while continuing Stage 1 targeted work in the weak one.
01
Tone quality
Student plays a single note in the middle octave, as beautifully as possible. Teacher listens for arm-weight initiation, resonance, and absence of tension. Student is then asked: "What did you hear in that tone?" Self-awareness is part of the pass criterion. Pass: a tone you would want to hear more of, produced with visible physical relaxation.
02
Ear — interval and pitch
Teacher plays 5 two-note intervals; student names each as step or skip with direction (e.g. "skip up"). Teacher sings a 3-note melody; student sings it back, then finds it on the keyboard. Pass: 4/5 intervals correct; melody sung back accurately and located on keyboard without hesitation.
03
Keyboard geography and staff reading
Teacher calls 5 note names; student finds each anywhere on the keyboard within 2 seconds. Teacher points to 5 notes on the grand staff (3 treble, 2 bass); student names each and plays it. Pass: 5/5 keyboard locations correct; 4/5 staff notes correctly named and played.
04
Repertoire — musical intention
Student performs their chosen piece from the stage 1 repertoire. One complete performance, no stopping. Teacher listens for: legato connection, pulse stability, at least one clear dynamic shape, and — most critically — whether the playing reflects a musical intention. Pass: the performance makes the teacher want to hear the next piece.

After the gateway — what next

A student who passes all four components moves fully to Stage 2: Coordination and Reading. A student with a strong pillar gap (e.g. ear training consistently weak) continues targeted ear work from Stage 1 while beginning the non-ear components of Stage 2. The goal is never to hold a student back across the board for one weakness. Progress is pillar-by-pillar, always moving forward where the foundation is solid.
