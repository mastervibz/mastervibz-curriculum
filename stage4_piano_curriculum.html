Octave technique — weight, rotation, and the wrist as shock absorber
Octaves are the most physically demanding technique in piano. They require lateral forearm rotation, wrist flexibility, and arm weight — never tension. The three common injuries from octave playing and how to prevent all of them.
Phase A
Why octaves require a completely new physical approach

Octave playing fails for one reason: the student grips. The hand spans an octave at maximum stretch, and the instinctive response to that stretch is tension — the forearm muscles lock, the wrist stiffens, and the fingers clamp onto the keys. This produces harsh tone, rapid fatigue, and — over time — injury. The correct octave technique involves the opposite: a relaxed span, forearm rotation to activate alternating finger pairs, and a wrist that acts as a flexible shock absorber rather than a rigid connector.

The three most common octave injuries are: forearm extensor tendinitis (from excessive wrist lowering), ulnar nerve compression (from a twisted arm position), and thumb joint strain (from gripping). All three are preventable by the technique described in this week's sessions. The student must understand that octave playing is an arm skill, not a hand skill.

Session 1 — The octave span: forearm rotation and the wrist-float
First 25 minBuilding the octave from physical principles
  • BodyForearm rotation anatomy: the forearm rotates around its own axis (the radius and ulna cross). In octave playing, a slight outward rotation brings the thumb into position; a slight inward rotation brings the 5th finger. The thumb and 5th finger do not both press simultaneously with equal weight — they alternate through rotation. Demonstrate this rotation with the arm hanging loose from the elbow before touching the keyboard.6 min
  • BodyThe wrist-float test: place the hand in octave span on C and the C an octave above. Depress both keys using only forearm rotation and arm weight — the wrist should be slightly raised and completely loose, capable of being gently pushed from side to side by the teacher without resistance. If the wrist is rigid, tension is present. The wrist floats; the arm drives.8 min
  • SoundSingle octave with full arm weight: drop the arm onto a single octave (C to C) from a height of about 5cm above the keys. Both notes should sound simultaneously and with identical weight. The sound should be full and resonant — not tight or brittle. If the sound is tight, the hand is gripping. Release the span between repetitions completely — spread the fingers, shake loose, re-form the span.8 min
  • EarSound comparison: gripped octave vs. rotation octave. Teacher demonstrates both. "The gripped octave sounds like someone shouting. The rotation octave sounds like someone projecting." Student identifies which the teacher is using in 10 rapid alternations. The ear learns the quality difference before the hand can produce it — which is the correct order.3 min
Next 20 minFirst octave passage — slow and weighted
  • AdvancedOctave scale: C major, one octave, RH only. Each octave: rotation into position, arm weight drop, wrist floats on release. At ♩=40. This is very slow — intentionally. The physical pattern must be correct before any tempo is added. An incorrect octave at any speed is damage accumulation.8 min
  • AdvancedBlocked octaves (both notes simultaneously) vs. broken octaves (thumb first, then 5th finger through rotation): practice both. The blocked octave confirms the span; the broken octave builds the rotation mechanism. Alternate: four blocked, four broken, four blocked. Hear the rotation in the broken octaves — it should feel like a gentle rocking motion in the forearm.7 min
  • RepertoireIdentify an octave passage in a piece the student will encounter in Phase D: play it at 30% of target tempo. "This is what we are building toward. Right now the technique is being planted. By week 7 it will be available at musical tempos." The future goal makes the present slow work meaningful.5 min
Session 2 — Moving octaves: lateral arm travel
First 20 minOctave passages — the arm leads the hand
  • BodyThe lateral arm travel principle for octaves is the same as for arpeggios (Stage 3 Phase A): the arm moves to the next position before the hand needs to be there. In octave passages, the arm "previews" the next octave position — it arrives slightly early. The hand is a passenger. Drill this with a three-octave passage: arm travels, hand follows, wrist floats throughout.8 min
  • SoundEven octave passage: RH, C major scale in octaves, two octaves ascending and descending. ♩=52. Teacher closes eyes and listens for: (1) any tightness of tone, (2) any accent louder than its neighbours, (3) any wrist rigidity (visible, not audible). These three checks are the week-by-week progress measure.7 min
  • AdvancedLH octaves: introduce left-hand octave technique separately. The LH typically has a wider natural span, making the octave physically easier — but the rotational principle is identical. Particular attention to the LH 5th finger (often weaker) carrying its octave note with full weight. "The 5th finger is not a passenger — it drives its note with the same authority as the thumb."5 min
Next 25 minStaccato octaves and ff octave power
  • AdvancedStaccato octaves (the Beethoven technique): the most demanding octave type. The arm drops and immediately rebounds — the wrist springs upward rather than remaining sustained. This rebound technique allows fast, powerful repeated octaves (as in Beethoven's Fifth Symphony, or the "Waldstein" Sonata left hand). Practice the rebound: drop, spring up, drop, spring up. The wrist is the spring.10 min
  • Soundff octave power: the ff octave is produced by arm weight and velocity, never by forearm muscle tension. The student must feel that the power comes from above — from the shoulder and upper arm — not from the forearm squeezing. At the loudest octave, the forearm should still be releasable by the teacher without resistance. "Powerful arms, loose forearms."8 min
  • EarCharacter test: play the same 4-octave passage (1) softly, as a nocturne melody; (2) powerfully, as a heroic declaration; (3) lightly, as a dance. The technique adapts — same rotational principle, different arm weight and velocity. Student identifies which physical adjustment produces which character. The octave is a musical tool, not just a technical challenge.7 min
Watch for — Week 1
  • Any forearm tension during octave playing — stop immediately if the forearm feels hard to the touch; this is the first sign of injury-path technique; shake loose and restart at a slower tempo with zero span tension
  • Thumb playing flat (nail-side down) in octaves — the thumb must still play on its outer edge even in octave position; a flat thumb causes wrist torque that compounds at speed
  • Student rushing to reach performance tempo within this week — the physical pattern must be established at slow tempo for a minimum of two weeks before any tempo increase
  • Wrist dropping below key level during octave passages — the wrist must remain level or slightly raised; wrist drop locks the joint and removes the shock-absorber function

10 min — Octave rotation drill: blocked octaves on every white key, C to C, both hands separately. Shake loose between each octave. Listen for tone quality.
5 min — Staccato octave rebound: single octave, drop and spring, 20 repetitions each hand. The rebound must be effortless — if it feels laboured, the wrist is still bracing.
10 min — Stage 3 repertoire maintenance: one complete performance of a Stage 3 piece. "Keep what you have while building what's next."
12 min — Scales and arpeggios (maintain Stage 3 targets). 8 min — Sight-reading (daily).
02
Double thirds, double sixths, and parallel movement
The double-note techniques that define the Romantic and Classical bravura style. Voice-leading within the double notes. The thumb-glissando on black keys.
Phase A
Why double notes matter beyond technique

Double thirds and sixths are not merely technical displays — they are a way of creating two-voice harmony within a single hand. When the right hand plays thirds, the upper voice and lower voice each carry melodic lines that interact harmonically. A student who understands this plays double thirds completely differently from one who sees them as a finger exercise: the upper voice sings as the melody, the lower voice as the inner harmony, and the balance between them shifts according to which voice is more important at each moment. This musical understanding is the goal — the technique is its physical vehicle.

Sessions 3–4Double thirds — the C major third scale
  • AdvancedC major third scale, RH: C+E, D+F, E+G, F+A, G+B, A+C, B+D, C+E. The fingering is not a single pattern — it alternates between 1-3 and 1-2 groupings. The key principle: both notes of a third must drop simultaneously with equal weight. No leading note. Practice each pair as a blocked interval first (100 repetitions, very slow) before moving to the ascending passage.12 min
  • EarVoice balance in thirds: play the C major third scale with the upper voice (fingers 3 and 4) slightly louder than the lower voice (thumb and finger 2). The upper voice is the melody. Then reverse — lower voice dominant. Hear how the character completely changes. "The same notes — two completely different pieces. The balance is the interpretation."8 min
  • AdvancedDouble thirds chromatic: adjacent half-step motion in thirds, same fingering principle. This is the most demanding fingering challenge in the standard double-note repertoire (Chopin Op. 25 No. 6). Begin very slowly — the chromatic third scale builds the finger independence and subtle rotation needed for all double-note playing.10 min
  • RepertoirePiece with prominent thirds: a Schubert Impromptu passage, a Mendelssohn Song Without Words (Op. 19 No. 1 "Sweet Remembrance" has thirds throughout). Apply voice-balance understanding — which voice carries the melody at each moment? Mark in the score before playing.10 min
Double sixthsThe wider span and the rotation difference
  • AdvancedDouble sixths require a larger arm rotation than thirds — the span between thumb and 5th finger (or 4th finger) is wider. The wrist must stay slightly more elevated to allow the arm to rotate into position without strain. C major sixth scale: C+A, D+B, E+C, F+D, G+E, A+F, B+G, C+A. Start with each pair blocked, same process as thirds.10 min
  • SoundSixth sound quality: the sixth interval sounds warmer and more consonant than the third — it is the inversion of the third. Playing a passage first in thirds then in sixths reveals how the harmonic colour changes. "Thirds are crisper; sixths are rounder." This aural distinction helps the student calibrate the physical approach — sixths need slightly more arm weight to match the warmth of the interval.8 min
  • ArtChopin and Brahms are the composers who most exploit double sixths for expressive richness (Brahms Intermezzo Op. 118, Chopin Nocturne Op. 9 No. 2). Play a simplified passage from one of these and identify: where does the lower voice of the sixth carry more expressive weight? Where does the upper voice? The double sixth is a conversation between two voices — it should sound like one.7 min
Watch for — Week 2
  • One note of a double-note interval consistently louder than the other — test by asking the teacher to close eyes and identify the louder note; adjust by reducing the louder finger's arm component
  • Double thirds played as two separate notes in quick succession (asynchronous) — the simultaneous drop test: raise the hand, let both fingers fall freely; if the notes sound apart, the fingers are landing at different times and the arm is not acting as a single unit
  • Wrist dropping during double sixths (wider span) — the wider the interval, the more the wrist must be kept elevated; watch for wrist dipping on the 5th finger side
Home practice — Week 2 (45 min/day)
8 min — C major third scale, RH: blocked pairs first (50×), then passage at ♩=40. Upper voice dominant, then lower voice dominant.
8 min — C major sixth scale, same process. 5 min — Octave maintenance from week 1 (rotation drill).
12 min — Repertoire piece with thirds or sixths. 7 min — Sight-reading. 5 min — Scales.
03
Trills at speed — the forearm trill and sustained-note trills
The trill as a forearm rotation exercise. Trills while holding other notes. The long trill with termination. Beethoven's and Chopin's different trill languages.
Phase A
Sessions 5–6The forearm trill and sustained-note independence
  • AdvancedForearm rotation trill: the most efficient trill mechanism for sustained, fast trills. The forearm rocks rapidly around its axis (the radius-ulna joint), driving fingers 2 and 3 (or 3 and 4) alternately. The finger joints themselves do not flex — they are relatively passive. This is the opposite of what most students instinctively do (wiggling the fingers). Build the rotation slowly: rock the forearm 2× per second, then 4×, then 8×, gradually increasing until the alternation becomes a trill.10 min
  • AdvancedSustained-note trill: in Beethoven's late works (Op. 109, Op. 111) and in many Classical concertos, a long trill must be sustained in one part of the hand while other fingers play an independent melody above or below. Practice: finger 4 trills on E-F# while fingers 1 and 2 hold C and D sustained. The trill hand must not sympathetically engage the held fingers — each pair of fingers operates independently. This is one of the most demanding coordination tests in piano.12 min
  • AdvancedTrill termination: every trill must end with a deliberate turn (the note above the trill, the trill note, and the note below — three quick notes) that leads cleanly into the resolution. Practice the termination in isolation — it is the moment most trills fail. The turn must be even, in tempo, and connected to the phrase cadence it is preparing.8 min
  • RepertoireTwo trill styles: (1) a Beethoven Bagatelle or Sonata with a sustained, powerful trill (the trill as a climactic held tension); (2) a Chopin Nocturne or Waltz with a decorative, shimmering trill (the trill as a vocal ornament). The physical production differs in character — the Beethoven trill has more arm weight behind it; the Chopin trill is lighter and more even. Hear the difference before playing.10 min
TheoryThe harmonic function of the trill
  • TheoryA trill is always on a harmonically significant note — typically the dominant, leading to resolution. The trill creates sustained harmonic tension that the cadence then releases. This is why a trill before a cadence is one of the most powerful rhetorical devices in tonal music: it holds the tension while the listener waits for resolution. Understanding this changes how the trill is shaped — the tension should be audible in the intensity of the trill, and the resolution should be a genuine release, not just a cessation.8 min
  • EarListen to two recordings of a cadential trill (the first movement cadenza of a Classical concerto, or the recapitulation of a Beethoven sonata): one pianist who plays the trill as a duration to survive, one who plays it as a sustained argument building to the resolution. Student identifies which performance communicates the harmonic purpose of the trill. "The trill is not waiting — it is insisting."7 min
Home practice — Week 3 (45 min/day)
8 min — Forearm rotation trill: build from 2 to 16 alternations per second. Each pair of fingers (2-3, 3-4, 4-5). Both hands.
8 min — Sustained-note trill: trill on fingers 3-4 while fingers 1-2 hold two notes. 3 minutes each hand. Confirm that held notes do not sympathetically press.
12 min — Trill repertoire pieces (both styles). 7 min — Sight-reading. 10 min — Octaves and double notes maintenance.
04
Rapid passages — velocity without tension
Fast scale and arpeggio passages up to ♩=160. The velocity ceiling and how to raise it. Even fast passages are produced by arm weight, not by finger speed.
Phase A
The velocity principle

Speed is not produced by moving the fingers faster. It is produced by reducing the physical weight on each key — which allows the rebound to be faster — while keeping the arm relaxed and the lateral travel smooth. A fast passage played with tension gets slower (the muscles fatigue and the rebounds become sluggish). A fast passage played with minimal weight per note and maximum arm freedom gets faster as the session continues (the relaxation compounds). The prescription for velocity is always: less weight per note, more fluid arm travel, better timing — never "try harder."

Sessions 7–8Velocity building — the relaxation method
  • AdvancedThe burst method: rather than playing a scale slowly and gradually speeding up, play a very short burst (4 notes) at the target fast tempo with complete arm relaxation, then immediately stop and rest. Alternate bursts and rests. "Fast-rest-fast-rest." The brain patterns the correct fast motion without the fatigue that ruins it. Begin with 4-note groups, expand to 8, then 12, then 16, then full passages.10 min
  • AdvancedThe rhythmic grouping method: play a 16-note passage in groups of 4, with a tiny accent and pause between groups. Then groups of 3. Then groups of 2. Each grouping trains different finger-pair relationships. When the accents and pauses are removed, the evenness that remains is genuine — not imposed by a metronome but built from independent sub-group control.10 min
  • SoundThe ceiling test: play a scale passage at maximum comfortable speed. Note the tempo. Rest 30 seconds. Now play the same passage at that tempo deliberately with 20% less finger pressure — slightly lighter touch per key. It will feel insecure at first. Listen: is the sound cleaner or dirtier? Usually cleaner. "Lightness enables speed. Pressure defeats it."8 min
  • RepertoireApply to a specific fast passage in the current repertoire: choose the 4-note burst that causes the most difficulty. Apply the burst method for 8 repetitions. Then attempt the full passage. The improvement within a single 8-minute session should be measurable — the burst method is fast-acting when done correctly.12 min
Arpeggios at speedThe fast arpeggio — arm lead and finger preparation
  • AdvancedFast arpeggio principle: at high speed, the arm must begin traveling to the next octave position before the thumb crossing occurs. The thumb is "pre-landing" — it is already positioned above the next target key before the previous note has finished sounding. This requires absolute arm relaxation and spatial awareness. Drill: play an arpeggio at ♩=60, with exaggerated early arm travel (the arm moves almost lazily ahead of the hand). Then reduce the anticipation until it is invisible but still present.15 min
  • EarFast arpeggio sound quality: at performance tempo, a well-played fast arpeggio sounds like a single unbroken gesture — a sweep of sound, not a series of individual notes. Listening test: teacher plays a fast arpeggio, student identifies whether it sounds like "notes" or "sweep." If "notes," the arm travel is lagging. If "sweep," the arm is leading and the technique is correct.10 min
Watch for — Week 4
  • Student using the metronome as a velocity target (playing to the click) rather than as a check — the metronome measures evenness but does not create it; the burst method builds evenness internally, then the metronome confirms it
  • Velocity built in one session and lost in the next — this means the relaxation technique is not yet habitual; the student is re-tensing in each new practice session; remind: the first two repetitions of any fast passage should always be slower than the student's "comfortable" tempo, to re-establish the relaxed physical state
  • Fast passages with audibly uneven notes — this is a finger independence issue; apply the rhythmic grouping method to the specific pair of fingers that is uneven
Home practice — Week 4 (45 min/day)
10 min — Burst method: one difficult scale passage, 8 repetitions (4-note burst, rest, expand). Apply to C major and D major at ♩=144.
8 min — Fast arpeggio practice: two keys, two octaves, exaggerated arm-lead technique. 5 min — Octave and double-note maintenance.
12 min — Repertoire fast passage work. 10 min — Sight-reading.

Polyrhythm — 2 against 3, 3 against 4, and the internal conductor
Polyrhythm as a pulse subdivision problem, not a counting problem. The internal conductor method. 2:3 mastered, 3:4 introduced. Chopin's characteristic use of polyrhythm.
Phase A
Why polyrhythm must not be solved by counting

The standard method for learning 2 against 3 is to find a common pulse (6 subdivisions) and map both rhythms onto it: "1-tri-plet-2-and" or "not difficult, not difficult." This works as a learning tool but produces mechanical, laboured polyrhythm in performance — the student is still counting internally, and the musical line is broken by the counting mechanism. The correct approach is the internal conductor method: each hand follows its own conductor (its own internal pulse) completely independently, and the two pulses coexist without either being subordinated to the other. This is how professional pianists actually produce polyrhythm — not by calculating but by holding two simultaneous streams of time.

Sessions 9–10The internal conductor method
  • CoordStep 1 — own conductor: student taps the RH rhythm on the knee while walking the LH rhythm in foot steps. Separate body parts, separate pulses. The body learns to hold two time streams before the piano demands it. Do this for 2:3 first: RH taps twice (evenly) while feet take three steps (evenly). The moment both become natural simultaneously, the coordination is in the body.8 min
  • CoordStep 2 — at the piano: LH plays three equal notes (C–E–G, repeated) while RH holds a whole note and feels the two pulses of the 2-beat grouping over the LH's three notes. Then switch: RH plays two notes while LH holds. Each hand knows only its own pulse. Neither listens to the other — they simply coexist.10 min
  • CoordStep 3 — combine with melody: LH plays a three-note repeated pattern as an accompaniment while RH plays a simple melody in 2. The melody is in free time relative to the accompaniment — it follows its own phrase shape, which happens to be in binary meter. This is exactly what Chopin does in many Nocturnes (melody in 4, accompaniment triplets in 12/8).10 min
  • RepertoireChopin Nocturne Op. 9 No. 2: the melody is in 3/4 with duplets while the LH has triplets throughout. This creates a constant soft polyrhythm. Play the LH alone as a complete musical statement. Then RH alone. Then combine — the LH continues its triplet flow, the RH sings its binary melody. The two pulses coexist without either being corrected to match the other.12 min
3 against 4The most complex standard polyrhythm
  • Coord3:4 body introduction: walk 4 steps while clapping 3. This is harder than 2:3 because the coincidence points are further apart (only the start and end of each group). Spend the full session on the body exercise before attempting 3:4 at the piano. "The body must own this before the piano can have it."10 min
  • RepertoireBrahms Intermezzo Op. 118 No. 2 (simplified passage) or Brahms Waltz Op. 39 No. 15: Brahms writes 3:4 as a characteristic texture. The 4-beat melody over a 3-beat accompaniment (or vice versa) creates the characteristic Brahmsian "two tempos at once" feeling. Listen to a recording and identify which voice has 3 and which has 4. "Once you hear it, you cannot unhear it."10 min
  • EarPolyrhythm as expression: polyrhythm is not a mathematical curiosity — it creates a sensation of floating, of time being stretched, of two emotional states coexisting. The Chopin melody that seems to drift above the accompaniment is producing this sensation through 2:3. The student who understands this plays polyrhythm expressively rather than carefully — which is the correct priority.5 min
Watch for — Week 5
  • One hand correcting its tempo to match the other (the most common polyrhythm error) — the student must hold their own pulse without listening to the other hand; practice with earphones in one ear playing a metronome for one hand only
  • 2:3 learned correctly but 3:4 rushed — 3:4 requires more time to establish in the body; do not move to the piano for 3:4 until the body exercise (walking + clapping) is fluent
  • Polyrhythm feeling mechanical rather than musical — if the student is still counting, the calculation is audible; shift the focus to the musical character of the piece and let the polyrhythm happen as a byproduct of each hand following its own musical line

Structural memorisation — the four memory systems
How professional pianists actually memorise. The four memory systems: muscle, aural, visual, and analytical. Why analytical memory is the only truly reliable one. Memorisation strategy for complex works.
Phase A
The four memory systems — and why most students only use one

Most students memorise through repetition, which builds muscle memory (also called motor memory or kinesthetic memory). Muscle memory is fast to acquire and reliable in ideal conditions — but it is the first to fail under pressure, because anxiety disrupts the motor sequence. A pianist relying solely on muscle memory will often find that a mistake in performance causes a "skip" or a loop — the muscle sequence breaks and cannot recover mid-phrase. The solution is not more repetition but the deliberate development of the other three memory systems: aural (hearing the piece in the inner ear), visual (seeing the score mentally), and analytical (knowing the harmonic and formal structure). A performance backed by all four memory systems is essentially unforgettable under any performance conditions.

Sessions 11–12Building all four memory systems deliberately
  • AdvancedAural memory exercise: student closes eyes and sings (or internally hears) the complete piece — every note, every rhythm, every dynamic — without touching the piano. If any moment is unclear in the inner ear, that moment is not aurally memorised. Mark those moments and return to them. Aural memory is confirmed when the student can "play" the entire piece in their head while walking.10 min
  • AdvancedVisual memory exercise: the student closes their eyes and "reads" the score from memory — can they see the page in their mind's eye? For which sections can they "see" the notes clearly? Many students discover that some pages of a memorised piece are visually clear and others are blank — those blank pages rely entirely on muscle memory and are the performance risk points.8 min
  • TheoryAnalytical memory exercise: without the piano, the student verbally describes the piece: "Section A is in C major, starts on I, the second phrase modulates to G major through V/V (D7), arrives in G at bar 9. Section B is in A minor, starts on vi, uses the harmonic minor (G# throughout), reaches a climax at bar 16 on V7, resolves back to C major at bar 17..." This verbal map is the analytical memory. It is never forgotten because it is knowledge, not pattern.10 min
  • AdvancedThe combined test: student starts from any bar in the piece named by the teacher. They must be able to begin mid-phrase, mid-section, anywhere in the work, without "rewinding" to the beginning. This test is passed only when all four memory systems are active — muscle memory alone cannot navigate to an arbitrary starting point.12 min
Slow practice as memorisationThe 10% speed method
  • AdvancedPlay a fully memorised piece at 10% of performance tempo. Every note must be played with deliberate, conscious decision — not from muscle memory habit. "At this tempo, what finger am I using? What harmony am I on? What is the phrase doing?" This ultra-slow practice simultaneously deepens all four memory systems and identifies any latent uncertainty in note choices or technique.12 min
  • ArtSilent practice (away from the piano): the student "plays" a memorised piece on a flat surface — a table, their lap — with full finger movement while hearing the music internally and tracking the analytical structure mentally. This is not a substitute for piano practice — it is a reinforcement of the aural and analytical memory systems that the piano cannot uniquely provide.8 min
  • EarMemory stress test: teacher introduces a mild distraction (conversation, sudden question) during a performance run-through. Student must continue without stopping. The memory systems that survive distraction are the reliable ones. Note which sections are disrupted — these are the muscle-memory-only passages that need analytical reinforcement.5 min

Teacher's framing for Phase B

Phase B
Why accompaniment sight-reading is a distinct skill

Solo sight-reading is about following the score. Accompaniment sight-reading is about following a person while simultaneously following the score. The accompanist must watch the soloist (for breathing cues, tempo fluctuations, and entrances), listen to the ensemble sound (is the piano too loud? too soft?), maintain their own technical execution, and adjust in real time. These demands require that the piano part be read at a lower level than solo sight-reading — because cognitive capacity is split. A pianist who can read Grade 5 solo music fluently will find Grade 3 accompaniment parts challenging at first, because the mental load of accompanying is far higher than the notation difficulty suggests.

Sessions 21–22Reduction reading — prioritising voices

The sight-reading habit — the most valuable daily practice investment

The three most important chromatic chords in tonal music explained from the ear outward. Each chord's physical production, characteristic resolution, and appearance in standard repertoire.
Phase C
Teacher's framing for Phase C

Phase C is the harmonic theory that explains the 19th century. Most pianists who play Romantic music do not fully understand the harmonic language they are speaking — they follow the notes correctly but cannot explain why a passage creates an overwhelming sense of yearning, or why a sudden chord change feels like the ground shifting. Phase C answers these questions: not as academic theory but as practical musicianship tools that change how the student hears and plays every Romantic and late Classical work in their repertoire. The sequence is always: ear first, theory second, keyboard third, repertoire fourth.

Sessions 29–30Three chromatic chords — ear first
  • EarDiminished seventh (dim7): four notes, symmetrical — each note a minor third apart (e.g., B–D–F–Ab). Play it and sit in the sound. "How many different places does this want to resolve?" Teacher demonstrates four resolutions (each note as a leading tone). Student hears the ambiguity. "This chord is tonally homeless — it belongs everywhere and nowhere. That is why composers use it for maximum dramatic intensity."8 min
  • TheoryDiminished seventh resolution: the most common resolution is as a viio7 (leading tone seventh chord) resolving to I. In C major: B–D–F–Ab resolving to C–E–G (with the Ab as a passing note through G). But the same chord can also resolve as viio7/ii, viio7/iii, viio7/iv etc. — making it the most harmonically flexible chord in tonal music. Find it in three pieces of repertoire and identify which resolution each uses.8 min
  • EarAugmented sixth chord: the Italian sixth (flat-6, 1, raised-4 in relation to the key). In C major: Ab–C–F#. The Ab–F# augmented sixth interval (10 semitones) "wants" to expand outward to an octave G (the dominant). "This chord is the most dramatically tense pre-dominant in tonal music. When you play it, lean into the tension — let the F# pull visibly upward and the Ab pull visibly downward in your performance gesture."8 min
  • TheoryThree augmented sixth types: Italian (3 voices — Ab, C, F#), French (adds D — Ab, C, D, F#), German (adds Eb — Ab, C, Eb, F#). The German augmented sixth sounds identical to a dominant seventh chord in the parallel key (Ab7 in Db major = German Aug6 in C major). This enharmonic equivalence is how composers use the German sixth for dramatic modulation — Schubert does this in almost every major work.8 min
  • EarNeapolitan chord (N or bII): a major chord built on the flattened supertonic (in C major: Db–F–Ab). Its resolution: typically to V (Db–F–Ab → G–B–D), with the Ab descending to G and the F moving to F# (the leading tone). The Neapolitan has a characteristic pathos — a "wrong" major chord in a minor key context. Find it in Beethoven Op. 13 (Pathétique), Chopin Prelude Op. 28 No. 20, Schubert.8 min

These drills at the keyboard make the theory physical and automatic — the student's hands begin to know where the chromatic chords live in any key.12 min

The Neapolitan here is a moment of pathos — give it a breath before the resolution."13 min

Sessions 35–36Two voices — independence and interaction
  • TheoryCounterpoint principle: when two voices move together, three relationships are possible: (1) Parallel motion — both move in the same direction by the same interval (weakest — the voices merge and lose independence); (2) Similar motion — both move in the same direction by different intervals (acceptable but must be used carefully); (3) Contrary motion — the voices move in opposite directions (strongest — the voices are maximally independent and create the richest texture). Bach favours contrary motion constantly: the Two-Part Inventions are studies in how far two voices can be made to sound both independent and inevitable.8 min
  • AdvancedPlaying two voices simultaneously in one hand: some Bach passages require the RH to maintain two independent voices (a melody above and an inner voice below in the same hand). Phase D
The consistency paradox

Every performance of a piece will be different — different room acoustics, different audience energy, different physical state of the performer. Attempting to reproduce an identical performance is both impossible and artistically wrong: a performance that is identical to the previous one is not a performance but a playback. The goal is not consistency of notes but consistency of intention. The harmonic architecture, the phrase shapes, the emotional narrative — these are stable. The specific micro-timing, the exact shade of colour at a given moment — these are alive and respond to the room and the day. A fluent performer at Stage 4 level holds the stable core with confidence and allows the variable surface to be genuinely alive.

Session 56 — The Stage 4 Gateway
Stage 4 Gateway Assessment
Six components. The decisive standard: is the piano technically fluent? Does technique serve music — or does music wait for technique? A student who communicates musical intelligence while performing advanced technical demands has passed Stage 4.
01
Advanced technique — in musical context
Teacher names two technical demands from the gateway repertoire (e.g., "the octave passage in bars 34–38" and "the trill and resolution in bars 56–60"). Student plays each. Pass criterion: the technique is invisible — no tension visible or audible, no change in musical character at the technically demanding moment. The technique is background; the music is foreground.
02
Sight-reading — Grade 7 with musical intelligence
One unseen Grade 7 piece. Advanced scan (90 seconds including harmonic analysis). One continuous play-through. Pass criterion: all five criteria met (pulse, expression, chromatic accuracy, eyes-ahead, harmonic phrasing). The decisive indicator: does the harmonic structure influence the phrasing even on first reading? If yes — the reader is hearing ahead of the notes. That is Grade 7 sight-reading fluency.
03
Harmonic theory — identification and performance connection
Written test: 8 chord identifications in a 16-bar passage (chromatic chords included), one modulation type identified with pivot chord named. Then verbal: "In which bar of your gateway piece 1 does the most harmonically significant chromatic chord appear? What type is it? How did you communicate it in your performance?" Pass criterion: 6/8 correct in the written test; the verbal question answered with both theoretical accuracy and performance specificity.
04
All four memory systems — confirmed under pressure
Teacher names a starting point mid-phrase in each gateway piece. Student begins immediately (no rewind). During the playing: teacher asks one harmonic question ("what key are we in now?") — student answers while continuing. After: student sings the opening theme of each piece from memory. Pass criterion: immediate start from any named point; harmonic question answered correctly without stopping; opening theme accurately recalled. All four memory systems operational simultaneously.
05
Programme performance — fluency demonstrated
Both pieces in programme order. Before beginning: student states one sentence of interpretive intention for each piece. Full programme with inter-piece pause. No stopping. Teacher evaluates: (a) Is technical demand invisible — is the octave passage, the trill, the fast passage, the polyrhythm all produced without audible or visible technical effort? (b) Is the harmonic language communicating — are chromatic chords given the harmonic lean, modulations performed as narrative events? (c) Does the voice-leading sing — in contrapuntal passages, are the individual voices independently shaped? The gateway question: is the piano fluent? Does technique serve music? Pass criterion: at least three distinct harmonic events communicated with specific physical gestures, and at least one technical demand that the teacher cannot locate in the performance — because it has disappeared into the music.
06
Musical maturity — the Stage 5 readiness question
After the programme: teacher asks: "What do you want to say next? What music do you want to play that you cannot yet play?" Student answers. The answer reveals whether they are ready for Stage 5 — Depth, Repertoire and Stylistic Identity. A student who has a specific musical desire that Stage 4 cannot yet fulfil is ready for Stage 5. A student who has no musical desire beyond the present repertoire is not yet ready — not for lack of technique, but for lack of artistic hunger. That hunger is the prerequisite for Stage 5.

After the gateway — what Stage 5 brings

Stage 5 is Depth: Repertoire and Stylistic Identity. The student who passes Stage 4 has acquired technical fluency, harmonic intelligence, contrapuntal awareness, and advanced sight-reading. Stage 5 asks what to do with all of it. The work of Stage 5 is not about adding new skills — it is about developing a musical identity. Which repertoire speaks to this student? Which composers does their technique and temperament serve best? What is their musical voice? Stage 5 introduces improvisation, stylistic specialisation across all four major Western periods, and the first steps toward teaching — because the student who can explain the music to someone else understands it at a level no amount of practice alone can produce. The hunger expressed in the Stage 4 gateway question is the compass for all of Stage 5.
